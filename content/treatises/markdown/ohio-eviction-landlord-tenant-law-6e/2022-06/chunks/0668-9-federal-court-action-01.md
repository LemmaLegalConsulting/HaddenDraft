# 9. Federal Court Action

## Source text

A HCVP participant may be entitled to contest the PHA termination decision by filing an action in federal court, under 42 U.S.C § 1983, alleging a violation of applicable federal statutes and regulations (and, if applicable, due process).

The participant may elect to file the federal court action instead of, or in addition to, the R.C. Chapter 2506 appeal.

However, if the participant files an R.C. Chapter 2506 appeal in state court and a § 1983 action in federal court, under the doctrine of collateral estoppel, the state court may decide the federal court’s liability issue. If entered first, the state court’s R.C.

Chapter 2506 decision, relative to the PHA’s alleged noncompliance with federal law, may be binding on the fed eral court action. See Osborn v. Ashland County Bd. of Alcohol, Etc., 979 F.2d 1131 (6th Cir. 1992) (state court ruling on due process claims in R.C. Chapter 2506 appeal precluded federal court action on those claims). See also Copeland v. Hous. Auth. of Hollywood, 300 Fed. App’x 843, 2008 WL 4997184 (11th
Cir. 2008) (res judicata effect of state court judgment).

If the participant failed to perfect timely (i.e., within the 30-day period)410 an R.C.

Chapter 2506 appeal, the federal court action may be the participant’s only avenue for
appeal. In Ohio, the statute of limitations for the § 1983 action is two years.Browning
v. Pendleton, 869 F.2d 989, 992 (6th Cir. 1989) (en banc).

In the federal court action, “[t]he level of deference shown to the factfindings of a hearing officer is not absolute but it is significant.” Clark v. Alexander, 85 F.3d 146, 151 (4th Cir. 1996). Accord Gammons v. Mass. Dep’t of Hous. & Com. Dev., 502 F.

Supp.2d 161 (D. Mass. 2007); Litsey v. Hous. Auth. of Bardstown , No. 3:99CV-114- H, 1999 WL 33604017 (W.D. Ky. Apr. 1, 1999). “[T]he reviewing court must be satisfied that the hearing officer’s conclusions are supported by substantial evidence.” Clark, 85 F.3d at 152. See also Ellis v. Ritchie , 803 F. Supp. 1097, 1101 (E.D. Va.
1992) (court determines whether “a rational basis exists for the agency’s decision”).
