# b. Picklo Rule

## Source text

The Ohio Supreme Court has determined thatR.C. 4705.01 applies to persons who meet the R.C. 1923.01(C)(2) definition of landlord bu t are not the owner of the
premises. Cleveland Bar Ass’n v. Picklo, 96 Ohio St.3d 195, 772 N.E.2d 1187 (2002).

It held that, when such persons– landlords who are agents of the owner – are plaintiffs in an eviction complaint, if they proceed pro se, they are engaging in the unauthorized practice of law. Id.

The First Appellate District has narrowed (or more clearly defined) the scope of the Picklo rule. It held tha t the Picklo prohibition does not apply to non -owner plaintiffs who are a party to the lease as the landlord. Ebbing v. Bobbitt, No. CA2012- 03-061, 2012 WL 4862548 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Oct. 15, 2012).

A violation of the Picklo rule is a defense to the eviction action. Ebbing v.

Lawhorn, No. CA2011 -07-125, 2012 WL 2877434 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., July 16, 2012); Batt v. Nairebout, No. L-03-1001, 2003 WL 21489414 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., June 30, 2003); Begue v. Calloway, No. 03-CVG-00286 (Ohio Mun., Akron, Feb. 4, 2003).

A violation of the Picklo rule is also grounds to vacate an FED judgment and to dismiss the case under the court’s inherent authority to vacate void judgments.

Patterson v. Tegge, No. 2019-CVG-025075 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., Mar. 3, 2020).

In such cases, the complaint is null and void ab initio. Id.
