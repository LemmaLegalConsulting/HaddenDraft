## Source text

The term “innocent tenant” is used in cases in which the termination of an assisted housing tenancy is based on the criminal activity (usually, drug -related criminal
activity) of a household member or a guest. In this context, innocent tenant me ans a tenant who did not participate in, and neither knew nor reasonably should have known of, the criminal activity.

The U.S. Supreme Court has ruled that 42 U.S.C. §1437d(l)(6) does not bar a housing authority from terminating the Public Housing tenancy of an innocent tenant, even when the criminal activity occurred outside the rental unit. Dep’t of Hous. & Urban Dev. v. Rucker , 535 U.S. 125 (2002). However, it also held that a housing authority has discretionary authority not to terminate the tenancy of an innocent tenant.213 Id.

HUD provided housing authorities with two guidance letters relative to theRucker
decision. Letter from Mel Martinez, Secretary, U.S. Dep’t of Hous. & Urban Dev., to Public Housing Directors (Apr. 16, 2002); Letter from Mich ael M. Liu, Ass’t Secretary, U.S. Dep’t of Hous. & Urban Dev., to Public Housing Directors (June 6, 2002).

In his April 16 letter, Secretary Martinez urged housing authorities “to be guided by compassion and common sense in responding to cases involving illegal use of drugs.” He stated that “[e]viction should be the last option explored, after all others have been exhausted.” In his June 6 letter, Assistant Secretary Liu noted that “theRucker decision upholds HUD regulations that, since 1999, have made it clear that ...
