If a trial court fails to consider a tenant’s equity defense, when entering a judgment for the landlord, the appellate court, as a matter of law, should reverse and remand the trial court decision. E.g., Gorsuch Homes, Inc. v. Wooten, 73 Ohio App.3d 426, 435- 36, 597 N.E.2d 554 (2nd Dist. 1992).

“Once the trial court has weighed the equitable considerations and reached a decision on whether to decla re a forfeiture, appellate review is limited to a determination of whether the lower court abused its discretion in its weighing of the equities.” BWS Props. v. LaSalle, Inc., No. 51119, 1986 WL 9110, at *2 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Aug. 21, 1986). Accord Showe Mgmt. Corp. v. Notwithstanding this standard of review, appellate courts have reversed trial court decisions on a tenant’s equity defense.

They have reversed trial court decisions denying the equity defense. E.g., Greene
Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Manning, No. 98-CA-55, 1999 WL 76456 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist.,
Feb. 19, 1999) (blind tenant’s failure to recertify timely due to housing authority’s failure to provide notice in an accessible format and its refusal to provide tenant with any auxiliary aid); Ward v. Washington Distribs., Inc., 67 Ohio App.2d 49, 425N.E.2d 420 (6th Dist. 1980) (tenant’s failure to exercise timely an option to renew the lease, as a result of an honest, good faith mistake). See also Urban Hollow Apts. v. Johnson,
No. 93 -APG10-1495, 1994 WL 232274 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., May 26, 1994) (remand to reconsider equity defense, relative to nonpayment of $239.66 repair charge, where trial court “made a conclusory assertion that defendant had not established grounds for equitable relief, [but] the findings of fact do not support that conclusion”).
