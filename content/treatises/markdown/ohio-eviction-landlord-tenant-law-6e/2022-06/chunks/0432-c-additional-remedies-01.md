# C. Additional Remedies

## Source text

When a landlord violates R.C. 5321.15(B), the tenant may assert common law claims against the landlord, in addition to the R.C. 5321.15(C) claim. See Section V.C,
above. For example, the tenant may have a claim for:
(1) bailment, Edwards v. C.N. Inv. Co., 27 Ohio Misc. 57, 272 N.E.2d 652 (Ohio Mun., Shaker Heights, 1971); cf. Kayanda v. Kamenir, 16 Ohio Misc.2d 1, 475 N.E.2d 519 (Ohio Mun., Akron, 1984); and/or
(2) conversion, Fisher v. Barker , 159 Ohio App.3d 745, 825 N.E.2d 244 (2nd Dist. 2005); Gordon v. Morris, No. 2000-CA-69, 2001 WL 85797 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 2, 2001);Milburn v. Henness, No. 03-CVH- 208 (Ohio Mun., Chillicothe, Jan. 29, 2004).

The tenant also may have an R.C. 5321.04(B) claim (and related common law
claims) against the landlord if the landlord entered the premises in violation of R.C.

5321.04(A)(8) or 5321.04(B), whenthe landlord seized the tenant’s personal property.

For a discussion on the R.C. 5321.04(B) claim (and the related common law claims), see Chapter X, above.

Also, instead of requesting injunctive relief in an R.C. 5321.15(C) action, the tenant may file a replevin action for the return of the property.

Two court decisions address tenant conversion claims against landlords arising from the execution of a writ of restitution in an eviction action: Castle Apts., Inc. v.

Allgood, 43 Ohio App.3d 163, 539 N.E.2d 1154 (9th Dist. 1988), and Keesey v.

Superior Mobile Homes, Inc. , No. 2000 -AP-080057, 2001 WL 293253 (Ohio App., 477 5th Dist., Mar. 20, 2001).

In Allgood, the court held the landlord liable. The tenant’s personal property had been seized pursuant to the writ of restitution and, thereafter, the return of the regular mail service disclosed that the eviction complaint had not been served in accordance with the applicable law.

In Keesey, the court held the landlord not liable. The tenant’s mobile home had been removed from the mobile home park pursuant to the writ of restitution and, thereafter, the court granted the tenant’s motion to vacate the eviction judgment based upon the landlord’s acceptance of certain rent. “Because appellee [landlord] proceeded under a lawfully issued writ of restitution, the municipal court’s subsequent decision to vacate the judgment does not operate to retroactively transform a lawful act into a unlawful one.” 2001 WL 293253, at *2. Cf. Sacksteder v. Gisslen, 197 Ohio App.3d 484, 968 N.E.2d 11 (2nd Dist. 2011) (denial of R.C. 5321.15(B) claim arising from executed writ of restitution followed by sustained objection to eviction judgment);

Jansen v. Barclay Square , 34 Ohio Misc. 14, 295 N.E.2d 443 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., 1973) (denial of tenant’s trespass claim arising from properly executed writ of restitution).
