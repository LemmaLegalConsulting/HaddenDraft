In the project -based subsidized housing program, the regulations require “a meeting between landlord and tenant in which both parties attempt to resolve whatever problems gave rise to the ten-day notice [of termination].” Crossroads Somerset Ltd.
v. Newland , 40 Ohio App.3d 20, 24, 531 N.E.2d 327 (10th Dist. 1987). Accord Independent Mgmt. Servs. of Ohio, Inc. v. Davis , No. 2014-CVG-01244, slip op. at 2 (Ohio Mun., Elyria, July 18, 2014) (held the notice of termination invalid because it “suggests that the decision to terminate Defendant’s tenancy already occurred” and thus “[i]t is not clear what benefit could be derived from the tenant’s ‘discussion’ of a matter already decided”).

The purpose of the meeting “is to attempt to resolve the controversy in a mutually satisfactory manner, that will, if possible, avoid the tenant’s loss of subsidized housing while protecting the rights of the landlord.” Gorsuch Homes, Inc. v. Wooten, 73 Ohio App.3d 426, 434, 597 N.E.2d 554 (2nd Dist. 1992). Accord Independent Mgmt. Servs.

of Ohio, Inc. v. Davis, No. 2014-CVG-01244, slip op. at 2 (Ohio Mun., Elyria, July 18,
2014) (as to the meeting, “this is not a mere formality, but confers on Defendant, beneficiary of the federal subsidy, a first chance at procedural due process to meet with Plaintiff to try to avoid the potential termination”).

If a tenant cures the breach at or before the meeting with the landlord, generally the cure of the breach should resolve the controversy. In such circumstances, if the landlord still seeks to terminate the tenancy, the landlord arguably violates the landlord’s obligation to attempt in good faith to resolve the controversy at the meeting.

However, if a tenant repeatedly has breached a lease obligation, the tenant might not be entitled to another opportunity to cure the breach. See, e.g., Vill. S. Apts. v.

Jensen, No. CA96-10-018, 1997 WL 148067, at *2 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Mar. 31, 1997); Vistula Mgmt. Co. v. Armstrong, No. L-93-168, 1994 WL 262571, at *5 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., June 10, 1994). Cf. Fed. Prop. Mgmt. v. Brown, No. 17424, 1999 WL 961275, at *5-6 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., June 25, 1999) (applying R.C. 5321.11 right to cure).

A landlord may include language in a federal notice of termination that creates an explicit right (in addition to the implicit right) to cure. When the notice informs the tenant to take or cease certain action or the lease will be terminated, and t he tenant timely complies with the directive, the landlord may not terminate the tenancy for the underlying issue. See Rockville Ctr. Hous. Auth. v. Boggen, 20 Misc.3d 1126(A), 2008 WL 2927834 (N.Y. Dist. Ct. July 31, 2008) (tenant complied with directive : “Please have your guest vacate your apartment immediately or your lease will be terminated and you will be evicted”).

Also, if the reason for the proposed termination of tenancy is tenant conduct that breaches an R.C. 5321.05 obligation that materiallyaffects health and safety, the tenant has a right to cure the breach and thereby avoid a termination of the tenancy under R.C.

5321.11. See Sections I.D.2.d and I.D.2.e, above.

In the Public Housing program, a tenant may not have a right to cure the breac h during the pendency of a grievance procedure hearing, which generally is held after the expiration of the time period of the federal notice of termination. See, e .g., Trumbull Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Reid , No. 3673, 1987 WL 18019 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., S ept. 30, 1987) (reason for termination was tenant’s refusal to sign required releases; tenant contested the termination in the grievance procedure, and sought to cure the breach after the parties had submitted their evidence to the hearing panel but before the panel had rendered its decision; court held that the tenant did not have a right to cure the breach at that time).
