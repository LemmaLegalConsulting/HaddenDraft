# 1. County Court

## Source text

County courts have limited subject -matter jurisdiction in civil actions. See Gallagher v. Billmaier, 79 Ohio Law Abs. 417, 154 N.E.2d 472(Ohio App., 6th Dist., 1958). Its scope is defined principally by R.C. Chapter 1907.

Relevant to the landlord-tenant claims that are discussed in Chapters I-XIX, above, the subject-matter jurisdiction of county courts includes:
(1) civil actions for the recov ery of sums not exceeding fifteen thousand dollars, R.C. 1907.03, 1907.031;
(2) actions for trespass on real estate in which the damages demanded do not exceed fifteen thousand dollars, R.C. 1907.05(A);
(3) replevin actions in which the value of the personal prop erty that is sought to be recovered does not exceed fifteen thousand dollars, R.C.

1907.06; and 363 A tenant may avoid this jurisdictional issue by filing the R.C. 5321.15 action in the court of common pleas. See Section XX.C.3., below.
(4) forcible entry and detainer actions, R.C. 1923.01(A), 1907.031(A)(6).

A county court has subject -matter jurisdiction in an eviction action that concerns a land installment contract. Shelton v. Smith, No. CA-323, 1984 WL 7469 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Apr. 30, 1984).

A county court has subject-matter jurisdiction to enter a judgment that declares a cancellation and forfeiture of a land installment contract. Williams v. Bjork, 112 Ohio Misc.2d 57, 753 N.E.2d 995 (Ohio County Ct., Ashtabula Cty., 2001).

A county court has subject -matter jurisdiction in an eviction action that concerns whether the occupant exercised an option to purchase and thereby has an equitable interest in the title to the property. Sears v. Fechuck, No. 1999AP-080048, 2000 WL 874727 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., June 6, 2000).

A county court does not have subject-matter jurisdiction in an eviction action if a dispute exists as to which party holds present record title. See Section I.L.3, above.
