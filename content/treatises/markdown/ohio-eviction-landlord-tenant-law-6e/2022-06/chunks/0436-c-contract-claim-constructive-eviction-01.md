# C. Contract Claim, Constructive Eviction

## Source text

Under common law, constructive eviction is “surrender of possession [of the premises] by the tenant on justifiable grounds.” Liberal Sav. & Loan Co. v. Frankel Realty Co., 137 Ohio St. 489, 498, 30 N.E.2d 1012 (1940). Justifiable grounds exist when there “is such a failure or interference on the part of the landlord with the intended enjoyment of the leased premises as to be of a substantial nature and so injurious to the tenant as to deprive him of the beneficial enjoyment of the leased 321 The Roberts decision seems to establish a fourth element for this claim, which does not exist in any of the other court decisions. It seems to require the tenant, before leaving the premises, to register a protest with landlord against the notice to leave the premises.

premises.” Nye v. Schuler, 110 Ohio App. 443, 445-46, 165 N.E.2d 16 (4th Dist. 1959).

There cannot be a constructive eviction if the tenant has not relinquished possession of the premises. Lexford Props., Inc. v. Schiltz, No. 2003-CA-00025, 2003 WL 22128788 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Sept. 8, 2003); Burnside v. Ickes, No. 02-COA- 015, 2003 WL 42455 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Jan. 3, 2003); Manifold v. Schuster , 67 Ohio App.3d 251, 586 N.E.2d 1142 (4th Dist. 1990).

There also cannot be a constructive eviction before the tenant’s lease term and occupancy begins. Harpel v. Pierce, Nos. 17163,17168, 1999 WL 55700 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 5, 1999).

A constructive eviction may arise from a lan dlord’s failure to remedy a defective condition at the premises. E.g., Stiffler v. Canterbury Run n Apts., No. 19308, 2002 WL 31243541, at *3 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Oct. 4, 2002) (“apartment was infiltrated with leaking water, sewage, growing mold, soaked carpets, and a sagging ceiling”);

Harpel v. Pierce, Nos. 17163,17168, 1999 WL 55700 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 5,
1999) (severe infestation of roaches, leaking pipes, exposed electrical wiring, and gas leaks in stove and furnace); Schutte v. Nicholson , N o. C-910261, 1992 WL 19396 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Feb. 5, 1992) (lack of heat for five days); Katzin v. Murad, No.

46553, 1984 WL 7150 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Jan. 5, 1984) (broken locks, broken glass in windows, sink pulling out from the walls, failure to c lean up garbage, and inoperative refrigerator); Keener v. Ewert, 67 Ohio App.2d 17, 425 N.E.2d 914 (6th
Dist. 1979) (lack of hot water, failure of bathtub to drain, basement flooded with water and debris, and a lockout); Katz v. Comisar , 28 Ohio N.P. (n.s. ) 10 (Ohio C.P., Hamilton Cty., 1930) (infestation of vermin).

A constructive eviction also may arise from a landlord’s refusal to provide the tenant with a copy of the lease, if it prevents the tenant from securing utility services for the premises. E.g., Proctor v. Frame, 90 Ohio Misc.2d 11, 695 N.E.2d 357 (Ohio Mun., Akron, 1998) (deprived of electrical service and, consequently, heat).

A constructive eviction cannot arise from the criminal activity of a third party, if the landlord provides reasonable security measures in the common areas (see Section VII.C.8, above) and the lease does not require the landlord to provide any additional security measures. Hawk v. Fairmeadows Apts., No. CA86-12-180, 1987 WL 13707 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., June 29, 1987).

A tenant’s constructive eviction claim against a landlord is a contract claim. Ma Chere Hair Style Acad. v. Rolo Co., No. L-85-287, 1986 WL 7111, at *3 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., June 20, 1986). “A constructive eviction is a breach of the landlord’s duty 481 imposed by the covenants of possession and quiet enjoyment.” Id.

Therefore, in a constructive eviction claim, a tenant may recover compensatory damages but not punitive damages. Id., 1986 WL 7111, at *3,7.
