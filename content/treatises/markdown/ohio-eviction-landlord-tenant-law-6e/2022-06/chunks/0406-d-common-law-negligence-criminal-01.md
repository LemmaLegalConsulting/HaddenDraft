# D. Common Law Negligence, Criminal

## Source text

Activity of Third Party A third party’s intentional criminal acts at the premises may give rise to a negligence claim by the tenant against the landlord. The elements of this claim are:
(1) the landlord’s violation of an obligation to maintain the premises or to provide reasonable security ( e.g., statutory or code obligation, implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, express lease provision), (2) a criminal intrusion, a proximate c ause of which is the violation, (3) an injury to the tenant’s person or property as a result of the criminal intrusion, and (4) the reasonable foreseeability of the criminal intrusion and the criminal act directed at the tenant (i.e., the tenant’s injury),which may be based in part on the prior criminal activity in the neighborhood or building.

Under Ohio case law, there are at least three circumstances where a landlord’s violation of an obligation and a resulting criminal intrusion may give rise to a negligence claim by the tenant against the landlord.

The first is where the landlord violates the landlord’s obligation to provide in the common area reasonable security measures against criminal activity. See Section VII.C.8, above.

The second is where the l andlord violates an express provision of the rental agreement that requires the landlord to provide a specified security measure, such as a window lock. Blair v. Prop. Mgmt. Consultants, 40 Ohio App.3d 103, 531 N.E.2d 752 (1st Dist. 1987);Buncy v. Daniels, No. CA-6059, 1983 WL 6486 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., June 13, 1983). See also Cipollene v. Hoffmeier, No. C-060482, 2007 WL 2141578, ¶ 20 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., July 27, 2007); Brown v. Campbell , Nos. 85698,85702, 2005 WL 1793519, ¶ 23 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., July 28, 2005).

The third is where the landlord violates the landlord’s obligation to exercise 449 reasonable care in providing the security services that the landlord does provide.Pamer
v. Pritchard Bros., 61 Ohio Misc.2d 150, 575 N.E.2d 900 (Ohio C.P., C lermont Cty., 1990).

Ohio courts generally have held that, except for the foregoing obligations, the common law does not impose an obligation on landlords to protect tenants against the criminal activity of a third party. Thomas v. Hart Realty, Inc., 17 Ohio App.3d 83, 477 N.E.2d 668 (1st Dist. 1984). See also Cowan v. Hidden Lake , No. 17053, 1995 WL 523349 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Sept. 6, 1995); Johnson v. Monroe Realty Co. , No.

67964, 1995 WL 322278 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., May 25, 1995); Meier v. Vistula Heritage Vill., 62 Ohio Misc.2d 632, 609 N.E.2d 1360 (Ohio C.P., Lucas Cty., 1992).

Likewise, Ohio courts generally have held that R.C. 5321.04(A) does not, per se, impose an obligation on landlords to protect tenants against the criminal activity of a third party. See Section VII.C.8, above.

Even when a landlord’s R.C. 5321.04(A) violation facilitates the criminal intrusion, courts seem reluctant to find that the R.C. 5321.04(A) violation is a proximate cause of the criminal act directed at the tenant. E.g., Runge v. Rosewater,
Nos. 47650,47651, 1984 WL 5072 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., June 14, 1984) (insufficient evidence on whether entry occurred through door with defective lock); Cherkiss v.

Thomas, No. L -83-416, 1984 WL 7829 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Apr. 13, 1984) (entry through window with defective latch); Emm v. Sheskey , No. 79AP -632, 1980 WL 353284 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Feb. 14, 1980) (entry through back door with defective lock). See also Brown v. Campbell, Nos. 85698,85702, 2005 WL 1793519 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., July 28, 2005) (entry through side door, which landlord promised to replace with more secure door).

“The foreseeability of criminal acts, that is, the defendant’s knowledge, is determined from the totality of the circumstances.” Brown v. Campbell, Nos. 85698, 85702, 2005 WL 1793519, ¶ 16 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., July 28, 2005). However, the courts are mindful that “criminal behavior of third persons is not predictable to any degree of certainty.” Johnson v. Spectrum of Supportive Servs., No. 82267, 2003 WL 21982915, ¶ 20 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Aug. 21, 2003). As a result, they require “(1) specific knowledge of a potential future problem based on past occurrences along with
(2) a substantial likelihood that such an incident would occur. ” Id., 2003 WL 21982915, ¶ 21. “[E]vidence that the [housing] complex is in a particularly crime ridden location” may be a relevant factor. Pieper v. Williams, No. L-05-1065, 2006 WL 964725, ¶ 55 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Apr. 14, 2006).

Courts seem relu ctant to find this requisite foreseeability. E.g., Brown v.

Campbell, Nos. 85698,85702, 2005 WL 1793519 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., July 28, 2005) (murder by non -tenant husband, who was subject to anti -stalking order, not foreseeable); Doe v. Beach House Dev. Co., 136 Ohio App.3d 573, 737 N.E.2d 141 (8th Dist. 2000) (notwithstanding a series of prior violent and aggressive acts of a third party (another tenant’s child) toward plaintiff and other residents of the complex, which included holding a knife on two young girls, the third party’s first degree felony sexual assault on plaintiff was not foreseeable); Sayles v. SB-92 Ltd. P’ship, 138 Ohio App.3d 476, 741 N.E.2d 613 (8th Dist. 2000) (it was not foreseeable that the tenant’s stalker would enter the building as a result of the exterior door’s defective lock and then start a fire at the tenant’s dwelling unit door).
