When a tenant’s race, color, religion, sex, handicap, familial status, or national origin is a significant factor in the landlord’s decision to terminate a tenancy, generally it is a defense to the eviction action under one (or more) of the following five statutes.
(1) The federal Fair Housing Act. 42 U.S.C. § 3604.
(2) Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (“Section 504”), if disability discrimination is alleged and the landlord receives federal financial assistance.142 29 U.S.C. § 794.
(3) Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act (“ADA”), if disability discrimination is alleged and the landlord receives state or local government financial assistance. 42 U.S.C. §§ 12131, 12132.
(4) The Civil Rights Act of 1866, if race discrimination is alleged. 42 141 The U.S. Supreme Court is similarly split on the meaning of the term “because of” in the context of both the ADEA ( see Gross v. (5) The Ohio Civil Rights Act. R.C. 4112.02(H).

The federal Fair Housing Act does not apply to any tenancy in a building that contains four or fewer dwelling units, if the landlord resides in one of the units. 42 U.S.C. § 3603(b)(2). The other statutes do not contain this exception.
