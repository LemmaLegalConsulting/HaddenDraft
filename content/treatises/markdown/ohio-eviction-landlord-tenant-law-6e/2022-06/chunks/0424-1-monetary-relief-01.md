When a landlord violates R.C. 5321.15(A), under R.C. 5321.15(C), the tenant may bring an action against the landlord for “all damages.” All damages under R.C. 5321.15(C) include general damages, which are the tenant’s damages that necessarily result fromthe R.C. 5321.15(A) violation. Gordon 467
v. Morris, No. 2000-CA-69, 2001 WL 85797, at *4 -5 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 2, 2001). In the tenant’s pleading, the tenant is not required to allege specifically the general damages. Id.

All damages under R.C. 53 21.15(C) also include special damages, which are the tenant’s damages that result from the R.C. 5321.15(A) violation but were not a necessary consequence of it. Id. In the tenant’s pleading, the tenant is required to allege specifically any special damages. Id. See also Ohio R. Civ. P. 9(G).

317 A comparison between R.C. 5321.15(C) and R.C. 5 321.02(B) provides a reasonable basis for the conclusion that R.C. 5321.15 authorizes an award of punitive damages even without a finding of malice. R.C. 5321.02(B) provides that, when a landlord violates R.C. 5321.02(A), the tenant may recover “actual da mages.” R.C. 5321.15(C) provides that, when a landlord violates R.C. 5321.15(A), the tenant may recover “all damages.” By using the term “all damages” instead of “actual damages” in R.C. 5321.15(C), the legislature seems to authorize an award of actual damages and all other types of damages, which includes punitive damages, when a landlord violates R.C. 5321.15(A).

469 If the requisite malice exists, the tenant is entitled to punitive damages even if the actual damage award is nominal damages. Gordon v. Morris, No. 2000-CA-69, 2001 WL 85797, at *6 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 2, 2001). E.g., Blausey v. Frase, No. 06- CVG-00748 (Ohio Mun., Licking Cty., Feb. 28 , 2007) ($25 nominal damages and $2,500 punitive damages); Milburn v. Henness , No. 03 -CVH-208 (Ohio Mun., Chillicothe, Jan. 29, 2004) ($100 nominal damages and $750 punitive damages).

“The determination of the amount of punitive damages is within the discretion of the trier of fact as long as the award is not based upon passion and prejudice.” Meacham v. Miller, 79 Ohio App.3d 35, 40, 606 N.E.2d 996 (4th Dist. 1992).

Factors that courts consider in determining the amount of the punitive damage award includ e: “the relationship between the parties, the probability of recurrence unless the conduct is deterred, the harm that is likely to occur from similar conduct, the harm that actually occurred, the reprehensibility of the conduct, the nature of the wrong, the removal of any financial profit so that future conduct results in a loss, the financial status of the parties, the deterrence value, a reasonable relationship between compensatory damages and punitive damages, and whether the wrong is a single occurrence or a pattern of wrong conduct.” Brown v. Ray, No. 08-CVG-25314, ¶ 81 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Feb. 18, 2009). Accord Smith v. Akarui Enters., LLC, No. 2010-CVH-18860 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., June 30, 2011).

The amount of the punitive damage award “‘must be an amount that fairly punishes the [landlord] for his malicious or malevolent acts and that will deter others from similar conduct.’” Babcock v. Milligan , No. 01 -CA-013, 2002 WL 924297, at *1 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., May 3, 2002). When a landlord, acting with malice, executes a “self-help eviction” of the tenant in violation in R.C. 5321.15(A), a punitive damage award of $50 is inadequate. Id.
