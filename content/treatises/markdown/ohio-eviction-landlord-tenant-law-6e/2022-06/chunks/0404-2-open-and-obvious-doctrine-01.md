# 2. Open and Obvious Doctrine

## Table-of-contents context

- IX. Tenant Claims for Defective
  - C. Common Law Negligence, Premises

## Source text

The open and obvious doctrine states that, “where a danger is open and obvious, a landowner owes no duty of care to individuals lawfully on the premises .” Lang v.

Holly Hill Motel, Inc. , 122 Ohio St.3d 120, 909 N.E.2d 120, ¶ 11 (2009). Accord Robinson v. Bates, 112 Ohio St.3d 17, 857 N.E.2d 1195, ¶ 21 (2006). This means “the owner has no duty to protect against dangers ‘which are known to the invitee or are so obvious and apparent to such invitee that he may reasonably be expected to discover them and protect himself against them.’”Howson v. Amorose, No. 00AP-8, 2000 WL 447 1753112, at *3 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Nov. 30, 2000) (quotingSidle v. Humphrey, 13 Ohio St.2d 45, 233 N.E.2d 589, syllabus no. 1 (1968)).

“The question of whether a danger is open and obvious is an objective one.” Canidate v. Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth., No. 101753, 2015 WL 1121444, ¶ 15 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Mar. 12, 2015). “When reasonable minds could differ with respect to whether a danger is open and obvious, the obviousness of the risk is an issue for the jury to determine.” Id., ¶ 16.

“‘[T]he open and obvious doctrine remains viable in Ohio.’” Lang v. Holly Hill Motel, Inc., 122 Ohio St.3d 120, 909 N.E.2d 120, ¶ 11 (2009) (quoting Armstrong v.

Best Buy Co., 99 Ohio St.3d 79, 788 N.E.2d 1088, syllabus (2003)). Accord Robinson
v. Bates, 112 Ohio St.3d 17, 857 N.E.2d 1195, ¶ 21 (2006).

“[T]he open-and-obvious doctrine can excuse a defendant’s breach of a common law duty of care.”Lang v. Holly Hill Motel, Inc., 122 Ohio St.3d 120, 909 N.E.2d 120, ¶ 14 (2009).

As a result, “[t]he open and obvious doctrine ... applies to common law premises liability even when it involves claims against a landlord.” Packman v. Barton , No.

CA2009-03-009, 2009 WL 3165605, ¶ 33 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Oct. 5, 2009).

Accord Hatto v. McLaughlin, No. 109307, 2020 WL 3283624, ¶ 24 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., June 18, 2020); Hanson v. Knoppe, No. 2019CAE-08-0046, 2019 WL 7290524, ¶ 24 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Dec. 26, 2019); Rawlings v. Springwoods Apts. of Columbus, Ltd., 125 N.E.3d 312, 2018-Ohio-4845, ¶ 24 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., 2018);

Levy v. Huener, No. L-17-1081, 2018 WL 388406, ¶ 17 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Jan. 12, 2018); Callentine v. Mill Invs., LLC, No. 2017-AP-06, 2017 WL 5565502, ¶ 39 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Nov. 17, 2017); Mayhew v. Massey, 86 N.E.3d 758, 2017-Ohio-1016, ¶ 15 (Ohio App., 7th D ist., 2017); Base-Smith v. Lautrec, Ltd., No. CA2013-07-115, 2014 WL 408041, ¶ 18 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Feb. 3, 2014); Bierl v. BGZ Assocs. II, LLC, No. 9-12-42, 2013 WL 684707, ¶ 18 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., Feb. 25, 2013).

For a discussion on the inappl icability of the open and obvious doctrine to R.C.

5321.04 negligence per se claims, see Section IX.B.7.a, above.
