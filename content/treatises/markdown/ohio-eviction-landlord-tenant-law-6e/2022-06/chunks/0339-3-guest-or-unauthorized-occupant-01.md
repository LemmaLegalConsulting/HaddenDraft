# 3. Guest or Unauthorized Occupant

## Source text

A lease may identify the authorized occupants under the tenancy and prohibit any other persons from occupying the premises, absent prior landlord approval. For example, see Section III.G.5, above, for a discussion on the authorized occupant rules in assisted housing leases.288 As a result, an issue often arises as to when a guest becomes an unauthorized
occupant. The case law provides some guidance.

An occupancy does not exist without overnight stays. See Lucas Metro. Hous.
Auth. v. Wood , No. CVG -0900329 (Ohio Mun., Sylvania, June 23, 2009) (the guest was often at the rental unit visiting with his children, but he stayed overnight only one time in six years).

Generally, a person who stays overnight up to two (and sometimes three) times a week is not an occupant of the premises. E.g., Sumac Real Estate Holding, LLC v.

Mitchell, No. 2014-CVG-00670 (Ohio Mun., Euclid, Apr. 18, 2014) (two/three nights per week); Countrybrook Apts. v. Forsythe , No. M86 -CVG-20896 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., Aug. 28, 1986) (weekends);Andrews Place Assoc. v. Loebsack, No. 84- CVG-1222 (Ohio Mun., Mentor, Jan. 3, 1985) (three nights per week). See als o 288 For the definition of “assisted housing,” see note 163, above.

365 Gallatin Hous. Auth. v. Gifford, No. 89-25-II, 1989 WL 100268 (Tenn. App. Aug. 30,
1989) (two/three nights per week);Messiah Baptist Hous. Dev. Fund Co. v. Rosser, 92 Misc.2d 383, 400 N.Y.S.2d 306 (City Ct. 1977) (two/three nights per week). Cf.

Vistula Mgmt. Co. v. Scott , No. 92 -CVG-76 (Ohio Mun., Fremont, May 6, 1992) (babysitting 5:30 A.M. to 7:00 or 8:00 P.M. daily, plus overnight irregularly);

Generally, a person who stays up to two consecutive weeks is not an occupant of the premises. Wilmington Green Apts. v. Estep , No. 88 -CVG-64 (Ohio Mun., Wilmington, Apr. 7, 1988) (two consecutive weeks). See also Mihil Co. v. Paradiso, 107 Misc.2d 867, 436 N.Y.S.2d 115 (Civ. Ct. 1980) (seven consecutive weeks);Ritter
v. Cecil County Office of Hous. & Cmty. Dev., 33 F.3d 323 (4th Cir. 1994) (upholding public housing agency’s two-week rule in the Section 8 Certificate program).

Factors to consider in distinguishing between an occupant and a guest include: (1) the number of days per week or length of stay, (2) any use of another r esidence, (3) where clothes and other personal belongings are maintained, (4) where meals are eaten,
(5) where mail is received, (6) address used for driver’s license and other documents, and (7) other indicia of residency. Juarovisech v. Portage Metro. H ous. Auth., No.

C83-4961 (N.D. Ohio Jan. 12, 1984). See Urban Hollow Apts. v. Lewis, No. M9403- CVG-007533 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., May 25, 1994); Vistula Mgmt. Co. v. Scott,
No. 92-CVG-76 (Ohio Mun., Fremont, May 6, 1992). See also Gallatin Hous. Auth.
v. Gifford, No. 89-25-II, 1989 WL 100268 (Tenn. App. Aug. 30, 1989).

The lease may establish standards for this determination, which may be given deference by the courts. However, the standards may not unreasonably interfere with the tenant’s right to invite guests, which is discussed in Section VII.G.1, above. See Ashley Court Enters. v. Whittaker, 249 N.J. Super. 552, 592 A.2d 1228 (1991) (seven days per month limit was an unreasonable interference with the covenant of quiet enjoyment).

For Public Housing and project-based subsidized housing, any lease standard must be reasonable because federal law prohibits unreasonable lease terms. 42 U.S.C.

§ 1437d(l)(2) (Public Housing); 12 U.S.C. § 1715z-1b(b)(3) (project-based subsidized housing). However, i n construing these guest provisions, some courts have found relatively short time periods to be reasonable, if the time period can be extended by prior landlord approval. E.g., Heritage Court L.L.C. v. Merritt, 187 Ohio App.3d 117, 931 N.E.2d 194 (3rd Dis t. 2010) (one -week visitation period for each six months);

Norwich Hous. Auth. v. Majewski , No. CV -219883, 2000 WL 73034 (Conn. Super.
Jan. 13, 2000) (seven days per month).

A natural child of a tenant, who becomes a member of the household after the commencement of the tenancy, cannot be considered a guest or an unauthorized
occupant. See Hicks v. Hubert , No. 96 -CVG-34 (Ohio County Ct., Sandusky Cty., June 4, 1996).
