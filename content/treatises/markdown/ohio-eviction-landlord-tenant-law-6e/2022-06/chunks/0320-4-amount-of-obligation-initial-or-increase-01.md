# 4. Amount of Obligation, Initial or Increase

## Source text

Ohio law does not contain rent control provisions for residential tenancies. See Mesnick v. De Ville Apts., No. 71517, 1997 WL 599376, at *1 (Ohio App., 8th Dist.,
Sept. 25, 1997) (“Ohio law does not place property owners under an obligation to limit their rental prices to any standard of reasonableness”).

Therefore, a tenant’s rental obligat ion generally may be whatever amount the landlord and tenant agree upon, unless the tenant resides in assisted housing.278 A landlord’s right to demand a rent increase to any particular amount is subject to three limitations, which are summarized below.

One limitation to the landlord’s right to demand a rent increase is the term of the tenancy, even if it is a month-to-month tenancy.

To effect a rent increase in a month -to-month tenancy (or any other periodic tenancy), the landlord must terminate the existing tenancy, and the landlord and tenant must enter into a new tenancy with the increased rental obligation. See Steiner v.

Minkowski, 72 Ohio App.3d 754, 596 N.E.2d 492 (6th Dist. 1991).

If the landlord terminates the existing month -to-month tenancy and proposes the new month -to-month tenancy with the increased rental obligation, and the tenant objects to the proposed rent increase but remains in possession of the premises, the tenant then becomes a holdover tenant. Id. See also Fredricks v. Stromber g, No.

42601, 1981 Ohio App. LEXIS 10469, at *8 -15 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Apr. 2, 1981).

In these circumstances, the tenant’s rental obligation is not the increased rental obligation that the landlord proposed. Id. Rather, it is the reasonable value of the tenant’s use and occupancy of the premises. Id.

Since R.C. 5321.17(B) requires a thirty-day notice to terminate a month-to-month tenancy, a month-to-month tenant is generally entitled to thirty days advance notice of any proposed rent increase. Schwenkel v. Daley , No. 92 -CVG-2825 (Ohio Mun., Lakewood, Jan. 19, 1993). But cf. McMahon v. A second limitation to the landlord’s right to demand a rent increase is the R.C.

5321.02 prohibition against retaliation. R.C. 5321.02 prohibits a landlord from demanding a rent increase because the t enant: (1) complained to an appropriate governmental agency regarding code violations that materially affect health and safety;
(2) complained to the landlord regarding violations of R.C. 5321.04; or (3) joined a tenant organization. E.g., Bland v. Kovein, No. 16179, 1993 WL 473837 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Nov. 10, 1993) (rent increase in retaliation for tenant’s complaint to health department). For further discussion on the R.C. 5321.02 prohibition against retaliation, see Section II.T, above, and Chapter XI, below.

A third limitation to the landlord’s right to demand a rent increase is the R.C.

5321.14 prohibition against unconscionable lease provisions. When an alleged unconscionable provision is an increased rent, the relevant issue is whether the amount of the new rent (and not the amount of the rent increase) is unconscionable. Pojman
v. Columbia-Brookpark Mgmt., LLC, No. 88666, 2007 WL 2269682 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Aug. 9, 2007). Given the Pojman holding, an earlier trial court decision, which was vacated on jurisdictional grounds, also erred in holding that the analogous R.C.

3733.16 (now R.C. 4781.48) prohibition against unconscionable lease provisions is inapplicable to rent charges. Columbia-Brook Park Mgmt., LLC v. Schindler, No. 01- CVJ-02061 (Ohio Mun., Berea, Mar. 1, 2002), vacated and dismissed , No. 81092, 2003 WL 1090620 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Mar. 13, 2003).
