# G. Timely Surrender of Possession

## Table-of-contents context

- II. Eviction Action, Common Defenses
  - G. Timely Surrender of Possession

## Source text

The R.C. 1923.04 notice to vacate is a condition precedent to the commencement of any eviction action. See Section I.C.1.a, above. It informs the residential tenant that, “[i]f you do not leave, an eviction action may be initiated against you.” R.C.

1923.04(A). For residential landlords, the purpose of an eviction action is to recover possession of the rental premises, if the tenant does not timely vacate the rental premises in response to the R.C. 1923.04 notice to vacate. See Section I.A, above.

When a tenant timely surrenders possession of the rental premises in response (or
prior) to the service of the R.C. 1923.04 notice to vacate, it is a defense to the landlord’s claim for an eviction judgment. Rejection of the eviction claim is justified because the tenant has compl ied with the R.C. 1923.04 notice to vacate ( i.e., surrendered
possession) and no longer resides in the rental premises. See Castlebrook Apts. v.

Ballard, No. 22421, 2008 WL 4183980, ¶ 22 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Sept. 12, 2008).

In these circumstances, thelandlord has filed an eviction complaint without any factual basis, since the landlord already has possession.

If a tenant vacates the premises and returns the keys for the premises before the eviction hearing, the only issue in the eviction action (i.e., right to immediate
possession) is moot. Richmond’s Enter., Inc. v. Anderson , No. 26674, 2016 WL 685398 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 19, 2016). Therefore, in these cases, the trial court is required to grant a motion to dismiss on mootness grounds. Id.
