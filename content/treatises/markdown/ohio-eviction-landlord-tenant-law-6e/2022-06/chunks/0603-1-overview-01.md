# 1. Overview

## Source text

In Moore v. Lorain Metro. Hous. Auth. , 121 Ohio St.3d 455, 905 N.E.2d 606 (2009), the Supreme Court clarifies the R.C. Chapter 2744 sovereign immunity of public housing authorities (“PHAs) relative to tenant claims.

The Moore decision contains two significant conclusions of law.

First, it holds that “the operation of a public housing authority is a governmental function under R.C. 2744.01(C)(2).” Moore, 121 Ohio St.3d 455, ¶ 19. Therefore, as to tenant claims, PHAs are not liable under the R.C. 2744.02(B)(2) exception “for injury, death, or loss to person or property caused by the negligent performance of acts by their employees with respect to proprietary functions.” Id., ¶ 20 n.5.

Second, it holds that “a unit of public housing is a building ‘used in connection with the performance of a governmental function’ within the meaning of R.C.

2744.01(C)(2).” Id., ¶ 24. Therefore, as to tenant claims, PHAs are li able under the R.C. 2744.02(B)(4) exception for injury, death, or loss to person or property that: (1) is caused by the negligence of a PHA employee, (2) occurs within or on the rental premises, and (3) is due to a physical defect within or on the rental premises. Id.

But if the tenant’s claim arises from a physical defect in Housing Choice Voucher program rental premises, the PHA does have immunity from liability under R.C.

2744.02. Holimon v. Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth. , 180 N.E.3d 1226, 2021-Ohio- 3840 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., 2021). The R.C. 2744.02(B)(4) exception is inapplicable in these cases because, unlike the Public Housing program, the rental premises are privately owned (not governmentally owned) buildings. Id.

R.C. Chapter 2744 does not de fine the term “physical defect” for R.C.

2744.01(C)(2) purposes. However, the appellate courts have adopted a definition, namely: “if the instrumentality that caused the plaintiff’s injury did not operate as intended due to a perceivable condition– or, to put it in the words of theHamrick court, if the instrumentality contained a perceivable imperfection that impaired its worth or utility.” Leasure v. Adena Local Sch. Dist. , 973 N.E.2d 810, 2012 -Ohio-3071, ¶ 20 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., 2012) (improperly s et up bleachers – physical defect). Accord Johnson v. Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth., No. C-210240, 2022 WL 68345 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Jan. 7, 2022) (loose floor mat on top step of stairs– genuine issue on whether it was physical defect and thus summaryjudgment denied);Moncrief v. Bohn, 9 N.E.3d 508, 2014 -Ohio-837, ¶ 15 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., 2014) (lack of adequate security services – no physical defect); Jones v. Delaware City Sch. Dist. Bd. of Educ. , 995 N.E.2d 1252, 2013-Ohio-3907, ¶ 22 (Ohio App.,5th Dist., 2013) (orchestra pit without reflective tape and lights – physical defect); Gibbs v. Columbus Metro. Hous. Auth. ,
No. 11AP-711, 2012 WL 1852086, ¶¶ 13 -16 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., May 22, 2012) (trash chute in which body found – no physical defect); Duncan v. Cuyahoga Cmty.

Coll., 970 N.E.2d 1092, 2012-Ohio-1949, ¶¶ 26,27 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., 2012) (lack of floor mats in self-defense class – no physical defect); Hamrick v. Bryan City School Dist., No. WM-10-014, 2011 WL 2090038, ¶¶ 27,28 (Ohio A pp., 6th Dist., May 27,
2011) (unmarked service pit in bus garage – no physical defect).

PHA employees, as employees of a political subdivision, may rely on their R.C.

2744.03(A)(6) immunity from liability “for injury, death, or loss to person or property allegedly caused by an act or omission in connection with a governmental or proprietary function.” However, this immunity is subject to three exceptions. It does not apply if the “employee’s acts or omissions were manifestly outside the scope of the employee’s employment or official responsibilities.” R.C. 2744.03(A)(6)(a). It does not apply if the “employee’s acts or omissions were with malicious purpose, in 619 bad faith, or in a wanton or reckless manner.”390 R.C. 2744.03(A)(6)(b). And it does not apply if the “[c]ivil liability is expressly imposed upon the employee by a section of the Revised Code.” R.C. 2744.03(A)(6)(c).

The Moore decision does not address the R.C. Chapter 2744 sovereign immunity of PHAs with respect to intentional tort claims. But “courts have generally held that because R.C. 2744.02(B) includes no specific exemption for intentional torts, political subdivisions are immune from intentional tort claims.” Griffits v. Vill. of Newburgh Heights, No. 91428, 2009 WL 280376, ¶ 26 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Feb. 5, 2009).

Accord Zieber v. Heffelfinger, No. 08-CA-0042, 2009 WL 695533, ¶ 27 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Mar. 17, 2009) (“Ohio courts have consistently held that political subdivisions are immune under R.C. 2744.02 from intentional tort claims”).E.g., Rid- All Exterminating Co. v. Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth., No. 98174, 2012 WL 5370978, ¶ 9 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Nov. 1, 2012) (contractor’s fraud claim barred); Kelly v.

Chillicothe Metro. Hous. Auth., No. 10-00430 (Ohio Mun., Chillicothe, Apr. 5, 2011) (tenant’s trespass and conversion claims barred under R.C. 2744.02,.05 immunity).

The R.C. Chapter 2744 sovereign immunity of PHAs does not apply to contract
claims. R.C. 2744.09(A). See also Bozeman v. Cleveland Metro. Hous. Auth. , Nos.

92435,92436, 2009 WL 3321402, ¶ 11 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Oct. 15, 2009);Carter v.

Complete Gen. Constr. Co., No. 08AP-309, 2008 WL 5104787, ¶ 16 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Dec. 4, 2008).

The R.C. Chapter 2744 sovereign immunity of PHAs also does not apply to federal claims (statutory or constitutional). R.C. 2744.09(E).
