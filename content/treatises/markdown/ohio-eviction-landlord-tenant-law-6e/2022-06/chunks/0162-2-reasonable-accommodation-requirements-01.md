# 2. Reasonable Accommodation Requirements

## Source text

The Fair Housing Act requires a landlord “to make reasonable accommodations in the rules, policies, practices, and services,” when i t is necessary to accommodate the needs of a tenant with a disability. 42 U.S.C. § 3604(f)(3)(B). This requirement also exists under Section 504 and the ADA. Roe v. Hous. Auth. of City of Boulder, 909 F.
Supp. 814, 821 (D. Colo. 1995). See also Anast v. Commonwealth Apts., 956 F. Supp.

792, 800 (N.D. Ill. 1997).

In the eviction context, “a reasonable accommodation is required where there is a causal link between the disability for which the accom modation is requested and the misconduct that is the subject of the eviction or other challenged action.”Boston Hous.
Auth. v. Bridgewater, 452 Mass. 833, 848, 898 N.E.2d 848 (2009).

Absent the requisite causal link, the tenant is not entitled to the r easonable
accommodation. See, e.g., Norwich Apts. II v. Sanders, 159 N.E.3d 340, 2020-Ohio- 4540 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., 2020) (insufficient evidence to find a causal link between the disability and the failure to pay rent);Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Brown, No.

C-120580, 2013 WL 5432087, ¶ 18 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Sept. 25, 2013) (insufficient evidence of the requisite link between the disability and the misconduct – threats and yelling at other tenants).

“Once a tenant shows that a disability is the reason for noncompliance with a lease, the tenant must show that an accommodation will allow the tenant to remain compliant with the lease.” Bazelon Center, Fair Housing Information Sheet #4, Using Reasonable Accommodation to Prevent Eviction (rev. Dec. 11, 2003), at 2.

The tenant’s initial request for the reasonable accommodation may be made during the eviction action. Radecki v. Joura, 114 F.3d 115 (8th Cir. 1997);Roe v. Hous. Auth.

of City of Boulder, 909 F. Supp. 814 (D. Colo. 1995);Roe v. Sugar River Mills Assocs., 820 F. Supp. 636 (D.N.H. 1993); Boston Hous. Auth. v. 171 898 N.E.2d 848 (2009); Lebanon County Hous. Auth. v. Landeck, 967 A.2d 1009 (Pa.
Super. 2009); Douglas v. Kriegsfeld Corp., 849 A.2d 951 (D.C. App. 2004); Cornwell & Taylor LLP v. Moore , No. C8-00-1000, 2000 WL 1887528 (Minn. App. Dec. 22, 2000).

The reasonable accommodation requirement does not apply to any tenant whose tenancy would constitute a direct threat to the health or safety of other individuals. 42 U.S.C. § 3604(f)(9) (Fair Housing Act); 24 C.F.R. § 8.3 (qualified individual with
handicaps) (Section 504); 56 Fed. Reg. 35693, 35701 (July 26, 1991) (refers to 28 C.F.R. § 36.208) (ADA). See, e.g., Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Brown , No. C- 120580, 2013 WL 5432087 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Sept. 25, 2013).

However, when determining whether such threat to health and safety exists, the court is required to consider the threat (if any) that would exi st if the reasonable accommodation were granted. Boston Hous. Auth. v. Bridgewate r, 452 Mass. 833, 841, 898 N.E.2d 848 (2009); Douglas v. Kriegsfeld Corp. , 849 A.2d 951, 960 (D.C.
App. 2004); Roe v. Sugar River Mills Assocs., 820 F. Supp. 636, 639-40 (D.N.H. 1993).

The health and safety exception “does not come into play until after an effort for accommodation has been made and the court has determined, through a probing factual inquiry, that no reasonable accommodation could ameliorate the situation sufficiently to protect the health, safety, and property of others.”Douglas v. Kriegsfeld Corp., 849 A.2d 951, 960 (D.C. App. 2004).

Also, the health and safety exception is an affirmative defense to the duty to provide a reasonable accommodation. Twp. of W. Orange v. Whitman , 8 F. Supp.2d 408 (D.N.J. 1998). The burden of proof is on the landlord to prove this defense.

Dadian v. Vill. of Wilmette, 291 F.3d 831 (7th Cir. 2001). The landlord is required to prove that “no ‘reasonable accommodation’ will eliminate or acceptably minimize the risk that he [the tenant] poses to other residents” of the property. Roe v. Sugar River Mills Assocs., 820 F. Supp. 636, 640 (D.N.H. 1993). Accord Roe v. Hous. Auth. of City of Boulder, 909 F. Supp. 814, 822-23 (D. Colo. 1995).

When a landlord has made a reasonable accommodation in the rules and the tenant violates the modified rules, the landlord may evict the tenant for noncompliance with the modified rules. E.g., Parma Pub. Hous. v. Hoag, No. 07-CVG-00695 (Ohio Mun., Parma, May 2, 2007) (accommodation permitted one cat for therapeutic purposes and tenant kept two or more cats, even after landlord’s notice of violation). See also Woodside Vill. v. Hertzmark , No. SPH9204 -65092, 1993 WL 268293 (Conn. Super.

June 22, 1993) (failing to walk dog in designated area and failing to clean up after it), aff’d on other grounds, 36 Conn. App. 73, 647 A.2d 759 (1994).

For a summary of the reasonable accommodation requirement in the eviction context, see the Bazelon Center, Fair Housing Information Sheet #4, Using Reasonable Accommodation to Prevent Eviction (rev. Dec. 11, 2003). For articles on the reasonable accommodation requirement in the eviction context, see Liam Garland, Fairer Housing for People with Disabilities: Securing an In-Place Tenant’s Right to Reasonable Accommodation, 40 Clearinghouse Rev. 503 (2007); Fred Fuchs, Using Reasonable-Accommodation Provision of the Fair Housing Act to Prevent the Eviction of a Tenant with Disabilities, 41 Clearinghouse Rev. 272 (2007).

Under the Supremacy Clause, state courts are required to consider Fair Housing Act defenses in eviction actions. Rodriguez v. Westhab, Inc. , 833 F. Supp. 425 (S.D.N.Y. 1993).

Ohio courts recognize the right of tenants to assert a Fair Housing Act defen se in eviction actions. E.g., Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Brown , No. C-120580, 2013 WL 5432087, ¶¶ 2-5,11-19 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Sept. 25, 2013);K&D Mgmt., L.L.C.
v. Masten, No. 98894, 2013 WL 3422890, ¶¶ 15 -17,19 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., July 3, 2013); Olympic Realty v. Zaleski, No. 11AP-971, 2013 WL 1303777, ¶ 15 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Mar. 29, 2013); Palombo v. Hayes, Nos. 65781,66714, 1995 WL 229120, at *7-8 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Apr. 13, 1995). See also Lable & Co. v. Flowers, 104 Ohio App.3d 227, 661 N.E.2d 782 (9th Dist. 1995) (fair housing counterclaim in FED action); Gladden v. Walker, No. 12-CV-29809 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., Aug. 15,
2014) (fair housing third-party complaint in FED action).
