## Source text

R.C. 1923.04(A) specifies three alternative methods for serving a notice to vacate:
(1) certified mail, return receipt requested; (2) personal service; and (3) leaving it at the tenant’s usual place of abode or at the premises.

The third method – leaving the notice at the premises – permits the landlord to leave the notice at the premises without physical delivery to a person. Atwood v. Bare,
No. 1854, 1988 WL 117999 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Oct. 28, 1988).

Under the third method, a landlord may serve the notice by posting it on the outside of the exterior door. Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Morgan , 104 Ohio St.3d 445, 820 N.E.2d 315 (2004); Hauke v. Harris , No. 2005 -CA-000003, 2005 WL 3316338 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Dec. 8, 2005); Hall v. Kottha , No. 64965, 1994 WL 110963 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Mar. 31, 1994);Chillicothe Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Anderson, No.

1406, 1988 WL 69118 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., June 28, 1988). Posting means properly securing the notice to the door – thumbtack, adhesive tape, glue, etc. – and not simply leaving it in the crack of the door. Oakwood Mgmt. Co. v. Bryant, No. 04-CVG-0057 (Ohio Mun., Licking Cty., Mar. 17, 2004).

The service by posting does not, on its face, violate due process.Cincinnati Metro.
Hous. Auth. v. Morgan, 104 Ohio St.3d 445, 820 N.E.2d 315 (2004). But, if the rental unit is located in a neighborhood where notices frequently are removed from exterior doors, an “as applied” due process claim may exist. Id.

“Service of [the R.C. 1923.04] notice in a manner not specified in the statute is not proper [service].” Youngstown Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Higgins , No. 99 -CA-81, 2000 WL 1159418, at *2 (Ohio App., 7th Dist., Aug. 4, 2000). See Manifold v. Schuster, 67 Ohio App.3d 251, 586 N.E.2d 1142 (4th Dist. 1990).

For example, regular mail service is not a permissible method of service because it is not expressly authorized under R.C. 1923.04(A). Higgins, 2001 WL 1159418, at *2; Tompkins v. Goins, No. 93-CVG-01097 (Ohio Mun., Zanesville, Dec. 13, 1993).

Nonetheless, one appellate courtpermitted regular mail service where the landlord first attempted certified mail service and it was refused, there was no evidence that the tenant failed to receive the notice to vacate via the regular mail service, and, based on the date of the regular mail service, there was sufficient time for that service to deliver the notice timely. Aultman Home for Aged Women v. Ott , No. CA -7688, 1989 WL 64055 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., June 5, 1989).
