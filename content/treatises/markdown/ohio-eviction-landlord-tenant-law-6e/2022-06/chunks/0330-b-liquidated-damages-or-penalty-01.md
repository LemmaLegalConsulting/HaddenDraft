# b. Liquidated Damages or Penalty

## Source text

Late payment fees are subject to the contract law on liquidated damages and
penalties. The rule for distinguishing between contract terms that are enforceable liquidated damages and contract terms that are unenforceable penalties is:

Where the parties have agreed on the amount of damages, ascertained by estimation and adjustment, and have expressed this agreement in clear and unambiguous terms, the amount so fixed should be treated as liquidated damages and not as a penalty, if the damages would be
(1) uncertain as to amount and difficult of proof, and if (2) the contract as a whole is not so manifestly unconscionable, unreasonable, and disproportionate in amount as to justify the conclusion that it does not express the true intentions of the parties, and if (3) the contract is consistent with the conclusion that it was the intention of the parties that damages in the amount stated should follow the breach thereof.

Samson Sales, Inc. v. Honeywell, Inc ., 12 Ohio St.3d 27, 465 N.E.2d 392, syllabus (1984). Accord Lake Ridge Acad. v. Carney , 66 Ohio St.3d 376, 613 N.E.2d 183 (1993); Mark-It Place Foods, Inc. v. New Plan Excel Realty Trust, Inc ., 156 Ohio App.3d 65, 804 N.E.2d 979 (4th Dist. 2004).

The principal elements of the Honeywell test, for determining whether a late payment fee is a penalty, are (1) whether the damages are uncertain as to amount and difficult of proof and (2) whether the amount of the fee is reasonably related to the landlord’s actual damages.

The Eighth Appellate District applied these factors in two late payment fee cases:

Siara Mgmt. v. Nedley , No. 61433, 1992 WL 2 92248 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Oct. 15, 1992), and 200 W. Apts. v. Foreman , No. 66107, 1994 WL 505271 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Sept. 15, 1994).

In Nedley, the court held that “the damages flowing from a breach of a lease are not uncertain as to amount and difficult of proof.” 1992 WL 292248, at *1. Based on this holding and the Honeywell test, the court held that the late payment fee was a penalty and thus unenforceable. See also Beatley v. Schwartz , No. 03AP -911, 2004 WL 1244360, ¶ 26 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., June 8, 2004) (“Clearly damages resulting from the breach of a residential lease are not difficult to prove or uncertain as to
amount. Thus, plaintiff’s argument that he is entitled to the security deposit because of the ‘liquidated damage fee’ provision in the lease is without merit”).

In Foreman, the court held that the late payment fee was not reasonably related to the landlord’s actual damages because the landlord failed to adduce any evidence that it had sustained actual damages. The court held that the tenant’s failure to pay rent for two months did not constitute actual damages because the landlord was awarded a money judgment for the unpaid rent. 281 Based on these holdings, the court held that the late payment fee was a penalty and thus unenfor ceable. See also ATKM Mgmt.

The Nedley and Foreman rulings may support the conclusion that, with respect to any late payment fee under a residential lease, the fee is a penalty and thus
unenforceable. See Calabria v. Greene , No. 95 -T-5181, 1995 WL 787360, at *1 -2 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Sept. 8, 1995). E.g., Hall v. Leonard, No. 94-CG-667 (Ohio Mun., Marietta, Mar. 7, 1995).

In Calabria, 1995 WL 787360, at *2, the Eleventh Appellate District expressly refused to follow the Nedley and Foreman rulings and, instead, “concluded that an agreed upon, one-time late fee, that is reasonable in proportion to the rental rate, and that has a rational basis supporting the imposition of the charge, is proper.” In Drake Townhouses, LLC v.
