Fictitious Name Registration R.C. 1329.10 bars any person doing business under a fictitious (or trade) name from commencing or maintaining an action in that name, unless the fictitious name has been registered with the Secretary of State in compliance with R.C. 1329.01. Buckeye Foods v. Cuyahoga County Bd. of Revision, 78 Ohio St.3d 459, 678 N.E.2d 917 (1997).

Therefore, if a landlord is doing business under a fictitious name that has not been properly registered, the landlord is barred from commencing or maintaining an eviction
action. Fisco v. Chisholm, Nos. 52111,52112, 1987 WL 11193 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., May 7, 1987); Oxford Square Apts. v. Nicholson, No. 07-CVG-657 (Ohio County Ct., Tuscarawas Cty., Dec. 19, 2007); Wee v. Lawrence , No. 01 -CV-15817 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., Oct. 1, 2001); Alwood Enters. v. Vance, No. 87-CVG-32 (Ohio Mun., Hillsboro, Oct. 21, 1987). Cf. Ebner v. Caudill, 93 Ohio App.3d 785, 639 N.E.2d 1231 (10th Dist. 1994) (action for collection of rent).

When a landlord owns and operates a rental property under a properly registered fictitious name, the landlord may bring , in either the landlord’s name or the fictitious name, an eviction action (and monetary claims) against the tenant. Woods v. Marcano, 122 N.E.3d 633, 2018-Ohio-4324 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., 2018).

Similarly, R.C. 1777.04 bars any partnership transacting business under a fictitious name from commencing or maintaining an action in the partnership name, unless the name has been registered with the county recorder in compliance with R.C. 1777.02.

R.C. 1329.10(B) “provides that an action may not be commenced or maintained until the name is registered.” Frate v. Al -Sol, Inc., 131 Ohio App.3d 283, 287, 722 N.E.2d 185 (8th Dist. 1999). “R.C. 1329.10(B) ... provides expressly that such action may not be commenced ... until first complying with the licensing [registration] requirements.” Nat’l Crime Reporting, Inc. v. McCord & Akamine, L.L.P., 177 Ohio App.3d 551, 555-56, 895 N.E.2d 255 (10th Dist. 2008).

A failure to register a fictitious name in compliance with R.C. 1329.10 (or R.C.

1777.02) “denies the user of [the] fictitious name the capacity to sue.”159 Frate, 131 Ohio App.3d at 287. Accord Nat’l Crime Reporting, Inc., 177 Ohio App.3d at 555-56.

Since R.C. 1329.10(B) denies the user of a fictitious name the capacity to sue, the controlling facts are those at the time of the commencement of the action. “Capacity to sue ‘refers to the eligibility of a person to commence an action.” Wanamaker v.

Davis, No. 2005 -CA-151, 2007 WL 2405708, ¶ 43 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Aug. 24, 2007). See, e.g. , Nat’l Crime Reporting, Inc. , 177 Ohio App.3d at 554 (“the trial court’s rationale for granting the dismissal was that at the time of the filing of the complaint, the plaintiff lacked the capacity to maintain an action in Ohio due to its failure to obtain a license pursuant to R.C. 1703.29”).

Therefore, any registration with the secretary of state after the commenc ement of the action is immaterial to the plaintiff’s capacity to sue.

The Dodd and MBA Realty interpretation of R.C. 1329.10(B) violates a fundamental rule of statutory construction, namely: “The entire statute is intended to be effective.” R.C. 1.47(B). Accord D.A.B.E., Inc. v. Toledo -Lucas County Bd. of Health, 96 Ohio St.3d 250, 256, 773 N.E.2d 536 (2002). By effectively limiting the scope of the prohibition to maintenance of an action, it renders inoperative the statute’s prohibition against commencement of an action.

The Dodd and MBA Realty decisions misconstrue the R.C. 1329.10(B) phrase “but upon compliance, such an action may be commenced or maintained on any contracts or transactions entered into prior to compliance.” This phrase simply means that, when an action is dismissed under R.C. 1329.10(B), it shall be dismissed without prejudice.
