Ribovich, 90 Ohio St.3d 439, 739 N.E.2d 333 (2000);Colonial Am. Dev. Co. v. Griffith, 76 Because Pappenhagen is a pre -2006 court decision, it cites to the corresponding pre -2006 Civil Rule 53(E)(3)(b) (see note 7, above).

93 48 Ohio St.3d 72, 549 N.E.2d 513 (1990);77 Oneida Props., Inc. v. Pickett, No. 24626, 2009 WL 3119647 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Sept. 30, 2009) (applies Miele and Griffith to Civil Rule 53(D)(4)(e)(i)); Lewis v. Powers , No. 15461, 1997 WL 335563 (Ohio
App. 2nd Dist., June 13, 1997).

Nevertheless, “the court retains its discretion t o grant a stay while it reviews objections.” Calhoun v. Ivey , No. 2006 -CVG-27001, slip op. at 1 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Mar. 28, 2007). “The court can protect a plaintiff’s interest in prompt execution of judgment by ruling promptly on objections when it has granted a discretionary stay.” Id., slip op. at 2.

Therefore, it is often necessary and appropriate for a tenant to move for a stay of execution pending the court’s ruling on the tenant’s objections. The customary time period between the filing of a magistrate’s decision and the execution of the writ of restitution (see Section I.O.2, below) varies significantly among jurisdictions, but it may be as short as a few days.

If a tenant does not secure a stay pending a ruling on the tenant’sobjections and is consequently evicted under a writ of restitution, the eviction action may be subject to a dismissal as moot, without any ruling on the merits of the objections. See, e.g., Gold Key Realty v. Collins, No. 2013-CA-57, 2014 WL 5409159 (OhioApp., 2nd Dist., Oct.

24, 2014).
