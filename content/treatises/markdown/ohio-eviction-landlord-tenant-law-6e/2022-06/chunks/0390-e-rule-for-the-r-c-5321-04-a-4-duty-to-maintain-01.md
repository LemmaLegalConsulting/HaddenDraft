Since November 1998, the Second Appellate District has modified the Shroades (now Shroades/Sikora) notice for R.C. 5321.04(A)(4) cases. Lansdale v. Dursch, No.

16858, 1998 WL 769852 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Nov. 6, 1998) (carbon monoxide emanating from the furnace). See also Hammaker v. Ohio Edison, Inc. , No. 98-CA-37, 1999 WL 6444 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Jan. 8, 1999) (defect in electrical wiring).

In R.C. 5321.04(A)(4) cases, the Second Appellate District generally does not require actual or constructive notice to the lan dlord of the defective condition. Id.

Instead, it requires actual or constructive notice to the landlord of the improper maintenance of the appliance or fixture (“Lansdale notice rule”). Id.308 The Lansdale notice rule is premised on the difference between a duty to maintain and a duty to repair. Id. A duty to maintain requires a landlord to take preventative action before a defective condition occurs, 309 whereas a duty to repair requires a landlord to remedy an existing defective condition. Id.

The notice required by the Lansdale notice rule “may be demonstrated by, inter alia, evidence regarding the procedures necessary to maintain the particular appliance;

any inspection, testing, or lack of the same, of the appliance for defects; or the landlord’s schedule for regular maintenance or lack thereof.” Lansdale, 1998 WL 769852, at *4.

The Second Appellate District does not apply theLansdale notice rule if the latent defect “would not be ... ordinarily discoverable by an inspection ordered by the landlord.” Hammaker v.
