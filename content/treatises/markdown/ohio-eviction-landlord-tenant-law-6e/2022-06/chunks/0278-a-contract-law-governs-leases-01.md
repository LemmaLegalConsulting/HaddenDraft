# A. Contract Law Governs Leases

## Source text

“It is well established that leases are contracts and, as such, are subject to traditional rules governing contract interpretation.” Heritage Court, L.L.C. v. Merritt, 187 Ohio App.3d 117, 931 N.E. 2d 194, ¶ 14 (3rd Dist. 2010). Accord Krantz v.

Pahnke, No. 2021-CA-0043, 2022 WL 39486,¶ 42 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Jan. 5, 2022);

Langfan v. Carlton Gardens Co. , 183 Ohio App.3d 260, 916 N.E.2d 1079, ¶ 9 (3rd
Dist. 2009); Brown v. Spitzer Chevrolet Co. , 181 Ohio App.3d 642, 910 N.E.2d 490, ¶ 36 (5th Dist. 2009); Stauffer v. TGM Camelot, Inc., No. CA2005-12-508, 2006 WL 1974612, ¶ 12 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., July 17, 2006); Pool v. Insignia Residential Group, 136 Ohio App.3d 266, 270, 736 N.E.2d 507 (1st Dist. 1999); Christe v. GMS
Mgmt. Co., 124 Ohio App.3d 84, 88, 705 N.E.2d 691 (9th Dist. 1997); Russell Realty
Co. v. Feghali, 121 Ohio App.3d 291, 294, 699 N.E.2d 976 (8th Dist. 1997);Delker v.

Kerr, No. L-96-110, 1996 WL 532319, at *2 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Sept. 20, 1996).

See also U.S. Corrections Corp. v. Ohio Dep’t of Indus. Relations, 73 Ohio St.3d 210, 216, 652 N.E.2d 766 (1995) (“a lease is a contract”).

The purpose of lease construction is to discover and effectuate the intent of the
parties. Krantz v. Pahnke, No. 2021-CA-0043, 2022 WL 39486, ¶ 42 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Jan. 5, 2022); Heritage Court, L.L.C. v. Merritt, 187 Ohio App.3d 117, 931 N.E.

2d 194, ¶ 14 (3rd Dist. 2010); Stauffer v. TGM Camelot, Inc. , No. CA2005 -12-508, 2006 WL 1974612, ¶ 13 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., July 17, 2006); Local Marketing Corp.
v. Prudential Ins. Co. of Am. , 159 Ohio App.3d 410, 824 N.E.2d 122, ¶ 8 (1st Dist.

2004); Wauseon Plaza Ltd. P’ship v. Wauseon Hardware Co., 156 Ohio App.3d 575, 807 N.E.2d 953, ¶ 26 (6th Dist. 2004);Buckeye Union Ins. Co. v. Consol. Stores Corp., 68 Ohio App.3d 19, 24, 587 N.E.2d 391 (10th Dist. 1990).

The intent of the parties is presumed to reside in the language that the parties chose to use in their lease agreement. Id.

When the language of a lease is clear and unambiguous, the court must follow and enforce the plain language of the lease as written, and not look beyond the plain language of the lease to determine intent. Langfan v. Carlton Gardens Co., 183 Ohio App.3d 260, 916 N.E.2d 1079, ¶ 18 (3rd Dist. 2009); Brown v. Spitzer Chevrolet Co., 181 Ohio App.3d 642, 910 N.E.2d 490, ¶ 36 (5th Dist. 2009);Stauffer v. TGM Camelot, Inc., No. CA2005-12-508, 2006 WL 1974612, ¶ 13 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., July 17, 2006); Pool v. Insignia Residential Group, 136 Ohio App.3d 266, 270,736 N.E.2d 507 (1st Dist. 1999); Russell Realty Co. v. Feghali, 121 Ohio App.3d 291, 294, 699 N.E.2d 976 (8th Dist. 1997); Delker v. Kerr , No. L -96-110, 1996 WL 532319, at *2 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Sept. 20, 1996).

When the language of a lease provision i s ambiguous, the court may consider parole evidence in its determination of the meaning of the provision. Altman v.

Kardous Bayard Props., No. C-200406, 2021 WL 4269933 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Sept.

15, 2021). For a discussion on contract construction rul es, relative to an ambiguous lease forfeiture provision, see Section II.X, above.

“In interpreting the language of a lease agreement, common words are presumed to hold their ordinary meaning, unless ‘(1) manifest absurdity results, or (2) some other meaning is clearly evidenced from the instrument.’”Heritage Court, L.L.C. v. Merritt, 187 Ohio App.3d 117, 931 N.E. 2d 194, ¶ 14 (3rd Dist. 2010). Accord Alexander v.

Buckeye Pipe Line Co., 53 Ohio St.2d 241, 245-46, 374 N.E.2d 146 (1997).
