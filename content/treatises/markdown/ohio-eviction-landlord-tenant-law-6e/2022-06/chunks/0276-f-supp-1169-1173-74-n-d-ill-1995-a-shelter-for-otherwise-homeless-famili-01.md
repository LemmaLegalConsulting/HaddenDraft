It erroneously determines that the general presumption does not app ly (see Section VI.F.2, above) and then erroneously determines that the nine-month occupancies at the “Project Share” transitional housing facility meet the R.C. 5321.01(C)(10) “transient occupancy” requirement.

The Parker court presents two basic grounds in support of its transient occupancy determination, each of which is without merit. The first ground is that the nature of the facility suggests a transient occupancy. The second ground is that the expectation of the occupants is that the stay is temporary.

Neither ground has merit for the reasons discussed in detail in the Reply Brief of Appellant, in theParker appeal. Three examples of those reasons are as follows. First, the court’s reliance on the dwelling unit being furnished and without cooking facilities is misplaced because tenants often lease furnished dwelling units and a dwelling unit may be (and, in this case, was) simply a “sleeping place” ( see R.C. 5321.01(F)).

Second, the court’s reliance on the occupant’s understanding that his nine-month stay “was temporary” is misplaced because (1) every tenant understands that the tenant’s stay is limited to the lease term, (2) virtually all residential leases are month-to-month or one-year terms, and (3) therefore, the nine -month occupancy at th e Project Share facility is no more temporary than the typical residential tenancy. Third, the court’s reliance on the social service requirements for the occupants is misplaced because rental agreement provisions that require the tenant to participate i n certain social service programs are not unusual (see, e.g., 24 C.F.R. § 582.315(b) (Shelter Plus Care rental subsidy program)).
