# E. Residential Premises

## Table-of-contents context

- VI. Ohio Landlord-Tenant Act,
  - E. Residential Premises

## Source text

Residential premises are “a dwelling unit for residential use and occupancy and the structur e of which it is a part, the facilities and appurtenances therein, and the grounds, areas, and facilities for the use of tenants generally or the use of which is promised the tenant.” R.C. 5321.01(C).

A dwelling unit is “a structure or the part of a struc ture that is used as a home, residence, or sleeping place by one person who maintains a household or by two or more persons who maintain a common household.” R.C. 5321.01(D).

“The yard of a single-family home is considered to be part of the tenant’s property to possess and control.” Good v. Murd, No. L-13-1235, 2014 WL 2170302,¶ 10 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., May 23, 2014). Accord Brown v. Terrell, 114 N.E.3d 783, 2018-Ohio- 2503, ¶ 13 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., 2018).

The common areas of residential premises “generally include areas such as sidewalks, parking lots, foyers, hallways ... which [are] reasonably necessary to the use and enjoyment of the leased property.” Hau v. Gill , No. 98 -CA-007061, 1999 WL 492633, at *5 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., July 14, 1999). Accord Burrell v. Iwenofu , No.

81230, 2003 WL 1090635, ¶¶ 15,16 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Mar. 13, 2003). They do not include “public sidewalks abutting the property.”Ryder v. McGlone’s Rentals, No.

3-09-02, 2009 WL 1664777, ¶ 21 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., June 15, 2009).

Residential premises include only those portions of the premises that are for the use of tenants. For example, residential premises do not include grounds tha t are left in a wild and natural state to serve as a privacy screen. Kueber v. Haas , 47 Ohio App.3d 62, 546 N.E.2d 1351 (1st Dist. 1988). Likewise, residential premises do not include a space that is under renovation and behind a door marked “Danger-Keep Out.” Lukehart v. W. Ninth St. Partners , 137 Ohio App.3d 361, 738 N.E.2d 839 (8th Dist.

2000).

Residential premises include dwelling units that a college owns or operates, but student tenants who occupy those dwelling units are denied two basic tenant ri ghts under R.C. Chapter 5321. R.C. 5321.01(H), 5321.031, 5321.07(D). First, these student tenants may not rent deposit or use the other R.C. 5321.07 tenant remedies (see Chapter VIII, below). R.C. 5321.07(D). Second, a college may terminate the rental agreement of a student tenant and, without filing an eviction action, require the student tenant to vacate the dwelling unit, so long as the college follows its notice and hearing
procedures. R.C. 5321.031. See also R.C. 1923.02(D).

R.C. 5321.01(C) speci fies ten types of properties or occupancies that are not residential premises for R.C. Chapter 5321 purposes. They are prisons, hospitals, transient occupancies in hotels and other similar facilities, certain elementary and secondary boarding schools, orp hanages, certain farm residences, certain agriculture labor camps, occupancies by an owner of a condominium unit, certain occupancies in certain licensed SRO facilities, and transient occupancies in non -profit emergency
shelters. R.C. 5321.01(C)(1)-(10).

Under the R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) exception, residential premises do not include the rental of a vacation house in a recreational area for a short -term, such as four days.

Tomasko v. Sohnly , No. 15CAE -10-0078, 2016 WL 1643036 (Ohio App., 5th Dist.,
Apr. 21, 2016).

The R.C. 5321.01(C)(9)(a)(ii) exception, relative to shelters for homeless persons, is not limited to facilities that only take persons literally from the streets. Coyne v.

301 YWCA of Greater Toledo, No. CVG-06-15975 (Ohio Mun., Toledo, Hous. Div., Nov.

7, 2006) (preliminary injunction denied).

For a discussion on the term “transient occupancy,” for R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) and R.C. 5321.01(C)(10) purposes, see Section VI.F., below.
