# b. Open and Obvious Doctrine Inapplicable

## Source text

“A violation of R.C. 5321.04(A)(3) constitutes negligence per se and obviates the open-and-obvious doctrine.” Mann v. Northgate Investors, L.L.C. , 138 Ohio St.3d 175, 5 N.E.3d 594, ¶ 33 (2014).

In Mann, and in the corresponding R.C. 5321.04(A)(1) decision in Robinson v.

Bates, 112 Ohio St.3d 17, 857 N.E.2d 1195 (2006), the Supreme Court is applying well-established Ohio law: “[W]hen the General Assembly has enacted statutes the violation of which constitute negligence per se, the open-and-obvious doctrine will not protect a defendant from liability.” Lang v. Holly Hill Motel, Inc., 122 Ohio St.3d 120, 909 N.E.2d 120, ¶ 15 (2009).

These Supreme Court decisions overrule the earlier appellate court decisions that relied on common law and the analysis in LaCourse v. Fleitz, 28 Ohio St.3d 209, 503 N.E.2d 159 (1986), to justify their use of the open and obvious doctrine to define the 323 scope of the R.C. 5321.04(A)(3) obligation. 271 Those earlier de cisions include:

Mowery v. Shoaf, 148 Ohio App.3d 403, 773 N.E.2d 1053 (7th Dist. 2002) (no duty – lack of exterior lighting in the common area); Howson v. Amorose, No. 00AP-8, 2000 WL 1753112, at *3 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Nov. 30, 2000) (no duty – water-filled pothole in the parking lot); McDaniels v. Petrosky , No. 97 -APE08-1027, 1998 WL 54363, at *4 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Feb. 5, 1998) (no duty – tree stump in the backyard); Wiggans v. Glock , No. 15967, 1997 WL 111745 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist.,
Mar. 14, 199 7) (no duty – excessive grass clippings resulting from lawn mowing);

Hayes v. Murtha, No. 96-APE04-512, 1996 WL 589268 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Oct.

10, 1996) (no duty – wet grass).

For discussions on the open and obvious doctrine in negligence claims, seeSection IX.B.7.a, below (R.C. 5321.04 negligence per se claims) and Section IX.C.2, below (common law premises liability claims).
