# c. Specificity of the Notice – General Rule

## Source text

The notice “need not specifically express the state of the defect with particularity beyond the scope of a layperson’s knowledge.” McKenzie v. Marlowe , No. 70714, 1996 WL 715502, at *5 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Dec. 12, 1996). Accord Wilhelm v.

Heritage Mgmt. Co., No. CA97-07-144, 1998 WL 24342, at *3 (Ohio App., 12th Dist.,
Jan. 26, 1998); Blakley v. Riley, No. 91AP-597, 1992 WL 1163, at *2 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Jan. 7, 1992).

However, the required specificity is not met where the notice informs the landlord in general terms that the premises are in defective condition but does not specify any particular defect. Palazzi v. Donohue , No. 92 -CA-005319, 1992 WL 368874 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Dec. 2, 1992).

Eight court decisions that illustrate the required specificity are: Robinson v.

Larchmont E. Apts., Inc. , No. L -12-1323, 2014 WL 4049874 (Ohi o App., 6th Dist.,
Aug. 15, 2014);Walker v. RLI Enters., Inc., No. 89325, 2007 WL 4442725 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Dec. 20, 2007); Scott v. Kirby , No. L -05-1287, 2006 WL 1047480 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Apr. 21, 2006); Wilhelm v. Heritage Mgmt. Co. , No. CA97 -07-144, 1998 WL 24342 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Jan. 26, 1998); Davis v. Burns, No. 9-97-39, 1997 WL 638276 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., Oct. 10, 1997); Obermiller v. Tahya, No. 91- CA-005092, 1992 WL 41275 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Mar. 4, 1992); Blakley v. Riley,
No. 91AP-597, 1992 WL 1163 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Jan. 7, 1992); and White v.

Fuller, No. CA-84-15, 1984 WL 7584 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Nov. 27, 1984).

Robinson concerned damages resulting from a fire caused by defective wiring inside the wall of the utility room. The court held that the shock that the tenant received from the garbage disposal switch did not provide the landlord with constructivenotice of the faulty wiring in the building.

Walker concerned damages resulting from a fall in a pothole in a parking lot, which was filled with potholes. The court held that, because the landlord was on notice that the parking lot was filled with potho les, the landlord was on notice of the specific pothole.

Scott concerned damages resulting from a fall, when a guest started to step off a porch that chipped away and onto stairs that had no handrail. The court held that, based on a discussion with the tenant regarding the poor appearance of the steps, the landlord should have known that the steps and porch were in need of repair.

Wilhelm concerned damages resulting from a fire caused by an alleged defect in the electrical system. In reversing a summaryjudgment for the landlord, the court held that the tenants’ notices met the specificity requirement and that material issues of fact existed as to proximate cause. One tenant complained that “her electricity would blink on and off repeatedly.” 1998 WL 24342, at *3. A second tenant complained that she “had problems with an outlet near her balcony and that whenever she plugged in anything, sparks would fly out and if you turned on the light switch for the balcony, you would get a red flash of light.”Id. A third tenant complained about problems with a ceiling fan going off and “lighting would flicker off and on quite a bit.” Id.

Davis concerned damages resulting from a fall caused by a step breaking when the tenant stepped on it. The court held that the tenant’s notice met the specificity
requirement. The tenant notified the landlord that “the stairwell was defective as it swayed under a person’s movement.” 1997 WL 638276, at *2. “Although appellant [tenant] did not specifically complain about the con dition of the step to appellee [landlord], he did complain about the general condition of the stairwell.” Id.

Obermiller concerned damages resulting from a fall caused by a front step
dislodging. The court reversed a summary judgment for the landlord, since reasonable minds could reach different results as to whether the landlord had sufficient knowledge of the defect. The landlord knew that the bricks were deteriorating, there were several cracks and gaps in the caulking, and it was loose around the boards.

Blakley concerned damages resulting from a fire caused by over-amperaged fuses.

The court held that the tenant’s notice met the specificity requirement. The tenant notified the landlord of light fixtures in several rooms failing to work with regular ity and a first -floor outlet fire. These notices constituted notice to the landlord that the electrical system in the house was in a state of disrepair and thus notice of the over - amperaged fuses.

White concerned damages resulting from a carbon monoxide leak from a stove caused by a clogged chimney. The court held that the tenant’s notices did not meet the specificity requirement. The tenant’s notices and the landlord’s repairs of the stove, relative to a small fire and a burned -out motor fan, were not notice to the landlord of any other problem with the stove, the stove pipe leading to the chimney, or the chimney.
