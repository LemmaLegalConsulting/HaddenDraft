# E. R.C. 5321.04 Emotional Distress Claims

## Source text

When a landlord’s failure to remedy an R.C. 5321.04(A) violation causes the tenant to suffer emotional distr ess that is both serious and disabling, the tenant may have a claim against the landlord for intentional, reckless, or negligent infliction of serious emotional distress. E.g., Allen v. Lee, 43 Ohio App.3d 31, 538 N.E.2d 1073 (8th Dist. 1987); Sanders v. McGee, No. 13 -CV-003983 (Ohio C.P., Franklin Cty.,
Apr. 3, 2014); Woods v. Coleman, No. 01-CVG-444 (Ohio Mun., Jackson Cty., Aug.

6, 2001); Kenwood Courts Apts. v. Williams, No. K90-CVG-1043 (Ohio Mun., Portage Cty., Oct. 25, 1991). See also Birkenhead v.Coombs, 465 A.2d 244 (Vt. 1983);Simon
v. Solomon, 385 Mass. 91, 431 N.E.2d 556 (1982); Dorgan v. Loukas, 19 Mass. App.
Ct. 959, 473 N.E.2d 1151 (1985). See generally Yeager v. Local Union 20, Etc. , 6 Ohio St.3d 369, 453 N.E.2d 666 (1983) (intentional); Schultz v. Barberton Glass Co., 4 Ohio St.3d 131, 447 N.E.2d 109 (1983) (negligent).

In Allen, the tenant, a fifty -eight-year-old woman who suffered from multiple physical disabilities, recovered emotional distress damages in the amount of $5,000.

The R.C. 5321.04(A) violations included an infestation of rats and roaches, broken windows, large holes in interior walls, holes to the basement in the bathroom floor, a corroded bathtub that would not drain, and stained and rusty sinks.

In Sanders, the tenant recovered $30,000 for the physical and emotional pain and suffering of the tenant and her children. The R.C. 5321.04 violations included, for a period of seven months: (1) a severe bed bug infestation that caused physical injury and made the bedrooms unusa ble; and (2) a virtually unusable bathroom because of the strong urine scent and the rusted our condition of the tub.

451 In Woods, the tenant recovered emotional distress damages in the amount of $5,000. The R.C. 5321.04(A) violations were the absence of run ning water and the absence of a working toilet for a period of six months.

In Kenwood Courts Apts., the tenant recovered emotional distress damages in the amount of $2,000. The R.C. 5321.04(A) violations included repeated flooding of the premises, plumbing problems, lack of screens and air conditioning, as well as many miscellaneous problems.

The elements of a tenant’s claim against a landlord for serious emotional distress are: (1) the landlord either intended to cause the tenant to suffer emotional distress or knew or should have known that the landlord’s actions or omissions would cause the tenant to suffer emotional distress, (2) the landlord’s conduct was so extreme and outrageous as to go beyond the bounds of decency and could be considered as utterl y intolerable in a civilized community, (3) the landlord’s actions or omissions were the proximate cause of the tenant’s emotional distress, and (4) the emotional distress that the tenant suffered was serious. See Pyle v. Pyle, 11 Ohio App.3d 31, 34, 463 N.E.2d 98 (8th Dist. 1983).

Contemporaneous physical injury is not necessary to succeed on a serious emotional distress claim. Id.

Also, a tenant does “not need expert medical testimony to establish that the landlord’s conduct caused her emotional injury, if other evidence sufficiently demonstrate[s] its gravity and cause.”Allen v. Lee, 43 Ohio App.3d 31, 34, 538 N.E.2d 1073 (8th Dist. 1987). Accord Paugh v. Hanks , 6 Ohio St.3d 72, 451 N.E.2d 759 (1983); Uebelacker v. Cincom Sys., Inc. , 48 Ohio App.3d 2 68, 549 N.E.2d 1210 (1st
Dist. 1988); Foster v. McDevitt, 31 Ohio App.3d 237, 511 N.E.2d 403 (2nd Dist. 1986).

However, while expert medical testimony is not necessarily required, the Paugh “guarantee of genuineness” test requires the plaintiff to “present some evidence beyond the plaintiff’s own testimony that he or she experienced emotional distress due to the defendant’s actions.”Buckman-Pierson v. Brannon, 159 Ohio App.3d 12, 822 N.E.2d 830, ¶ 56 (2nd Dist. 2004).

The importance of medical testimony is illustrated by the decisions in Menges v.

Superior Mobile Homes, Inc. , No. 94AP-100068, 1995 WL 498820 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., July 12, 1995), andChilders v. Russell, Nos. 01-4142,4145, 2002 WL 31950741 (Ohio C.P., Montgomery Cty., Feb. 7, 2002). Theemotional distress claim in Menges concerned sewage back-ups into a manufactured home. The emotional distress claim in Childers concerned a wrongful termination of water service to a manufactured
home. In both cases, the claims were denied because of the absence of medical testimony relative to the alleged emotional distress.

For these claims, the tenant should also be mindful that “Ohio ... does not permit recovery for negligent infliction of emotional distress caused by witnessing the destruction of one ’s property.” Stechler v. Homyk , 127 Ohio App.3d 396, 399, 713 N.E.2d 44 (8th Dist. 1998) (neighbor tenant negligently left water running, which overflowed into tenant’s apartment).
