# 2. Pay to Stay Defense in Eviction Actions

## Source text

At least eight Ohio municipalities have enacted ordinances that establish a pay to stay affirmative legal defense in eviction actions for nonpayment of rent (“pay to stay defense”). Those ordinances (and municipalities) are: Akron OH Municipal Code tit.

15, ch. 150, § 150.5; Cincinnati Municipal Code tit. 8, ch. 871, § 871-9(c); Dayton Code of Ordinances tit. 9, ch. 93, §§ 93.71-.74; Codified Ordinances of Maple Heights
pt. 14, tit. 6, ch. 1488; Codified Ordinances of Newburgh Heights pt. 7, ch. 727; South Euclid OH Code of Ordinances pt. 14, ch. 1415; Toledo Municipal Code pt. 17, tit. 3,
ch. 1770; Yellow Springs OH Code of Ordinances pt. 8, tit. 2, ch. 868.392 The basic elements of the pay to stay defense under these ordinances are: (1) the tenant or a third-party has tendered all past due rent and reasonable late payment fees before the filing of the eviction action and the landlord has refused to accept it; or (2) the tenant or a third party has tendered all past due rent, reasonable late payment fees, and court costs prior to the entry of the eviction judgment and the landlord has refused to accept it.

One significant difference among these ordinances is whether the pay to stay defense is subject to the court’s discretion in each case. The ordinances under which the defense is subject to the court’s discretion are the Akron , Cincinnati, and Dayton 392 In addition, at least three Ohio municipalities enacted emergency o rdinances for the Covid- 19 pandemic that establish, until their respective sunset dates, the pay to stay defense. Those ordinances (and municipalities) are: Cleveland Heights Council Ordinance 79 -2021 (expires
Dec. 31, 2022); Euclid Code of Ordinances ch. 1765 (expires Dec. 31, 2022); Lakewood City Council Ordinance 12-2021 (expires Dec. 31, 2022).

The following provisions, per city, illustrate other variations among these ordinances.

Akron Late payment fees are limited to the larger of twenty -five dollars or eight percent of the monthly rent amount. A tenant may assert the pay to stay defense only one time per calendar year.

Cincinnati The tenant may meet the tender requirements with the tender of a voucher issued by a social service, nonprofit, or governmental agency that guarantees the payment of these amounts to the landlord. When the tenant invokes the pay to stay defense based on a tender of funds during the eviction hearing, the amount of that tender must also include reasonable attorney’s fees (not to exceed $125).

Dayton The tenant may place the tender of past due rent, late payment fees, and court costs in escrow with the court. Under the pay to stay defense, the tenant cannot be liable for the landlord’s attorney’s fees. A tenant may assert the pay to stay defense only one time per calendar year.

Maple Heights, Newburgh Heights, South Euclid, and Yellow Springs If a tenant tenders all past due rent, reasonable late payment fees, court courts, and the cost of the writ of restitution before the writ of restitutionis executed (and after the entry of the eviction judgment), the landlord is required to accept the tender of these amounts and either dismiss the eviction action ornotify the court to vacate the eviction judgment and dismiss the eviction action.

Toledo Late payment fees are limited to the larger of twenty-five dollars or five percent of the monthly rent amount.

These ordinances codify, as an affirmative defense, a longstanding equity defense in eviction actions for nonpayment of rent. For a discussion of that equity defense, see Section II.EE, above.

Court decisions ruling in favor of the tenant based on the pay to stay defense include: Century Pacific Hous. P’ship v. Jeffries , No. 21 -CVG-4124 (Ohio Mun., Dayton, Oct.19, 2021) (tenant tendered the r ent and late fees for two months, during the second month, and landlord refused it); Buckeye Northwest Realty for Tahoe Blue, LLC v. Stigall, No. CVG-21-05045 (Ohio Mun., Toledo, Hous. Div., June 22, 2021) (tenant tendered the requisite amount during the eviction hearing); Smith v. Cade, No.

21-CVG-2160 (Ohio Mun., Akron, May 12, 2021) (tenant made the requisite tender via a rental assistance program and landlord refused it).
