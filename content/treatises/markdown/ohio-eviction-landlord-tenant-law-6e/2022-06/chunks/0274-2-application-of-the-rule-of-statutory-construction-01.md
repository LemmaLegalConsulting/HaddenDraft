for the Use of Identical Words in Same Statute A well-established general rule of statutory construction is that identical words used in different parts of the same statute are presumed to have the same meaning (“the general presumption”). Nijhawan v. Holder , 557 U. S. 29, 38 -39 (200 9); Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith v. Dabit, 547 U.S. 71, 86 (2006);Henry v. Trs. of Perry Twp., 48 Ohio St. 671, 30 N.E.2d 1122 (1891).

The general presumption yields only when there is “such variation in the connection in which the words are used as reasonably to warrant the conclusion that they were employed in different parts of the act with different intent.” Envtl. Defense
v. Duke Energy Corp., 549 U.S. 561, 574 (2007). Accord Gen. Dynamics Land Sys.,
Inc. v. Cline, 540 U.S. 581, 595 (2004). See also Henry v. Trs. of Perry Twp., 48 Ohio
St. 671, 30 N.E. 1122 (1891).

In both R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) and R.C. 5321.01(C)(10), the legislature used the exact same phrase – “circumstances indicate a transient occupancy;” and the shared phrase is used for precisely the same purpose – to establish a short -term occupancy requirement for exclusions from the definition of residential premises.

Given the common purpose of the shared “transient occupancy” phrase, the general presumption should apply to its use in R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) and R.C. 5321.01(C)(10).

Higdon v. Sign of the Cross, 126 Ohio Misc.2d 84, 803 N.E.2d 876, ¶ 14 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., 2003). There is no reasonable basis for concluding that the legislature employed the shared “transient occup ancy” phrase with a different intent in these
subsections. Id.

Therefore, under the general presumption, the definition of the term “transient occupancy” should be the same for R.C. 5321.01(C)(10) purposes as it is for R.C.

5321.01(C)(3) purposes. Id.

The decision inParker v. Salvation Army, 971 N.E.2d 995, 2012-Ohio-2069 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., 2012), erroneously rejects the foregoing analysis and conclusion. It recognizes the general presumption, but erroneously determines that the exception to the general presumption applies.

The Parker court presents two grounds for its determination, each of which is without merit. The first ground is that, in determining the meaning of transient occupancy for R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) purposes, courts have relied oncode sections related to certain hotel taxes and those code sections are not relevant to R.C. 5321.01(C)(10).

The first ground is without merit because the guidance of these other code sections, relative to the legislature’s intent in using the phrase “transient occupancy,” does not indicate in any fashion that the legislature intended the phrase to have a different meaning in R.C. 5321.01(C)(10) than in R.C. 5321.01(C)(3). The second ground is that the Higdon decision’s ruling on this issue is not appli cable to the Parker case because of differences between the Higdon program and the Parker program. This second ground is without merit because these program differences are irrelevant to the determination of the legislature’s intent in using (and hence the meaning of) the term “transient occupancy,” which is a length of time irrespective of the program and is a standard to be applied to the program.
