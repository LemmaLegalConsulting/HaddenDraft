# d. Actual Damages, Absent Late Payment Fee

## Source text

In the absence of a valid late payment fee, a landlord is still entitled to recover “the monetary equivalent of the actual damages” that the landlord incurred as a result of the tenant’s breach of the lease. Berlinger v. Suburban Apt . Mgmt. Co., 7 Ohio App.3d 122, 125, 454 N.E.2d 1367 (8th Dist. 1982).

Therefore, in such cases, a court may find that the landlord’s loss of interest on the unpaid rent constitutes the monetary equivalent of the landlord’s actual damages, and award a judgment for that amount. E.g., Schwenkel v. Daley, No. 92-CVG-2825 (Ohio Mun., Lakewood, Jan. 19, 1993) (held that the late payment fee provision in the lease was a penalty; awarded the landlord the actual damages that the tenant caused in failing to pay rent timely; held that the actual damages were lost interest, and then computed the interest at the R.C. 1343.03(A) rate of 10% per annum).

Similarly, in Allman v. Thorne, No. 2006-CVI-000629 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty.,
Aug. 10, 2006), the court found that “[l]ate fees are designed to compensate landlords for loss of use of the rent that they were due for whatever period they were deprived of it.” It then concluded that “the recovery of the full amount [of rent] due, plus interest from the date it was due, will adequately accomplish those ends. The court does not, therefore, separately award a judgment for late fees.”
