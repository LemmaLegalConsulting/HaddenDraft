## Source text

The Sixth Circuit has held, in two cases, that the tenants failed to demonstrate a due process property interest for these terminations: Golden v. City of Columbus, 404 F.3d 950 (6th Cir. 2005), and Midkiff v. Adams County Reg’l Water Dist. , 409 F.3d 758 (6th Cir. 2005).

In Golden, the tenant’s due process property interest argument was “the bare assertion that because water is ‘an absolute necessity of life,’ the City’s termination of her service was unconstitutional.” 404 F.3d at 958. Because the ten ant did not rely upon any provision of the Ohio landlord-tenant law, the court opted not to express any opinion on whether that law creates a due process property interest for these
terminations. Id.

In Midkiff, the tenant’s due process property interestargument was based on R.C.

5321.04(A)(6) and 5321.15. The court rejected this argument on the ground that neither of these sections provides tenants with a right to continued water service. The court’s analysis of R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) is correct. The court’s analysis of R.C. 5321.15 is incorrect because, for the reasons discussed in Section XII.B.3, above, a tenant may secure injunctive relief to maintain or restore water service, in response to a landlord’s violation of R.C. 5321.15. More importantly, these analyses are irrelevant because the tenant’s due process property interest, for these terminations, does not arise from R.C.

5321.04(A)(6) or R.C. 5321.15.

In Ohio, the tenant’s due process property interest for these terminations arises from R.C. 5321.04(A)(2) and, in many cases, R.C. 5321.04(A)(1) as well. The R.C.

5321.04(A)(2) requirement “to put and keep the premises in a fit and habitable condition” necessarily includes a requirement to furnish water service, if the rental agreement requires the landlord to pay for the water service. Absent the water service, the premises are not habitable. Similarly, where a local code requires water service for rental premises, and where the rental agreement requires the landlord to pay for the water service, the R.C. 5321.04(A)(1) requirement “to comply with the requirements of all applicable building, housing, health, and safety codes that materially affect health and safety” necessarily includes a requirement to furnish water service.

If the due process pro perty interest depends on whether a tenant may obtain injunctive relief to enforce these R.C. 5321.04(A)(1),(2) rights to continued water service, as Midkiff suggests, R.C. 5321.07(B)(2) expressly provides tenants with that injunctive remedy. R.C. 3767.41 likewise provides tenants with an injunctive remedy if their rental premises are “no longer fit and habitable” (e.g., without water service).

See Section IX.G.1, above.

For further discussion on these constitutional rights, see National Consumer Law Center, Access to Utility Service (5th ed. 2011).
