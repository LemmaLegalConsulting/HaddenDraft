# 11. Pending Eviction Action, Rent Payments

## Source text

During an eviction action, a court may order the tenant to deposit rent with the court for a variety of reasons, including: a continuance (see Section I.K, above), a delay resulting from a jury demand (see Section I.J.3.c, above), an R.C. 1923.061(B) defense (see Section II.O.2, above), a stay pending a ruling on objections (see Section I.N.4, above), a stay pending a ruling on a Civil Rule 60(B) motion (see Section I.P.2, above), or a stay pending an appeal (see Section I.Q.3, above).

While statutory or Civil Rule authority exists for a rent deposit order in many of these circumstances, the court also has inherent power to order a rent deposit during an eviction action. See Section I.J.3.c, above. Courts often use this inherent power to order the tenant to deposit with the court current rent and future rent, as it becomes due, when the length of the eviction proceeding will exceed one month.See PK Mgmt.

LLC v. Hayes , No. 2011 -CVG-008188 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., July 7, 2011).

Landlords often refuse to accept a tenant’s tender of rent immediately preceding the commencement of (and pending) an eviction action in order to avoid a waiver of the R.C. 1923.04 notice to vacate. See Section II.C, above.

When a landlord refuses a tenant’s tender of rent on the ground that an eviction 363 action is pending, the tenant is excused from tendering rent for the next month, provided the eviction action is still pending and the court has not issued an order for the payment or deposit of rent. Cont’l Inv. P’ship v. Hukill, No. 06 -CV-131 (Ohio Mun., Delaware, Mar. 8, 2006). In these circumstances, the landlord may not evict the tenant for failing to tender the rent for the next month. Id.
