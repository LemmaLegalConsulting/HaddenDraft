In these circumstances, appellate courts have invoked the rule: “where the amount recovered is small compared to the attorney fees as sessed, the court must give adequate reasoning as to how it arrived at the specific amount of the award.” Dunn, 2004 WL 1445270, ¶ 10.

Any downward adjustment of the attorney fee award, based on the amount of the security deposit judgment, may not reduce the attorney fee award to an amount that is unreasonable and inconsistent with the purposes of such fees.Congdon v. Senters, No.

99-CA-43, 1999 WL 1071768 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Nov. 16, 1999).

The Congdon trial court awarded the tenant a judgment of $550 for the security deposit, $550 for R.C. 5321.16(C) statutory damages, and $600 for attorney’s fees.

With respect to the attorney fee award, it found that the attorney’s time (36.7 hours) and rate ($140 per hour) were reasonable but made a downward adjustment to take into account the amount of the security deposit judgment.

The Congdon appellate court reversed the trial court on the attorney fee award, on the ground that the amount was too low:

For the trial court to concede that the amount of time ex pended was reasonable in light of the case presented and then to award a fee tantamount to a charge of $16.35 per hour for trial counsel’s endeavor, is to in fact condone an abuse of the court system by litigants.
