# 5. R.C. 1923.15 Remedial Order

## Source text

R.C. 1923.15 authorizes the court, during an eviction action, to order an appropriate governmental agency ( e.g., local housing department, county gener al health district) to inspect the premises and determine whether they are in compliance with the requirements of R.C. 5321.04 or R.C. 4781.38 (former R.C. 3733.10). E.g., Superior Mobile Homes, Inc. v. Russell , No. 7 -89-CVG-336 (Ohio Mun., New Philadelphia, Aug. 18, 1989); Marino v. Smart, No. 7-88-CVG-510 (Ohio Mun., New Philadelphia, Dec. 13, 1988).

The court may order this inspection at any time during the eviction action. For example, in Marino, the court held that it had jurisdiction to order, and t hen ordered, an R.C. 1923.15 inspection at the same time that it granted a motion to dismiss the
complaint. The Marino court dismissed the eviction claim as moot because the tenant had vacated the premises; it dismissed the monetary claim because of the l andlord’s failure to amend the complaint to cure its noncompliance with Civil Rule 10(D).

If the governmental agency determines and the court finds that the premises are not in compliance with those requirements, R.C. 1923.15 authorizes the court to issue certain remedial orders in the eviction action. The court may issue these remedial orders even if the tenant has not filed a counterclaim. Saddoris v. Sterling, No. L-89- 062, 1989 WL 135301, at *3 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Nov. 9, 1989).

An R.C. 1923.15 rem edial order may forbid the landlord from re -renting the premises after the tenant has vacated, unless and until the R.C. 5321.04 or R.C. 4781.38 (former R.C. 3733.10) violations are corrected. E.g., Kerrigan v. Campbell, No. 21- CVH-01039 (Ohio Mun., Akron , May 18, 2021); Fisher v. Bly , No. 03 -CVG-19705 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Nov. 14, 2003);Wilburn v. Watson, No. 81-CVG- 64993 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Sept. 15, 1981); Irvin v. Carson, No. 79- CV-471 (Ohio Mun., Akron, Feb. 21, 1979).

101 This remedial order may be entered in cases in which the tenant already has vacated the premises and in cases in which, on the landlord’s eviction claim, the court enters a judgment for the landlord. R.C. 1923.15. See also Papadelis v. French, No. 89-CVG- 3932 (Ohio Mun., Canton, Sept. 11, 1989).

This remedial order also may be entered on a conditional basis in cases in which, on the landlord’s eviction claim, the court enters a judgment for the tenant. Saddoris
v. Sterling, No. L-89-062, 1989 WL135301, at *3 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Nov. 9, 1989).

In such cases, the remedial order becomes effective if the tenant decides to vacate the premises, notwithstanding the tenant’s right to remain in possession. Id.

When a court enters this remedial order , if the landlord re -rents the premises in violation of the order, the former tenant may file a contempt motion against the
landlord. E.g., Papadelis v. French, No. 89-CVG-3932 (Ohio Mun., Canton, Sept. 11, 1989).

An R.C. 1923.15 remedial order also may require the landlord to correct the R.C.

5321.04 or R.C.4781.38 (former R.C. 3733.10) violations.E.g., Kerrigan v. Campbell,
No. 21-CVH-01039 (Ohio Mun., Akron, May 18, 2021). This remedial order may be issued in cases in which, on the landlord’s eviction claim, the court enters a judgment for the tenant. R.C. 1923.15. E.g., Saddoris v. Sterling , No. L -89-062, 1989 WL 135301, at *3 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Nov. 9, 1989). When a court issues this remedial order, it also may appoint a housing specialist to monitor the landlord’s compliance with the order. Saddoris, 1989 WL 135301, at *3.

In conjunction with any R.C. 1923.15 remedial order, if the court finds that the tenant caused the R.C. 5321.04 or R.C. 4781.38 (former R.C. 3733.10) violations, “the court may award damages to the landlord equal to the reasonable cost of correcting such conditions.” R.C. 1923.15.
