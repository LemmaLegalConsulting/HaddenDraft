# f. Drug-Related Criminal Activity

## Source text

A category of criminal activity that is referenced in Sections III.G.8.a.(4) ,(5), above, is “drug-related criminal activity.” For these purposes, “Drug-related criminal activitymeans the illegal manufacture, sale, distribution, or use of a dru g, or the possession of a drug with intent to manufacture, sell, distribute or use the drug.” 24 C.F.R. § 5.100.

Four Ohio court decisions, in concluding that the tenant did not violate the prohibition against drug -related criminal activity, provide hel pful guidance on this prohibition.

One Ohio court held that, if an assisted housing tenant is convicted for possession of marijuana under a criminal law that does not explicitly include the element of intent to use, it is not “drug-related criminal activity,” which means that it is not grounds to terminate the tenancy. Lucas Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Correa, No. CVG-0501010 (Ohio Mun., Sylvania, Nov. 9, 2005).

A second Ohio court held that the tenant’s alleged conduct – overdosing on an unknown drug – is not criminal conduct and thus not drug -related criminal activity, and thereupon entered the eviction judgment for the tenant. Stark Metro. Hous. Auth.
v. Minocchi, No. 2021-CVG-3485 (Ohio Mun., Stark Cty., Aug. 26, 2021).

A third Ohio court, in a case in which it found that the juveniles had smoked marijuana during the course of the evening, held: “The Court believes that law requires proof by a preponderance of the evidence that drugs (marijuana) were located or used in the apartment or found on the juveniles’ persons.” PK Mgmt., LLC v. McGee, No.

2014-CVG-282, slip op. at 3 (Ohio County Ct., Jefferson Cty., Dist. No. 1, Jan. 27, 2015). Since the police did not find drugs in the apartment or on the juveniles, the court entered the eviction judgment for the tenant. Id., slip op. at 4.

A fourth Ohio court held that the PHA did not prove the requisite drug -related criminal activity where its only evidence wasthe testimony of a witness that “he could smell marijuana in the hallway and that he sniffed defendant’s doorway.”Stark Metro.
Hous. Auth. v. Sanders, No. 2015-CVG-2490, slip op. at 2 (Ohio Mun.,Massillon, Jan.

22, 2016).
