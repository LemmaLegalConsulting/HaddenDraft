# A. Type of Conduct Prohibited

## Source text

R.C. 5321.15(A) prohibits a landlord from r ecovering, or attempting to recover, possession of the premises by terminating the utilities or changing the locks. It also prohibits a landlord from engaging in “any [other] act ... against a tenant ... for the purpose of recovering possession of residen tial premises, other than as provided in Chapters 1923., 5303., and 5321. of the Revised Code.” These prohibitions apply even if the tenant’s right to possession has terminated.

R.C. 5321.15(A). They also apply even if the tenant has vacated the premises but continues to store personal property at the premises. See Stark v. Weimer, No. H-98- 031, 1999 WL 253451 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Apr. 30, 1999) (R.C. 5321.15(B) violation); Mounts v. Foerg , No. R90 -CVG-0375 (Ohio Mun., Portage Cty., Dec. 5,
1990) (same).

If the utility service termination is due to the landlord’s nonpayment, and if the landlord’s failure to make the payments is not for the purpose of recovering possession of the property, the landlord’s action is not a violation of R.C. 5321.15(A). Brown v.

Williams, No. 2012-CVG-17491, slip op. at 2 -3 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div.,
Mar. 11, 2014, and May 2, 2014). However, the termination of the utility service still constitutes a violation of R.C. 5321.04(A) and the rental agreement (id.), for which the landlord is liable (see Sections IX.A,B, above).

R.C. 5321.15(A) does not prohibit a landlord from recovering possession of the premises upon a tenant’s surrender of the premises. Burkholder v. Straughn, No. 97- T-0146, 1998 WL 553623 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., June 26, 1998). “In order to achieve a valid surrender, both the landlord and tenant must consent to the termination [of the rental agreement].” Id., 1998 WL 553623, at *3. “Such mutual consent distinguishes surrender from abandonment, in which the tenant acts unilaterally without the consent of the landlord.” Id.

“In the absence of an express written agreement, ... a surrender may ... be implied from the conduct of the parties.” Id. Accord Tecumseh Landing, LLC v. Bonetzky, 38 N.E.3d 1 149, 2015 -Ohio-2741, ¶¶ 27,28 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., July 6, 2015);

Pietrykowski v. Hamblin, No. WD-84-86, 1985 WL 7092, at *2 (Ohio App., 6th Dist.,
Apr. 12, 1985). “An example of this would be an oral agreement to surrender coupled with the actual physi cal delivery of the premises by the tenant to the landlord.” Burkholder, 1998 WL 553623, at *3.

R.C. 5321.15(A) also does not prohibit a landlord from recovering possession of the premises upon a tenant’s abandonment of the premises. “Abandoned property ...

is property over which the owner has relinquished all right, title, claim, and possession with the intention of not reclaiming it or resuming its ownership, possession or enjoyment.” Doughman v. Long, 42 Ohio App.3d 17, 21, 536 N.E.2d 394 (12th Dist.

1987). Accord McCain v. Brewer, No. 2014-CA-8, 2015 WL 302829, ¶ 17 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Jan. 23, 2015); Gordon v. Morris, No. 2000-CA-69, 2001 WL 85797, at *4 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 2, 2001). “In order to prove that property has been abandoned, it must be shown that the owner intended to abandon the property, and engaged in acts or omissions implementing that intent.” Gordon, 2001 WL 85797, at *4. Accord McCain, 2015 WL 302829, ¶ 17; Barnes v. Welborn, No. 04-6855, 2005 WL 5533987 (Ohio C.P., M ontgomery Cty., July 5, 2005). Cf. Hayes v. Hayes , No.

4892, 1985 WL 7948 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Apr. 11, 1985) (personal property).

Therefore, “[i]n order to establish an abandonment of a leasehold, actual acts of relinquishment accompanied by intention to abandon must be shown. The primary elements are the intention to abandon and the external act by which that intention is carried into effect.” Gavin L. Phillips, Annotation,What Constitutes Abandonment of Residential or Commercial Lease– Modern Cases, 84 A.L.R. 4th 183, 192 (1991). Cf.

Hamilton v. Harville , 63 Ohio App.3d 27, 30, 577 N.E.2d 1125 (12th Dist. 1989) (underground storage tank). “[O]ccasional, infrequent or even complete non-use is not sufficient in itself to establish the existence ofan ‘abandonment,’ absent other evidence tending to prove the intent to abandon.” Hamilton v. Harville, 63 Ohio App.3d 27, 30, 577 N.E.2d 1125 (12th Dist. 1989). Accord McCain v. Brewer, No. 2014-CA-8, 2015 WL 302829, ¶ 17 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Jan. 23, 2015).
