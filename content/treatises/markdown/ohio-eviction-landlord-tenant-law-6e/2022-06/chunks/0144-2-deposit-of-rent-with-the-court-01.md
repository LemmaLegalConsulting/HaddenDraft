# 2. Deposit of Rent with the Court

## Table-of-contents context

- II. Eviction Action, Common Defenses
  - O. Rent Abatement, R.C. 1923.061(B)

## Source text

A court may order a tenant, who asserts the R.C. 1923.061(B) defense, to deposit with the court all or part of the past due rent and, as it becomes due, the rent that becomes due during the pendency of the action. R.C. 1923.061(B).

The court’s authority to order these R.C. 1923.061(B) rent deposits is
discretionary. Bester v. Owens, No. 17388, 1999 WL 179261, at *2 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Mar. 31, 1999); Sandefur Mgmt. Co. v. Smith , 21 Ohio App.3d 145, 148, 486 N.E.2d 1234 (10th Dist. 1985);Smith v. Wright, 65 Ohio App.2d 101, 107, 416 N.E.2d 655 (8th Dist. 1979). The court may, but is not required, to order them. Wright, 65 Ohio App.2d at 107.

When a court orders a tenant to deposit rent with the court under R.C. 1923.061(B), if the tenant fails to deposit the rent, the tenant forfeits the right to assert the R.C.

1923.061(B) defense. Bester v. Owens , No. 17388, 1999 WL 179261, at *2 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Mar. 31, 1999); Conway v. Nissley, No. 68536, 1995 WL 723298, at *3 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Dec. 7, 1995) (implied term of R.C. 1923.061(B)). See also Napolski v. Champney , 295 Or. 408, 419 -20, 667 P.2d 1013 (1983) (construing a provision of the Oregon Landlord and Tenant Act that is nearly identical to R.C.

1923.061(B)). Cf. Deerwood Mgmt. Co. v. Bernsdorf, No. 78AP-470, 1978 Ohio App.

LEXIS 10021, at *4 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Nov. 28, 1978) (order to deposit rent issued as condition for granting Civil Rule 60(B) motion for relief from eviction judgment).

These tenants still may assert any other relevant defense in the eviction action.

Bester v. Owens, No. 17388, 1999 WL 179261, at *2 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Mar. 31, 1999). A nd these tenants still may maintain their counterclaims separate and apart from the landlord’s claim for possession. See Section I.I, above.

Several appellate districts have adopted the position that a tenant may maintain the R.C. 1923.061(B) defense onl y if the trial court, in the exercise of its discretionary authority, decides to order the tenant to deposit rent with the court.

Under one line of cases, the tenant’s right to maintain the R.C. 1923.061(B) defense exists only if the court orders the tenant to deposit with the court all of the past due rent. Martins Ferry Jaycee Hous., Inc. v. Pawlaczyk , 4 Ohio App.3d 302, 304, 448 N.E.2d 512 (7th Dist. 1982); Smith v. Wright , 65 Ohio App.2d 101, 109, 416 N.E.2d 655 (8th Dist. 1979). See also Shelton v. H uff, No. 2012 -T-0101, 2014 WL 1352663, ¶ 24 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Mar. 31, 2014) (2 -1 decision); Dayton Tool 151 Rental v. Ealy , No. 21849, 2007 WL 2897807 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Oct. 5, 2007) (absence of R.C. 5321.07 rent deposit).

Under a second line of cases, it appears that the tenant’s right to maintain the R.C.

1923.061(B) defense exists only if the court orders the tenant to deposit with the court all or part of the past due rent. Heritage Hills, Ltd. v. Bragg , No. 1379, 1987 WL 16079, at *1-2 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Aug. 27, 1987);Sandefur Mgmt. Co. v. Smith, 21 Ohio App.3d 145, 149, 486 N.E.2d 1234 (10th Dist. 1985).

The position of these appellate courts has no basis in R.C. 1923.061(B). No provision in R.C. 1923.061(B) can reasonably be construed to state that a tenant may assert the R.C. 1923.061(B) defense only if the court exercises its discretionary authority to order a rent deposit.

Furthermore, the position of these appellate districts is untenable. Its premise is that, in R.C. 1923.061(B), the legislature granted courts the discretionary authority to determine, on a case-by-case basis, whether a tenant may assert the R.C. 1923.061(B)
defense. Yet, none of these courts identifies any standard, let alone a rational one, pursuant to which the courts are to make this determination.

The position of the first line of cases is also untenable because it renders meaningless the R.C. 1923.061(B) provision that states that a court may order a tenant to deposit with the court “part of the past due rent. ”122 Under the first line of cases, whenever a court issues a rent deposit order pursuant to this provision, the tenant is barred from asserting the R.C. 1923.061(B) defense, regardless of whether the tenant complies with the order. If the tenant complies with the rent deposit order, the tenant is still barred from asserting the R.C. 1923.061(B) defense because the tenant has not deposited all of the past due rent.

The Oregon Supreme Court has addressed this issue and correctly adopted a position contrary to the position of these Ohio appellate districts. Napolski v.

Champney, 295 Or. 408, 667 P.2d 1013 (1983). The Oregon Supreme Court had an opportunity to address this issue because Or. Rev. Stat. § 91.810(1) is nearly identical to R.C. 1923.061(B); both were patterned after the Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act § 4.105. The Oregon Supreme Court recognized that tenants are permitted to assert the Or. Rev. Johnson, 553 S.W.3d 280 (Mo. 2018) ( en banc ) (under the implied warranty of habitability defense, courts exercise their discretion, on a case -by-case basis, in determining whether to require the tenant, as a condition for asserting the defense, to deposit future rent (in whole or in part) during the pendency of the action).123 If a tenant has sufficient funds to deposit with the court all past due rent, an order requiring the tenant to deposit all past due rent is immensely helpful to the tenant’s effort to establish an R.C. 1923.061(B) defense. If a tenant deposits with the court all past due rent, no rent will remain due after the court disburses the rent deposit, irrespective of the judgments on the rent claim and the counterclaim. If a tenant has insufficient funds to deposit with the court all past due rent, the tenant may request a rent deposit order requiring the tenant to deposit “part of the past due rent,” such as an amount that the court reasonably anticipates wi ll be due after application of R.C. 1923.061(B).124 The tenant can reasonably argue that a rent deposit of part of the past due rent is consistent with the purpose, as well as the express language, of the R.C. 1923.061(B) rent deposit provision.
