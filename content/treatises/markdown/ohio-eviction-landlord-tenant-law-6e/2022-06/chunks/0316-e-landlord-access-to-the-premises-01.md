# E. Landlord Access to the Premises

## Table-of-contents context

- VII. Rental Agreement Provisions

## Source text

Generally, a landlord may enter the premises for any of the purposes enumerated in R.C. 5321.05(B), and a tenant may not unreasonably withhold consent for the entry, provided the landlord gives the tenant reasonable advance notice of the entry and the landlord enters the premises at a reasonable time. R.C. 5321.04(A)(8), 5321.05(B).

E.g., T.K.D. Enters. v. Zimmermann, No. 97-CA-44, 1998 WL 380496 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., July 2, 1998) (unlawful entry, lack of notice); Ayres v. Simmons , No. 93 -CA- 827, 1993 WL 405489 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Sept. 30, 1993) (lawful entry).

The purposes for which a landlord may enter the premises under R.C. 5321.05(B) are: (1) to inspect the premises; (2) to make ordinary, necessary, or agreed repairs, decorations, alterations, or improvements to the premises; (3) to deliver parcels that are too large for the tenant’s mail facilities; (4) to supply necessary or agreed services;

and (5) to exhibit the premises to prospective or actual purchasers, mortgagees, tenants, workmen, or contractors.

Generally, twenty-four hours advance notice is presumed to be reasonable advance notice of the landlord’s entry, in the absence of evidence to the contrary. R.C.

5321.04(A)(8). See also T.K.D. Enters. v. Zimmermann , No. 97 -CA-44, 1998 WL 380496 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., July 2, 1998); Ayres v. Simmons, No. 93-CA-827, 1993 WL 405489 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Sept. 30, 1993); Spencer v. Blackmon , 22 Ohio Misc.2d 52, 490 N.E.2d 943 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., 1985).

The landlord’s obligation to “give the tenant reasonable advance notice of his intent to enter” is met only if the tenant receives “some sort of notice.” Limage v.

Citiscene Props., No. 92AP-190, 1992 WL 131840, at *2 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., June 9, 1992). For example, this notice require ment is not met if the landlord’s only attempts to provide the tenant with notice of the entry are an unanswered telephone call on the day of entry and an unanswered knock on the door of the dwelling unit at the time of entry. Limage v. Citiscene Props., No. 92AP-190, 1992 WL 131840 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., June 9, 1992).

However, a landlord’s entry to perform repairs does not violate this notice requirement, notwithstanding the absence of an advance notice, if the tenant voluntarily permits the landlord to enter and the landlord’s entry is in response to the tenant’s repair request. Wayne Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Jackson, Nos. 2369,2403, 1988 WL 107026 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Oct. 12, 1988).

The twenty-four-hour advance notice requirement does not apply “in the case of emergency or if it is impracticable to do so.” R.C. 5321. 04(A)(8). Generally, “[a]n emergency means an unforeseen occurrence or combination of circumstances which calls for immediate action leaving no time for deliberation.” Helvich v. George A.

Rutherford Co., 96 Ohio App. 367, 371, 114 N.E.2d 514 (8th Dist. 1953).

One court has construed the R.C. 5321.04(A)(8) emergency provision consistent with this Helvich definition. Spencer v. Blackmon, 22 Ohio Misc.2d 52, 490 N.E.2d 943 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., 1985). The Spencer decision rejected a landlord’s claim that the emergency provision authorized his attempted entry. The landlord attempted to enter the premises without advance notice in order to install smoke
detectors. The purported emergency was that an ordinance deadline for installing the smoke detectors had expired. But the landlord had received advance notice of the deadline.

Two courts have held that: (1) a tenant’s storage locker, by itself, does not meet the R.C. 5321.01(F) definition of dwelling unit; (2) therefore, the tenant’s storage locker is not part of the tenant’s dwelling unit under R.C. 5321.01(F) ; and (3) as a result, a landlord’s unreasonable entry into the tenant’s storage locker does not violate R.C. 5321.04(A)(7) or R.C. 5321.04(A)(8). Gibson v. Shephard, 87 N.E.3d 846, 2017- Ohio-1157, ¶ 40 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., 2017);Smith v. CBert Props., LLC, No. 28058, 2019 WL 102081 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Jan. 4, 2019) (follows Gibson).

A better-reasoned position for this issue is that the tenant’s storage locker is one component of (and thus part of) the tenant’s dwelling unit under R.C. 5321.01(F) and, therefore, a landlord’s entry into the tenant’s storage locker is governed by R.C.

5321.04(A)(7) and R.C. 5321.04(A)(8). The components of “residential premises” 345 under R.C. 5321.01(C) fall i nto two general categories – those solely for the use and occupancy of the tenant and those for the use of all tenants ( i.e., common areas and common facilities). The main purpose for restricting a landlord’s entry into a tenant’s dwelling unit – protection of the tenant’s privacy interests – is best advanced if the term dwelling unit includes all properties and facilities to which the tenant has sole use and occupancy (and excludes all common areas and common facilities). Under this construction, the term dwelling unit includes a tenant’s storage locker (which is akin to an attic or a closet) because the tenant has sole use and occupancy of that area.

Irrespective of whether the purpose and the advance notice of the entry meet the requirements of R.C. 5321.05(B) and R.C. 5321.04(A)(8), a landlord may not enter the premises “in an unreasonable manner” and a landlord may not make “repeated demands for entry that are otherwise lawful but have the effect of harassing the tenant.” R.C. 5321.04(B).

Also, a landlord may ‘[n]ot abuse the right of access conferred by division (B) of section 5321.05 of the Revised Code.” R.C. 5321.04(A)(7).

These R.C. 5321.04(A)(7),(8), R.C. 5321.04(B), and R.C. 5321.05(B) rights and duties, on landlord access to the premises, c annot be waived or modified by a rental
agreement. R.C. 5321.06, 5321.13(A). See also T.K.D. Enters. v. Zimmermann, No.

97-CA-44, 1998 WL 380496, at *3 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., July 2, 1998).

Notwithstanding these rights and duties, neither R.C. Chapter 53 21 nor common law requires a tenant, who has installed a lock on an exterior door of the tenant’s dwelling unit, to provide the landlord with a key to the lock. Spencer v. Blackmon, 22 Ohio Misc.2d 52, 490 N.E.2d 943 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., 1985). How ever, a lease provision may require the tenant to do so and such provisions are valid and enforceable.
