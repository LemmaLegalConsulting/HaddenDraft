The exclusive use requirement reflects the common law on lease agreements for real property. Cuvier Press Club v. Fourth & Race St. Assocs., Ltd. , 1 Ohio App.3d 30, 34, 439 N.E.2d 443 (1st Dist. 1981). The indispensable elements of such agreements are the rights to exclusive possession of a certain quantity of land for a term certain. Id.

A roommate’s occupancy status generally falls under one of three categories. First, a roommate may be a co -tenant, based on a rental agreement among the tenant, the roommate, and the landlord. Second, a roommate may be a subtenant of the tenant, based on a rental agreement between the tenant and the subtenant for the roommate’s exclusive use of a bedroom (and any additional specified portion) of the residential

When a roommate is a co-tenant, the exclusive use requirement refers to a tenant’s rights in relation to the public, not in relation to the roommate. See Torbeck v.

Chamberlain, 138 Or. App. 446, 452, 910 P.2d 389 (1996).

Whether a live-in caretaker meets the definition of tenant depends on the particular
facts. Lee v. Wallace, 186 Ohio App.3d 18, 926 N.E.2d 328 (8th Dist. 2010). In Lee, the live-in caretaker, who occupied a room at the residential premises, was not a tenant because she: (1) did not pay rent (in money or services); (2) did not execute a rental agreement; (3) did not have exclusive use of the room; (4) did not identify the room’s address as her residence on any account or license; and (5) maintained another residential room elsewhere.
