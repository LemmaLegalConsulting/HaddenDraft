# 3. Transient Occupancy Means up to Thirty Days

## Source text

Since R.C. Chapter 5321 does not define or establish criter ia for determining the meaning of the term transient occupancy in R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) or R.C.

5321.01(C)(10), courts are required to consider other relevant factors for that determination.

Five relevant factors, which are summarized below, support the con clusion that, for both R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) and 5321.01(C)(10), the term transient occupancy means up to one month.

Whether any of these five factors relates more directly to R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) than to R.C. 5321.01(C)(10) is immaterial. The general presum ption of statutory construction, relative to the legislature’s use of identical words in different parts of the same statute (“the general presumption”), applies to the term transient occupancy in R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) and R.C. 5321.01(C)(10). See Section VI.F.2, above. The general presumption compels the conclusion that, if the term transient occupancy in R.C.

5321.01(C)(3) means an occupancy up to thirty days, the term transient occupancy in R.C. 5321.01(C)(10) also means an occupancy up to thirty days. Higdon v. Sign of the Cross, 126 Ohio Misc.2d 84, 803 N.E.2d 876, ¶ 14 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., 2003).

The first factor is, with respect to the hotels and motels to which the R.C.

5321.01(C)(3) exclusion applies, the “common understanding of the length of occupancy in such facilities is that it is measured in days or weeks.”Higdon v. Sign of the Cross, 126 Ohio Misc.2d 84, 803 N.E.2d 876, ¶ 14 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., 2003). Accord Alter v. Tucker Lake of the Woods of Farmersville, Inc. , No. 04-CV- 00259, 2004 WL 5255401 (Ohio C.P., Montgomery Cty., Oct. 8, 2004).

The second factor is two Ohio hotel tax provisions that are consistent with this common understanding. One is the R.C. 5739.01(N) sales tax definition of transient guests: “persons occupying a room or rooms for sleeping accommodations for less than thirty consecutive days.”260 The other is the R.C. 3731.01(A)(2) definition of transient hotel: certain hotels and motels that “are offered for pay to transient guests for a period of thirty days or less.” Since the R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) exclusion applies to transient occupancies in ahotel, 260 The R.C. 5739.01(N) definition of transient guest is used in connection w ith the sales tax that is levied on all transactions in which lodging by a hotel is furnished to transient guests.

it is reasonable to infer that the legislature intended the R.C. 5321.01(C)(3) definition of transient occupancy to be consistent with the R.C. 5739.01(N) definition of transient guests in a hotel and the R.C. 3731.01(A)(2) definition of a transient hotel. Therefore, an occupancy of a dwelling unit for more than thirty consecutive days is a significant factor for finding a non -transient occupancy under R.C. 5321.01(C)(3). But cf.

Franklinton Coalition v. Open -Shelter, Inc., 13 Ohio App.3d 399, 469 N.E.2d 861 (10th Dist. 1983).

The third factor is the length of a typical residential rental agreement. R.C.

5321.01(C)(3) and R.C. 5321.01(C)(10) rely on the transient nature of the occupancy to exclude it from the definition of residential premises and thus residential rental
agreements. Therefore, it is reasonable to construe the term transient occupancy, in these statutory provisions, as a time period significantly less than the length of a typical residential rental agreement.

The length of a typica l residential rental agreement is one year or less. David Genesove, The Nominal Rigidity of Apartment Rents, 85 Rev. Econ. & Statistics 844, 849 (Nov. 2003) (length of residential leases in the United States: 15.4% – no lease/ month- to-month; 40% – less than one year; 43% – one year; 2.3% – more than one year).

The fourth factor, which courts in other jurisdictions have relied on when construing transient occupancy provisions in their residential landlord tenant law, is the definition of transient guests in 42 U.S.C. § 2000a(b)(1). E.g., Lyons v. Kamhoot, 281 Or. 615, 620, 575 P.2d 1389 (1978) (transient occupancy where one -month stay and then returning to home, after fire repair); City of Evanston v. O’Leary , 244 Ill.

App.3d 190, 194 -96, 614 N.E.2d 114 (1993) (not transient occupancy where three - month stay and resident without alternative housing). Lyons and City of Evanston consider the resident’s intent for relatively permanent housing, based on the length of the stay and whether the resident will be returning to an existing home.
