May Be Enforced When a court enters a judgment for the landlord in an eviction action, the persons against whom the judgment may be enforced are, in some circumstances, limited to the persons who have been named as defendants in the eviction complaint.

For example, if a subtenant resides in th e premises and the landlord brings an eviction action against the tenant and fails to name the subtenant as a party defendant, generally the judgment cannot be enforced against the subtenant.81 Hooper v. Seventh Urban, Inc., 70 Ohio App.2d 101, 434 N.E.2d 1367 (8th Dist. 1980).

However, one municipal court has held that, in these circumstances, the judgment may be enforced against the subtenant if: (1) the eviction complaint names as defendants the tenant “and all other occupants,” (2) the eviction complaint is served by residential service in accordance with Civil Rule 4.1, (3) the landlord has no knowledge of the subtenant’s occupancy or that anyone other than the tenant is occupying the premises, and (4) the landlord took all reasonable steps available to prohibit and prevent the subtenancy, e.g., inclusion of a provision in the lease that prohibited subleasing without the landlord’s prior written consent. Mar-Lis Props. v. Ratliff, No.

M8502-CVG-04841 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., May 2, 1985).

If a husb and and wife reside in the premises and one of them is not named as a defendant in the eviction complaint, whether the eviction judgment may be enforced against the non-party spouse depends on whether the non-party spouse is a tenant under the rental agreement. If the non-party spouse is not a tenant under the rental agreement, the eviction judgment may be enforced against the non-party spouse. Studer v. Roark,
No. 94-CA-38, 1995 WL 347963 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Mar. 21, 1995); Dondorfer v.

Wolf, No. 13 -010, 1988 WL 105568 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Oct. 7, 1988). But cf.

Arrieta v. Mahon , 31 Cal.3d 381, 182 Cal. Rptr. 770 (1982) (due process bars the eviction of adults who are not named in the writ of restitution, unless they are afforded certain post-judgment notice and hearing opportunities). If the non -party spouse is a tenant under the rental agreement, the eviction judgment may not be enforced against the non-party spouse. Milano v. Jones, No. M8906-CVG-20458 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., July 10, 1989).

Based on Studer and Dondorfer, it appears that the eviction judgment may also be enforced against children of the tenant who are not named as defendants in the eviction
complaint. See also New York County Dist. Attorney’s Office v. Oquendo, 147 Misc.2d 125, 553 N.Y.S.2d 973 (Civ. Ct. 1990). The children, like the non -party spouse in Studer and Dondorfer, are not tenants under the rental agreement. The children of the tenant reside in the premises only “as an incident to the family relationship.” Id. at 81 When an owner maintains an eviction action against a subtenant in possession of the premises, the tenant who is not in possession is not an indispensable party to the action.

Baughman v. Semaan, 29 Ohio App.3d 365, 505 N.E.2d 643 (9th Dist. 1986). T he reason for this conclusion is that the eviction action relates solely to the right of present possession. Id.

97 127.

If a bailiff attempts to enforce a writ of restitution against a non -party occupant who is not subject to the eviction judgment, the non -party occupant may need to intervene and file a counterclaim in the eviction action, or file a new complaint, seeking injunctive relief against the enforcement. The non -party occupant may not obtain a writ of prohibition to prevent the improper enforcement of the writ of restitution. See Section I.O.2, above. If the writ of restitution is enforced improperly again st a non- party occupant, the non-party occupant may have a damage claim against the landlord, but not against the bailiffs. See Castle Apts., Inc. v. Allgood, 43 Ohio App.3d 163, 539 N.E.2d 1154 (9th Dist. 1988).
