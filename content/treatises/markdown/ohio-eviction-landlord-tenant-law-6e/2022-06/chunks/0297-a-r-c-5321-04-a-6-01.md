# a. R.C. 5321.04(A)(6)

## Source text

Subject to two exceptions, R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) requires the landlord to supply the dwelling unit with running water, reasonable amounts of hot water, and reasonable heat at all times.

The first exception is that the R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) obligation does not apply if the building that includes the dwelling unit is not required by law to be equipped with those services.

The second exception is that the R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) obligation to supply hot water and heat does not apply if the utility meter, for the utility that furnishes hot water or heat to the dwelling unit, is an individual meter that serves and measures consumption at only the tenant’s dwelling unit. The R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) obligation to supply running water is not subject to this second exception.

R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) does not require a landlord to prevent an interruption (or
reduction) of the flow of water from a municipal water system to the premises, unless it is the result of a defective condition at the premises that the landlord is obligated to
remedy. Stewart v. Kalman, No. 1109, 1982 WL 3509 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Aug. 13, 1982).

Likewise, R.C. 5321.04 does not require a landlord to prevent a storm sewer overflow or a sanitary sewer back -up at th e premises, unless it is the result of a defective condition at the premises that the landlord is obligated to remedy. Conway
v. Liddell, No. 79799, 2002 WL 398687 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Mar. 14, 2002);Paul v.

Semirale, No. 70438, 1996 WL 502143 (Ohio Ap p., 8th Dist., Sept. 5, 1996). “It is settled law that municipalities have the duty to maintain their public sewer systems.” Paul, 1996 WL 502143, at *1.

An issue on which the case law is split is whether the R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) obligation to supply running water, hot water, and heat requires the landlord to pay for the utility services that furnish the running water, hot water, and heat.

The initial appellate court to address this issue concluded that the R.C.

5321.04(A)(6) obligation requires the landlord to pay for the water service. Griffin v.

Holston, No. L-83-261, 1983 WL 2328, at *1 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Dec. 16, 1983) (“the term ‘supply,’ as used in R.C. 5321.04(A)(6), means to pay for the utility charges”). Cf. Woodrow v. McGuire , No. 1740, 198 9 WL 72799, at *3 (Ohio App., 329 4th Dist., June 27, 1989) (“even if appellee [landlord] did not agree to that term [landlord to pay for the water service], R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) specifically requires the landlord to ‘supply running water ...’” and thus, in failing to secure the reinstatement of the water service, “appellee did fail in his duty to provide water”).

Thereafter, two appellate courts and and at least one municipal court have concluded that the R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) obligation does not require the landlord to pay for the water service. Jenkins v. Roger C. Perry & Co., 83 Ohio App.3d 234, 236, 614 N.E.2d 850 (10th Dist. 1992) (“the word ‘supply’ in R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) means to provide water or make it available in the physical sense and without the added burden of paying for such service”); Hodgson v. Hodgson , No. 89 -L-14-074, 1990 WL 128267, at *2 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Sept. 7, 1990) (“Subsection (A)(6) does not require payment for the water by the landlord”); Bess v. McBride, No. 96-CVH-1586, slip op. at 3 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., May 6, 1997) (“The Court notes that there is nothing in the Landlord -Tenant Act which prohibits a landlord and tenant, as between themselves, from entering into a contractual arrangement such that the tenant agrees to pay for the water”).273 The Jenkins, Hodgson, and Bess conclusion that the R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) obligation only requires a landlord to make the water available in a physical sense causes the second exception in R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) to be nonsensical. Their conclusion means that, when the dwelling unit’s relevant utility meter is an individual meter (which triggers the second exception), the R.C. 5321.04(A)(6) obligation does not apply and thus the landlord is not required to make hot wate r and heat available in a physical
sense. None of these court decisions discusses the meaning of the second exception under its conclusion.
