## Source text

“[T]he [FED] statute … does not on its face limit the class of defendants to ‘tenants’and the class of plaintiffs to ‘landlords’….” Bio Energy (Ohio), LLC v.

Phoenix Golf Links, Ltd. , No. 12AP-171, 2012 WL 4470638, ¶ 11 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Sept. 27, 2012).

“Applying basic principles of statutory construction, there is no restrictionin R.C.

1923.01(A) upon who may bring an FED action, while under R.C. 1923.02 there is a defined class of defendants against whom it may be brought.”Id., ¶ 13. Accord CS/RW Westlake Indoor Storage, L.L.C. v. Kesi, L.L.C., No. 102535, 2015 WL 6819281, ¶ 15 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Nov. 5, 2015).

A tenant may bring an FED action against “a landlord who is otherwise one of the permissible defendants defined in R.C. 1923.02(A),” against a competing co -tenant, against a squatter, or against others. Bio Energy, 2012 WL 4470638, ¶ 15.

A property owner may bring an FED action against any non-tenant occupier of the property who is “without color of title.” R.C. 1923.02(A)(5). E.g., Hardin v. Hardin,
No. E-18-001, 2019 WL 259092 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Jan. 1 8, 2019) (occupancy by 3 permission of the owner);Deutsche Bank Nat’l Trust Co. v. Lovette, No. 106546, 2018 WL 6266863 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Nov. 29, 2018) (tenancy at sufferance extinguished by judicial foreclosure sale); White v. Bergman, No. 15-COA-010, 2015 WL 5783088 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Oct. 2, 2015) (guest); Lee v. Wallace, 186 Ohio App.3d 18, 926 N.E.2d 328 (8th Dist. 2010) (live -in caregiver, without rent or exclusive use);

Executive Bus. Ctrs., Inc. v. TransPacific Mfg., Ltd., No. L-08-1060, 2009 WL 282383 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Feb. 6, 2009) (office service agreement); Estate of Wos v. Wos,
No. L-05-1408, 2006 WL 2383966 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Aug. 18, 2006) (temporary occupancy, with decedent’s permission but without rent).

However, the property owner in these circumstances is not required to use an FED action to recover possession of the property. Lee v. Wallace, 186 Ohio App.3d 18, 926 N.E.2d 328 (Cuyahoga Cty. 2010). Cf. Northfield Park Assocs. v. Northeast Ohio Harness, 36 Ohio App.3d 14, 521 N.E.2d 466 (8th Dist. 1987) (commercial tenancy).

In addition, by express statutory authority: a unit owners association may bring an FED action against a tenant of a unit owner, for a violation of the deed restrictions or condominium rul es, R.C. 5311.19(B)(1); 1 and, with respect to land installment contracts, the vendor may bring an FED action against the vendee in certain circumstances (R.C. 5313.07,.08), which are discussed in Section XXIII.E.2, below.

Also, R.C. 1923.02(A)(6) permits an FED action “[i]n any o ther case of the unlawful and forcible detention of lands or tenements.” One court has held that R.C. 1923.02(A)(6) is not authority for a long -term acute care hospital to bring an FED action against a patient who is ready for discharge.

Regency Hosp. of Columbus v. Thomas, No. 2016-CVG-004483 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., Mar. 8, 2016).

In any FED action, a court may, pursuant to the plaintiff’s request, enter an order evicting one (or more) but not all of the occupiers of the premises. White v. Bergman,
No. 15-COA-010, 2015 WL 5783088 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Oct. 2, 2015). See also Sanders v. Favors, No. C-950304, 1995 WL 763681 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Dec. 29, 1995). In Bergman, the owner evicted his daughter’s guest but not his daughter.

1 In these FED actions, the unit owners association must bring the action in the name of the unit owner, and as the unit owner’s agent. Homeowners Ass’n at Arrow-Head Bay v. Fidoe , No.

12-MA-136, 2014 WL 1384577 (Ohio App., 7th Dist., Mar. 31, 2014).
