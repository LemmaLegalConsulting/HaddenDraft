# 6. Payment of Deficiency

## Source text

R.C. 1923.061(B) provides that, “[i]f no rent remains due after application of this division, judgment shall be entered for the tenant in the action for possession.” Clearly, this provision requires an eviction judgment in favor of the tenant if the sum of the counterclaim judgment (in favor of the tenant) and the R.C. 1923.061(B) rent deposit equals or exceeds the amount of the rent claim judgment (in favor of the landlord). E.g., Heritage Hills, Ltd. v. Bragg, No. 1379, 1987 WL 16079 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Aug. 27, 1987); Sandefur Mgmt. Co. v. Smith , 21 Ohio App.3d 145, 486 N.E.2d 1234 (10th Dist. 1985). See also Napolski v. Champney, 295 Or. 408, 417, 667 P.2d 1013 (1983) (construing a provision of the Oregon Landlord and Tenant Act that is nearly identical to R.C. 1923.061(B)). But see Lee v. Douglas, No. 80-CA29, 1981 WL 6083 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Nov. 24, 1981); Rains v. Temple , No. 88-CVG-74 (Ohio Mun., Hillsboro, May 22, 1988).

Less clear is whether there are any circumstances in which R.C. 1923.061(B) requires an eviction judgment in favor of the tenant even though rent remains due after application of R.C. 1923.061(B). One such circumstance may be where: (1) the court orders the tenant to deposit with the court part of the past due rent, (2) the tenant deposits that amount with the court, (3) the court enters a judgment in favor of the tenant on the counterclaim, (4) the court enters a judgment in favor of the landlord on the rent claim, (5) the sum of the counterclaim judgment and the rent deposit is slightly less than the amount of the rent claim judgment, and (6) the tenant then tenders the net rent due the landlord.

Both the implied warranty of habitability case law 131 and equity support the position that, in this circumstance, the tenant has a right to tender the net rent due the landlord and thereby obtain an eviction judgment in favor of the tenant.

Under the implied warranty of habitability case law, when a breach of the warranty results in a partial reduction in rent, the tenant has the right to maintain possession of the premises by tendering the amount of rent that the court determines is due and
owing. E.g., Pugh v. Holmes, 486 Pa. 272, 292, 405 A.2d 897 (1979);Teller v. McCoy, 162 W. Va. 367, 392, 253 S.E.2d 114 (1978);Green v. Superior Court of City & County of San Francisco , 10 Cal.3d 616, 639, 517 P.2d 1168 (1974) (en banc); Curtis v.

Surrette, 49 Mass. App. Ct . 99, 100, 726 N.E.2d 967 (2000); Javins v. First Nat’l Realty Corp., 428 F.2d 1071, 1083 (D.C. Cir. 1970); Acad. Spires, Inc. v. Brown, 111 N.J. Super. 477, 488, 268 A.2d 556 (1970).

Likewise, equity requires that tenants in this circumstance be given anopportunity to tender the net rent due the landlord and thereby obtain an eviction judgment in favor of the tenant. It would surely be an inequitable result if a tenant were evicted, for example, because the sum of the tenant’s counterclaim judgment and rent deposit was one dollar or ten dollars less than the amount of the landlord’s rent claim judgment.

131 The implied warranty of habitability case law is relevant because R.C. 1923.061(B) is essentially a codification of that case law. See Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act § 2.104, Comment.
