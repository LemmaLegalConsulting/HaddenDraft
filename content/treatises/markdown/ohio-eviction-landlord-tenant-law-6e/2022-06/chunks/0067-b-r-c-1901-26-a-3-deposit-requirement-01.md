# b. R.C. 1901.26(A)(3) Deposit Requirement

## Source text

R.C. 1901.26(A)(3) provides that the rules of a municipal court may require a party who demands a trial by jury in a civil action “to make an advance deposit as fixed by rule of court, unless, upon affidavit or other evidence, the court concludes that the party is unable to make the required deposit.” It also provides that the fees of a jury shall be taxed as costs.

Because Article I, Section 5 of the Ohio Constitution establishes the right to a trial by jury in an eviction action (see Section I.J.l, above), and because this constitutional right may be subje ct only to moderate and reasonable regulation ( see Walters v.

Griffith, 38 Ohio St.2d 132, 311 N.E.2d 14 (1974)), the amount of any R.C.

1901.26(A)(3) advance deposit requirement may not exceed the amount that constitutes moderate and reasonable regulationof this constitutional right. See Walters
v. Griffith, 38 Ohio St.2d 132, 311 N.E.2d 14 (1974); Hocking Metro. Hous. Auth. v.

Martin, No. 97 -CA-9, 1998 WL 100416 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Mar. 5, 1998);

Cuyahoga River Assocs. Ltd. P’ship v. MJK Corp., No. 68673, 1996 WL 17292 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Jan. 18, 1996); Kelly v. Abu-Aun, No. 64869, 1994 WL 86215 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Mar. 17, 1994).

The case law provides some guidance on the advance deposit amounts that meet this moderate and reasonable regulation standard. The Ohio Supreme Court has held that “requiring the cost of summoning jurors and the fees of jurors to be secured in advance by the party demanding the jury ... is ... a moderate and reasonable restriction upon the enjoyment of the right of a trial by jury and is not an impairment of that right.” Miller v. Eagle, 96 Ohio St. 106, 110, 117 N.E. 23 (1917). Accord Walters v. Griffith, 38 Ohio St.2d 132, 133, 311 N.E.2d 14 (1974).

Also, courts have held that advance deposit requirements in the amounts of ten dollars, one hundred fifty dollars, and one hundred seventy -five dollars, respectively, do not constitute a violation of Article I, Section 5 of the Ohio Constitution. Walters
v. Griffith, 38 Ohio St.2d 132, 311 N.E.2d 14 (1974) ($10 advance deposit);Dondorfer
v. Wolf, No. 13 -010, 1988 WL 105568 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Oct. 7, 1988) ($150 advance deposit); Hocking Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Martin , No. 97 -CA-9, 1998 WL 100416 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Mar. 5, 1998) ($175 advance deposit).

In contrast, an appellate court judge, in a concurring opinion, found that a requirement “of an advance deposit of $300.00 to $400.00 is not a moderate and 61 reasonable restriction of the right of a jury trial and constitutes an unconstitutional abridgement and impairment of the right of trial by jury.” Lewis Furniture Co. v.

Dunkin, No. 1175, 1979 Ohio App. LEXIS 12447, at *3 (Ohio App., 4th Di st., Mar.

26, 1979) (Stephenson, J., concurring).

When a party is unable to make the R.C. 1901.26(A)(3) advance deposit because of the party’s indigency, the court is required to waive this deposit requirement. R.C.

1901.26(A)(3) exempts from its advance deposit requirement those parties that the court concludes are unable to make the required deposit. If the party’s affidavit or other evidence demonstrates the party’s inability to make this advance deposit, under R.C. 1901.26(A)(3), the court is required to determine whether in fact the party is indigent and, if the determination is affirmative, the court is required to waive the R.C.

1901.26(A)(3) advance deposit. Cf. Lable & Co. v. Francis , No. 89-J-39, 1991 WL 24982 (Ohio App., 7th Dist., Feb. 22, 199 1) (construing analogous provision of R.C.

1907.24(A) on waiver of advance deposits for jury demands in county courts).44 Furthermore, because the Ohio Constitution establishes the right to a trial by jury in an eviction action (see Section I.J.1, above), the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution requires a waiver of the R.C.

1901.26(A)(3) advance deposit requirement for indigent parties.See Lecates v. Justice of Peace Court No. 4, Etc., 637 F.2d 898 (3d Cir. 1980).
