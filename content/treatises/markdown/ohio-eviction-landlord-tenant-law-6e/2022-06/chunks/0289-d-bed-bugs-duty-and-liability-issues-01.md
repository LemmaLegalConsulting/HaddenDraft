# d. Bed Bugs – Duty and Liability Issues

## Source text

A bed bug infestation at rental premises may constitute a violation of R.C.

5321.04(A)(2). E.g., Bynum v. Cotterman, No. 26222, 2015 WL 751720 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 20, 2015); Showe Mgmt. Corp. v. Wright , No. 12-CVG-00724 (Ohio Mun., Clinton Cty., Oct. 26, 2012).See alsoKolb v. DeVille I Props., LLC, 326 S.W.3d 896 (Mo. App. 2010) (implied warranty of habitability violation – bed bugs); Ludlow Props., LLC v. Young , 4 Misc.3d 515, 780 N.Y.S.2d 853 (Civ. Ct. 2004) (implied warranty of habitability violation – bed bugs). Cf. Morel v. Marks Group, LLC, Nos.

HCH-598, H-1532, 2011 WL 2712996 (Conn. Super. June 21, 2011) (implied warranty of habitability violation – bed bugs and cockroaches).

When a landlord is aware of a bed bug infestation at the rental premises (and thus is aware of the R.C. 5321.04(A)(2) violation), the landlord is obligated to remedy the infestation (like any other R.C. 5321.04(A) violation).

If the landlord fails to remedy timely the bed bug infestation ( i.e., the R.C.

5321.04(A)(2) violation), the tenant’s remedies are the remedies that are available for any R.C. 5321.04(A) violation ( see Chapters VIII and IX, below), including a claim for money damages. E.g., Bynum v. Cotterman, No. 26222, 2015 WL 751720 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 20, 2015) (damage award of $200 per month for eleven months, for total of $2,200); Sanders v. McGee, No. 13-CV-003983 (Ohio C.P., Franklin Cty.,
Apr. 3, 2014) (compensatory damage award included $4,000 for personal property damage and $5,000 for incidental expenses).

A tenant generally is not liable for the landlord’s costs for remediating the infestation (i.e., the R.C. 5321.04(A)(2) violation), unless the landlord can establish that the infestation is the result of the tenant’s violation of an R.C. 5321.05 duty.E.g., Showe Mgmt. Corp. v. Mountjoy, 154 N.E.3d 141, 2020-Ohio-2772 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., 2020) (tenant liable for cost of bed bug extermination where the bed bugs were the result of tenant’s carelessness/misuse/neglect);Spring Hill Town Homes v. Pounds, 972 N.E.2d 1121, 2012-Ohio-2800, ¶¶ 27-32 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., 2012) (tenant not liable for cost of bed bug extermination because trial court did not find tenant failed to keep the premises safe and sanitary) ; Showe Mgmt. Corp. v. Wright , No. 12 -CVG- 00724 (Ohio Mun., Clinton Cty., Oct. 26, 2012) (tenant not liable for cost of extermination because landlord did not establish at trial the source of the bed bugs).

The tenant has a duty to cooperate in the landlord’s efforts to remediate the
infestation. See Section IX.A.6, below. If a tenant violates this duty, it may be a defense to the tenant’ s claim for the R.C. 5321.04(A)(2) violation. E.g., Kenwood Gardens Ass’n, LLC v. Shorter, No. L-10-1315, 2011 WL 3654493 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Aug. 19, 2011).

The duties relative to a tenant’s personal property, when a bed bug infestation of 321 the rental premises causes an infestation of the personal property, raise two issues.

The first issue is whether the landlord has a duty to remediate (or compensate the tenant for) the infestation of the tenant’s personal property. If the landlord’s action or inaction caused the infestation of the rental premises and thereby the personal property, the landlord is liable for any damages resulting from this R.C. 5321.04(A)(2) violation (see Chapter IX, below), including the personal property remediation (or compensation). If the landlord’s action or inaction did not cause the infestation of the rental premises and thereby the personal property, the landlord has not violated R.C.

5321.04(A)(2) and thus is not liable for the personal property remediation (or compensation). See Chesterfield LLC v. Mackow , No. 08 -CVG-16093 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Mar. 5, 2009).

The second issue concerns the duty and rights of the tenant relative to the remediation or disposition of the tenant’s personal property. A landlord may elect not to remediate the infestation of the tenant’s personal property270 if the landlord’s action or inaction did not cause the infestation. In such cases, the landlord may ask the tenant either to remediate the infestation of the tenant’s personal property or to remove the personal property from the dwelling unit, within a reasonable period of time.

If the tenant fails to take either course of action, the issue is whether the landlord may then remove the tenant’s personal property from the rental premises in order to remediate fully the infestation of the premises. R.C. Ch apter 1923 provides the landlord with an expeditious process for a court to remove forcibly the tenant and the tenant’s personal property. If the landlord elects instead to remove the property unilaterally (i.e., via a “self-help” approach), it might constitute unlawful conversion.
