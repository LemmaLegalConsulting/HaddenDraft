# 3. Reasonable Time to Remedy Issue

## Source text

In an R.C. 5321.04 contract claim, an issue exists as to whether the landlord is liable for an R.C. 5321.04 violation upon receiving notice of the defective condition or upon the landlord’s failure (if any) to remedy the con dition within a reasonable time after receiving such notice.

One appellate court has held that a landlord is not liable for an R.C. 5321.04 violation if the landlord remedies the defective condition within a reasonable time after receiving notice of the condition. Pedraza v. Oster Invs., Inc., No. 7-87-10, 1989 WL 29215 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., Mar. 21, 1989). It concluded that, “[w]hile the landlord may have a duty to maintain the stairway in good repair, a reasonable amount of time must be allowed to effect the repairs.” Id., 1989 WL 29215, at *2. See also Stiffler v.

Canterbury Runn Apts., No. 19308, 2002 WL 31243541 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Oct. 4,
2002) (failed to remedy within reasonable time);Boice v. Emshoff, No. 13-98-23, 1998 WL 833686 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., Dec. 3, 1998).

The implied warranty of habitability case law in other jurisdictions is split on this
issue. Compare Berman & Sons, Inc. v. Jefferson , 379 Mass. 196, 396 N.E.2d 981
(1979) (liability upon receiving notice), and Knight v. Hallsth ammer, 29 Cal.3d 46, 171 Cal. Rptr. 707 (1981) with Glasoe v. Trinkle, 107 Ill.2d 1, 479 N.E.2d 915 (1985) (liability upon failure to remedy within a reasonable time after notice), and Detling v.

Edelbrock, 671 S.W.2d 265 (Mo. 1984).

The reasoning of the c ourt decisions holding that the landlord is liable upon receiving notice of the defective condition is summarized in Jefferson, 379 Mass. at 202-03 (alteration in original):

The landlord argues that the existence of a serious defect in an 401 apartment is a potential breach of the warranty; the breach becomes actual only after the landlord has been notified of the defect and has had a reasonable time to repair. In Hemingway, however, we set out a very different picture of the warranty: “[The warranty] means that at the inception of the rental there are no latent [or patent] defects in facilities vital to the use of the premises for residential purposes and that these essential fac ilities will remain during the entire term in a condition which makes the property livable.” Boston Hous. Auth. v.

Hemingway, supra at 199, quoting from Kline v. Burns, 111 N.H. 87, 92 (1971). Neither at the inception nor during the term of the lease did we leave room for a reasonable time to repair. Admittedly a tenant must notify her landlord of defects as a prerequisite to a rent abatement, but the purpose of this requirement is not to assure the landlord a reasonable time to repair. The requirement is designed to minimize the time the landlord is in breach and hence mitigate the permissible period of abatement of rent. The rent abatement begins when notice is given, not at a reasonable time after notice. Time for repairs has no place in the calculus.
