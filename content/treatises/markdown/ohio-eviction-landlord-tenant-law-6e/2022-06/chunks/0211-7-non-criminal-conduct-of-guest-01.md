The improper conduct of a guest may be good cause to terminate an assisted housing tenancy. E.g., Agler Green Coop. v. Rivers, No. 87AP-915, 1988 WL 37063 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Mar. 29, 1988). See also Chavez v. Hous. Auth. of City of El Paso, 973 F.2d 1245 (5th Cir. 1992).

However, in some circumstances, the conduct of a guest is not good cause to terminate an assisted housing tenancy if: (1) the tenant neither knows nor has reason to know that the guest is inclined to engage in improper conduct, (2) the tenant had no knowledge of the guest’s improper conduct, and/or (3) the tenant could not reasonably 206 For cases applying a similar standard relative to the conduct of the tenant’s guest, see Section III.G.7, below.

R.C. 5321.05(A)(6) establishes a similar standard for terminating a tenancy based on the improper conduct of a guest, and it may apply to assisted housing tenancies. It requires a tenant to “personally refrain and forbid any other person who is on the premises with his permission from intentionally o r negligently destroying, defacing, damaging, or removing any fixture, appliance, or other part ot the premises.” The R.C. 5321.05(A)(6) standard does not make the tenant strictly liable for a guest’s improper conduct. Allstate Ins. Co. v. Dorsey, 46 Ohio App.3d 66, 545 N.E.2d 920 (9th Dist. 1988). “R.C. 5321.05(A)(6) does not require the tenant to prevent damage to the leased premises, but rather personally to refrain from such activity and to forbid others from engaging in it.” DAK, PLL v. Borgerding, No. 02AP-1051, 2003 WL 21469136, ¶ 18 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., June 26, 2003).

Therefore, an assisted housing lease is subject to the R.C. 5321.05(A)(6) standard.

This means that, with respect to the R.C. 5321.05(A)(6) requirements (and any similar assisted housing lease provisions), a guest’s conduct may not constitute a violation of the lease (or good cause to terminate the lease) if it does not constitute a violation of R.C. 5321.05(A)(6).
