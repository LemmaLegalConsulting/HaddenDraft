# 5. Automatic Stay

## Source text

If an eviction action against the tenant is pending and an eviction judgment has not been entered against the tenant as of the date that the tenant files the bankruptcy petition, the bankruptcy filing generally operates as an automatic stay of the eviction
proceeding. 11 U.S.C. § 362. See, e.g., Hayes v. Kelly , No. L -14-1153, 2015 WL 995446 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Mar. 6, 2015).

The one exception to this general rule is that, under 11 U.S.C. §§ 365(b)(23), 365(m), the automatic stay may not apply to the pending eviction action if the landlord files with the bankruptcy court, and serves on the tenant, a certification that: (1) the tenant is endangering the real property or is using (or is allowing to be used) controlled substances on the property; and (2) the pending eviction action is based on these
grounds. Within 15 days of the filing of the certification, the tenant may file objections to the landlord’s claim under this exception, on the ground that the alleged underlying facts giving rise to the landlord’s claim did not exist or have been remedied. If the tenant demonstrates either of these grounds, the automatic stay will continue to apply to the pending eviction action.

If an eviction action against the tenant is pending and an eviction judgment against the tenant has been entered as of the date that the tenant files the bankruptcy petition, generally the bankruptcy filing does not operate as an automatic stay of the eviction
proceeding. 11 U.S.C. §§ 362(b)(22), 362(l)(a)(3).

The one exception to this general rule is that the bankruptcy filing operates as an automatic stay of the eviction proceeding for thirty days, if the tenant files with the bankruptcy petition and serves upon the landlord a certification that: (1) un der applicable non -bankruptcy law, “there are circumstances under which the debtor [tenant] would be permitted to cure the entire monetary default that gave rise to the judgment for possession, after that judgment for possession was entered;” and (2) the tenant “has deposited with the clerk of the court, any rent that would become due during the 30-day period after the filing of the bankruptcy petition.”246 Id. If, during the 30-day period, the tenant cures the entire monetary default that gave rise to the eviction judgment, the automatic stay will continue to apply to the eviction proceeding.
Id. However, “§ 525(a) eliminates the need for a Public Housing debtor to cure a prepetition default as a condition to rendering the stay exception in § 362(b)(22) inapplicable.” In re Kelly, 356 B.R. 899, 901 (Bankr. S.D. Fla.2006).

Since Ohio law does not provide a tenant with a right to cure a monetary default after the entry of an eviction judgment, this exception to the general rule appears to be inapplicable to Ohio tenants.

If an eviction judgment is subject to de novo review, it is not an eviction judgment for § 362(b)(22) purposes. In re Alberts, 381 B.R. 171 (Bankr. W.D. Pa. 2008) (under Pennsylvania law, a district magistrate court’s eviction judgment issubject to de novo review in the court of common pleas and, therefore, is not an eviction judgment for § 362(b)(22) purposes). For Ohio tenants, this means that a magistrate’s eviction decision, which is subject to de novo review by a judge (see Ohio R. Civ. P. 53(D)), is not an eviction judgment for § 362(b)(22) purposes.

246 See, e.g., In re Harris, 424 B.R. 44 (Bankr. E.D.N.Y. 2010) (landlord permitted to enforce pre-filing eviction judgment because tenant failed to file the §§ 362(b)(22),362(l)(a)(3) certification with the filing of the bankruptcy petition).
