## Source text

Limitation R.C. 5321.11, together with R.C. 1923.02(A)(8), permit a landlord to terminate a tenancy for a tenant’s violation of R.C. 5321.05, but only if the R.C. 5321.05 violation materially affects health and safety. They also make any such termination of tenancy subject to the notice and right to cure requirements that are discussed in Sections I.D.2.b-g, below.

A resulting issue is whether, despite the inapplicability of R.C. 5321.11,a landlord may terminate a tenancy for a tenant’s violation of R.C. 5321.05 if the violation does not materially affect health and safety. The case law is split.Compare Jones v. Burns,
No. R90 -CVG-2008 (Ohio Mun., Portage Cty., Oct. 10, 1990) (terminati on not
permitted) with Starr v. Kaderly, No. 96-APG03-293, 1996 WL 465256 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Aug. 15, 1996) (termination permitted), Berkeley Park, LLC v. Pile , No.

2005-CVG-046214 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., Apr. 11, 2007), and Klepetko v. Bettis,
No. 06-CVG-09092 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., May 11, 2006).

The better reasoned conclusion is that the landlord may not terminate the tenancy for such violations. Three factors support this conclusion.

First, R.C. 5321.11 is the only provision in R.C. Chapter 5321 that authorizes a landlord to terminate a tenancy for a tenant’s violation of R.C. 5321.05. This evidences the legislature’s intent to permit a termination of tenancy for an R.C. 5321.05 violation only if the violation materially affects health and safety.

Second, a construction of the statute that would permit a landlord to terminate a tenancy for an R.C. 5321.04 violation that does not materially affect health and safety would grant a tenant more rights – a 30 -day notice and right to cure – when the violation materially affects health and safety than when the violation does not materially affect health and safety. It is unlikely that the legislature intended the illogical consequence of granting tenants more rights for engaging in mor e harmful
behavior. When a statute is ambiguous, as in this matter, the courts are to consider “the consequences of a particular construction” (R.C. 1.49(E)) and “to presume that [a] just and reasonable result is intended” (R.C. 1.47(C)).

Third, prohibiting a termination of tenancy for R.C. 5321.05 violations that do not materially affect health and safety is consistent with the legal maxim “de minimis not curat lex,” which is discussed, with respect to residential leases, in Section II.W, below.

A relevant sub-issue is whether R.C. 1923.02(A)(9) is authority for terminating a tenancy for tenant conduct that violates R.C. 5321.05 and a rental agreement provision but does not materially affect health and safety. R.C. 1923.02(A)(9) authorizes eviction actions for breach of an obligation under a written rental agreement.

For oral periodic rental agreements, R.C. 1923.02(A)(9) is simply inapplicable and, therefore, cannot be a basis for terminating the tenancy for an R.C. 5321.05 violation that does not materially affect health and safety.

For written rental agreements, because R.C. Chapter 5321 does not permit a termination of tenancy for an R.C. 5321.05 violation that does not materially affect health and safety, R.C. 1923.02(A)(9) cannot be a basis for terminating the tenancy for such violations. R.C. 5321.06 and 5321.13(A) prohibit lease provisions that are inconsistent with R.C. Chapter 5321. As discussed in Section I.D.2.f, below, this restriction applies to terminations of tenancies for a tenant’s violation of R.C. 5321.05.
