# 1. Unconscionable Provisions, R.C. 5321.14

## Source text

If a rental agreement provision was unconscionable at the time that the parties executed the agreement, under R.C. 5321.14, a court may refuse to enforce the provision or may limit the application of the provision so as to avoid an unconscionable result.

A provision is unconscionable only if it is both substantively and procedurally
unconscionable. Oldendick v. Crocker, 70 N.E.3d 1033, 2016-Ohio-5621 (Ohio App., 373 8th Dist., 2016); Anderson v. Ballard, No. L-10-1007, 2010 WL 3292685 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Aug. 20, 2010). See also Pojman v. Columbia-Brookpark Mgmt., LLC, No.

88666, 2007 WL 2269682 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Aug. 9, 2007) (construing R.C.

3733.16 (now R.C. 4781.48) – the manufactured home park provision that is identical to R.C. 5321.14). Cf. Taylor Bldg. Corp. of Am. v. Benfield, 117 Ohio St.3d 352, 884 N.E.2d 12 (2008) (arbitration clause) ; Harbour Portfolio VII, LP v. Pulley , Nos. C - 150080,150090, 2015 WL 6438920 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Oct. 23, 2015) (arbitration clause, land installment contract).

Substantive unconscionability focuses on the fairness of the terms. Id. Procedural unconscionability focuses on the relative bargaining position of the parties. Id.

Under R.C. 5321.14, a party may assert the unconscionability of a rental agreement provision as a defense to a claim, without asserting a counterclaim. Anderson v.

Ballard, No. L-10-1007, 2010 WL 3292685 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Aug. 20, 2010).

When the alleged unconscionable provision is an increased rent, the relevant issue is whether the amount of the new rent (and not the amount of the rent increase) is
unconscionable. Pojman v. Columbia-Brookpark Mgmt., LLC, No. 88666, 2007 WL 2269682 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Aug. 9, 2007).

The “reasonable opportunity to present evidence” clause in R.C. 5321.14(B) does not preclude a summary judgment ruling, without a hearing, when the parties submit all of the relevant evidence and no genuine issue of material fact exists. Id.

The “determination of whether a written contract is unconscionable is an issue of law.” Taylor Bldg. Corp. of Am. v. Benfield, 117 Ohio St.3d 352,884 N.E.2d 12, ¶ 35 (2008). Hence, on appeal, the unconscionability determination is reviewed de novo, but the underlying factual determinations are reviewed with deference. Id., ¶ 37,38.

The R.C. 5321.14 prohibition against unconscionable provisions does not apply to an option agreement to purchase real property. Stacy v. Zupp, No. CA-9566, 1994 WL 369899 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., June 20, 1994); SMJ Growth Corp. v. Norman, No. 96- CVG-02843 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Aug. 29, 1996).

Court decisions on the R.C. 5321.14 prohibition against unconscionable provisions are summarized below and in Section VII.F.9.c., above (late payment fees).

Automatic Renewal:

Cont’l Enters., Ltd. v. Hunt, No. 102200, 2015 WL 9460300 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Dec. 24, 2015) Lease provision provided for automatic renewal of one -year lease term, unless tenant gave landlord 60 days (or more) advance written notice of
nonrenewal. Based on the relevant communications, it would be hypertechnical and unconscionable to find that the lease extended for another year. Those communications included: tenant’s ongoing complaints regarding a roof leak, the landlord’s refusal to transfer tena nt to another unit, tenant’s 90-advance written notice of her relocation plans and request for a month-to-month arrangement (which landlord rejected), and tenant’s 50-day advance written notice of nonrenewal.

Adair v. Landis Props., Inc. , No. 08AP -139, 2008 WL 4174130 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Sept. 11, 2008) Three-month lease with provision for automatic conversion to month -to- month tenancy, unless the tenant gave the landlord 30 days (or more) advance written notice of nonrenewal. Remand because genuine issue on whether the tenant provided the landlord with timely oral notice, but not written notice, of the tenant’s intent not to renew. If the tenant establishes that such oral notice was given, like McGowan (summarized below), in these circumstances, to require written notice would be unconscionable.

Hackman v. Szczygiel , No. 06AP -187, 2006 WL 3199278 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Nov. 7, 2006) The lease provision provided for the automatic renewal of a one-year term, unless the tenant gave the landlord 120 days (or more) advance written notice of nonrenewal by certified USPS mail. The tenant provided the landlord with timely oral notice, but not written notice, of the tenant’s intent not to renew. Like McGowan (summarized below), in the circumstances of this case, to require written notice would be unconscionable.

McGowan v. DM Group IX , 7 Ohio App.3d 349, 455 N.E.2d 1052 (10th

The lease provision was not in itself unconscionable.
