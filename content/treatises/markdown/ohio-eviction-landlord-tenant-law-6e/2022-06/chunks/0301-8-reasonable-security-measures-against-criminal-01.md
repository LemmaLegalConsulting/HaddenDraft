Activity R.C. 5321.04 does not require a landlord to protect the tenant against criminal activity of a third person. Carmichael v. Colonial Square Apts., 38 Ohio App.3d 131, 528 N.E.2d 585 (10th Dist. 1987); Cherkiss v. Thomas, No. L-83-416, 1984 WL 7829 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Apr. 13, 1984).See also Brown v. Campbell, Nos. 85698,85702, 2005 WL 1793519 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., July 28, 2005); Johnson v. Spectru m o f Supportive Servs., No. 82267, 2003 WL 21982915 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Aug. 21, 2003); Sayles v. SB-92 Ltd. P’ship, 138 Ohio App.3d 476, 741 N.E.2d 613 (8th Dist.

2000); Doe v. Beach House Dev. Co., 136 Ohio App.3d 573, 737 N.E.2d 141 (8th Dist.

2000); Doe v. Flair Corp. , 129 Ohio App.3d 739, 719 N.E.2d 34 (8th Dist. 1998);

Meier v. Vistula Heritage Vill. , 62 Ohio Misc.2d 632, 609 N.E.2d 1360 (Ohio C.P., Lucas Cty., 1992); Pamer v. Pritchard Bros. , 61 Ohio Misc.2d 150, 575 N.E.2d 900 (Ohio C.P., Clermont Cty., 1990).

However, a landlord does have a duty to provide in the common area reasonable security measures against criminal activity. Carmichael v. Colonial Square Apts., 38 Ohio App.3d 131, 528 N.E.2d 585 (10th Dist. 1987); Sciascia v. Riverpark Apts ., 3 Ohio App.3d 164, 444 N.E.2d 40 (10th Dist. 1981). See also Pieper v. Williams, No.

L-05-1065, 2006 WL 964725 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Apr. 14, 2006); Johnson v.

Spectrum of Supportive Services , No. 82267, 2003 WL 21982915 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Aug. 21, 2003); Sayles v. SB-92 Ltd. P’ship, 138 Ohio App.3d 476, 741 N.E.2d 613 (8th Dist. 2000);Doe v. Beach House Dev. Co., 136 Ohio App.3d 573, 737 N.E.2d 141 (8th Dist. 2000); Kelly v. Bear Creek Inv. Co., No. 58011, 1991 WL 19152 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Feb. 14, 1991);Flowers v. City of Cincinnati, No. C-880809, 1990 WL 27176 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Mar. 14, 1990); Hawk v. Fairmeadows Apts., No. CA86- 12-180, 1987 WL 13707 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., June 29, 1987); Pamer v. Pritchard Bros., 61 Ohio Misc.2d 150, 575 N.E.2d 900 (Ohio C.P., Clermont Cty., 1990).

Sciascia held that this duty arises from the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment.

3 Ohio App. 3d at 166. See also Doe v. New Bedford Hous. Auth., 417 Mass. 273, 630 N.E.2d 248 (1994). Carmichael relied on Sciascia for its holding and thus presumably for the source of the duty. Pieper, Johnson, Sayles, Doe, Kelly, Flowers, Hawk, and Pamer rely on Sciascia and/or Carmichael for their holdings and thus presumably rely on Sciascia for the source of the duty as well.

Also, a “landlord may contractually [in a lease provision] create a duty to provide security to his tenants.” Brown v. Campbell, Nos. 85698,85702, 2005 WL 1793519, ¶ 23 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., July 28, 2005). Accord Pamer v. Pritchard Bros., 61 Ohio Misc.2d 150, 153, 575 N.E.2d 900 (Ohio C.P., Clermont Cty., 1990). For example, two courts have held that, where a landlord promises to provide window locks, it may create a contractual obligation to do so and, if the breach of that obligation is the proximate cause of criminal activity, the landlord may be liable for injury resulting from the criminal activity. Blair v. Prop. Mgmt. Consultants , 40 Ohio App.3d 103, 531 N.E.2d 752 (1st Dist. 1987); Buncy v. Daniels , No. CA -6059, 1983 WL 6486 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., June 13, 1983). See also Brown v. Campbell , Nos. 85698, 85702, 2005 WL 1793519 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., July 28, 2005) (accepts theBlair rule, but finds no firm offer to install the new doors and thus no contractual duty).

But a lease provision that “states that ‘disorderly conduct of any nature will result in an eviction’ .... does not create a duty on the part of [the landlord] to protect tenants from the criminal actions of other tenants.” Flowers v. City of Cincinnati , No. C - 880809, 1990 WL 27176, at *2 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Mar. 14, 1990).

If a landlord provides security services to protect tenants against criminal activity, the landlord is obligated to exercise reasonable care in providing those services.Pamer
v. Pritchard Bros., 61 Ohio Misc.2d 150, 575 N.E.2d 900 (Ohio C.P., Clermont Cty., 1990).
