## Source text

A tenant’s testimony may be strengthened through the use of p hotographs of the
premises. Photographs of the defective condition often relate the identity and severity of the defect far more vividly than oral testimony.

Therefore, where appropriate, the tenant or another person should take photographs of the defecti ve condition for the purpose of introducing them into
evidence. The tenant can testify that the p hotographs are fair and accurate images of the scene at a particular time even if another person took the p hotographs, and such testimony is an adequate foundation for their admission into evidence.Hardy v. Miller, 425
No. 98-CA-13, 1999 WL 1256347 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Dec. 9, 1999); Solomon v.

Mote, 38 Ohio Law Abs. 169, 49 N.E.2d 703 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., 1942).

The acceptance of photographic evidence rest s within the sound discretion of the trial court. Glover v. Massey, Nos. 56351,56802, 1990 WL 1328, at *2 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Jan. 11, 1990).

For the landlord’s analogous right to introduce into evidence relevant photographs in support of an R.C. 5321.05(C) claim, see Section XVIII.F.5, below.
