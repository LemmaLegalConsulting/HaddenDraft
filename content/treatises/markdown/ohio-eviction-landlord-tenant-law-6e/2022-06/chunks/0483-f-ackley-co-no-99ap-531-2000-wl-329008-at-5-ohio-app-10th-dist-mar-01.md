When a landlord mitigates damages, the tenant is liable for the rent under the lease until the end of the lease term or until the premises are relet, whichever occurs first.

Dennis v. Morgan, 89 Ohio St.3d 417, 419, 732 N.E.2d 391 (2000).

If a landlord relets the premises before the end of the lease term for a higher rent than the rent under the first lease, an issue exists as to whether this difference in rent amounts may reduce the first tenant’s rental obligation under the first lease. Compare Strip Delaware, LLC v. Landry’s Rest., Inc.,No. 2010-CA-00316, 2011 WL 3587455, ¶¶ 22-29 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Aug. 15, 2011) (not reduced) with Way Int’l v. Ohio Ctr., 3 Ohio App.3d 451, 452, 445 N.E.2d 1158 (10th Dist. 1982) (reduced).

When the landlord mitigates damages, the tenant is also liable for the costs of the landlord’s reasonable efforts to re-rent the property. For exa mple, the landlord may recover the reasonable cost of newspaper advertisements that announce the availability of the rental unit. Carr v. Ed Stein Realtors, 10 Ohio App.3d 242, 461 N.E.2d 930 (9th
Dist. 1983); Alba v. Lockhart, No. 94-CVI-140 (Ohio County Ct., Jefferson Cty., Mar.

One appellate court has held that the landlord’s duty to mitigate damages also applies where a tenant remains in occupancy of the rental premises without making rent payments for an extended period. May v. Petrick, No. 2009-CA-00031, 2010 WL 1452604 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Apr. 8, 2010). In May, the tenant failed to make rent payments for 30 months. The court held that the landlord had a duty to commence an eviction action to mitigate damages. It thereupon upheld the trial court’s decision that awarded the landlord a money judgment for unpaid rent for only six of those months, based on the landlord’s breach of the duty to mitigate damages.
