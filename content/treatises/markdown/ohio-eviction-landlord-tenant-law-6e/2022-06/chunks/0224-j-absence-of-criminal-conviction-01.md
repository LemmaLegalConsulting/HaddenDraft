# j. Absence of Criminal Conviction

## Source text

The criminal conduct of a tenant, a household member, or a guest may be good cause to terminate an assisted housing tenancy, regardless of whether the person has been arrested or convicted for the conduct. 24 C.F.R. § 966.4(l)(5)(iii) (Public Housing); 24 C.F.R. § 982.310(c)(3) (HCVP); 24 C.F.R. § 5.861 (project -based subsidized housing), which applies to criminal conduct under 24 C.F.R. §§ 5.859 and 5.860 and is thereby incorporated into 24 C.F.R. § 247.3(a). See New Longwood
Assocs. v. Perry , No. 09 -CVG-12567 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Apr. 22, 251
2010) (subsidized housing lease,219 ¶ 23(c)(6)). Cf. Woodruff Vill. v. Bowers , No. L- 95-248, 1996 WL 339912 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., June 21, 1996) (criminal conviction not required by the lease);Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Blake, No. 98-CVG-05998 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Aug. 19, 1999) (criminal conviction not required under former 24 C.F.R. § 966.4).

The eviction action may proceed even if a criminal action is pending and awaiting the commencement of trial. New Longwood Assocs. v. Perry, No. 09-CVG-12567, slip
op. at 10 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Apr. 22, 2010). In the eviction action, the court concludes whether the landlord “has established by a preponderance of the evidence that the defendant [tenant] has engaged in criminal activity ..., for the limited purpose of being in breach of the terms of the Lease. This conclusion is not intended to indicate that the defendant has been found guilty of having committed a crime;

clearly in a criminal matter, the law requires the prosecution to prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt. These issues remain to be tried in another case, in another court.” Id., slip op. at 9.
