# b. Code Inspector Testimony

## Source text

While not required (see Section IX.A.12.a, above), when a tenant alleges an R.C.

5321.04(A)(1) violation, the best proof of the violation is the testimony of a governmental employee who is responsible for enforcing the code and who has inspected the premises (“code inspector”).

The code inspector may testify as to: (1) the conditions that the code inspector observed at the premises, (2) whether the code inspector cited the landlord for any violation of the applicable codes as a result of those conditions, and (3) whether the code inspector notified the landlord of those conditions and/or of any related citation.

E.g., Surrey Park Assocs. v. Kuhner, No. 89-L-14-120, 1990 WL 222990 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Dec. 28, 1990).

The code inspector usually will reduce to writing the code inspector’s findings and citations (“inspector’s report”). A stipulation or an admission as to the authenticity of the inspector’s report and the landlord’s receipt of the inspector’s report may be sufficient to introduce it into evidence.

Otherwise, to introduce the inspector’s report into evidence, the testimony of the governmental employee who is responsible for maintaining such reports ( i.e., the custodian of the records) may be necessary. The custodian of the records can testify as to the identity of the inspector’s report and the manner in which such reports are maintained in the normal course of business. Based on that testimony, the inspector’s report should be admissible. See Ohio R. Evid. 803(6),(8).

The inspector’s report may c ontain hearsay information, such as assertions concerning the premises that the code inspector did not observe. For example, it may contain assertions by the landlord or tenant concerning conditions at the premises on a date prior to the code inspection.

However, such assertions in the inspector’s report are not always hearsay. For example, if the assertion is a declaration by the tenant and the tenant’s testimony at trial is consistent with that prior statement, and if such testimony has been impeached or attacked as a recent fabrication, the prior statement of the tenant recorded in the inspector’s report is not hearsay. Ohio R. Evid. 801(D)(1). See Polk v. Gray , No.

36896, 1978 Ohio App. LEXIS 9771, at *5 -6 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Feb. 9, 1978);

Miller v. Piqua Transfer & Storage Co., 57 Ohio Law Abs. 325, 92 N.E.2d 452 (Ohio C.P., Butler Cty., 1950). And, if the assertion recorded in the inspector’s report is a declaration by the landlord, it may be an admission by a party-opponent and therefore not hearsay. Ohio R. Evid. 801(D)(2). See Polk, 1978 Ohio App. LEXIS 9771, at *8,9.

When the inspector’s report contains some information that is inadmissible and some information that is admissible, only the inadmissible portion of the report is subject to an objection and motion to exclude. See Yuin v. Hilton, 165 Ohio St. 164, 134 N.E.2d 719 (1956).

In these circumstances, an objection to the introduction of the whole report is insufficient and should be overruled. Id.; Carson v. Metro. Life Ins. Co., 156 Ohio St.

104, 100 N.E.2d 197 (1951). The objecting party is obligated to identify and object to 427 the portion of the document that constitutes hearsay or is otherwise inadmissible and, thereupon, move for the exclusion of only the incompetent evidence. Yuin v. Hilton, 165 Ohio St. 164, 134 N.E.2d 719 (1956). The court may then excise the inadmissible portion of the document and admit the excised document into evidence or, in lieu thereof, permit an appropriate witness to recite the admissible and relevant portion of the document. Sanders v. Hairston , 51 Ohio App.3d 63, 554 N.E.2d 9 51 (8th Dist.

1988).
