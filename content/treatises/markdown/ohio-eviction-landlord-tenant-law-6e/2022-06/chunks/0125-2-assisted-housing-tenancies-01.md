For assisted housing tenants, 100 an issue exists as to whether the landlord’s acceptance of the rent subsidy payment that is made on behalf of the tenant constitutes the acceptance of rent, for purposes of the waiver rule that is discussed in Section II.C.1., above. There are two basic types of rental assistance programs.

One type of program is tenant-based rental assistance, such as the Housing Choice Voucher program (“HCVP”). 24 C.F.R. part 982. Under the HCVP, the family selects a dwelling unit in the private market at which it will use the rent subsid y. 24 C.F.R.

§ 982.1(a)(2). “The PHA contracts with the owner to make rent subsidy payments on behalf of the family.” Id. The contract “only covers a single unit and a specified assisted family.” 24 C.F.R. § 982.1(b)(2). The family generally may move with the rent subsidy from one dwelling unit to another dwelling unit, at the end of any lease 100 For the definition of “assisted housing,” see note 163, below.
term. 24 C.F.R. §§ 982.1(b)(2), 982.354. The rent subsidy is tied to the family, not to the dwelling unit.

Under the HCVP, the contract rent is known as the “rent to owner,” and is defined as “the total monthly rent payable to the owner under the lease for the unit.” 24 C.F.R.

§ 982.4. The portion of the rent to owner that is paid by the family is known as the “family rent to owner.” Id. The portion of the rent to owner that is paid by the public housing authority (“PHA”) is known as a “housing assistance payment.” Id. The housing assistance payment is a “payment to the owner for rent to the owner under the family’s lease.” Id.

The second type of program is project-based assisted housing rental assistance,101 in which rental assistance is paid for families who live in specified housing developments or units. The subsidy is tied to particular dwelling units at a particular
development. Families who live in one of those dwelling units receive the benefit of the rental assistance. If the family vacates its dwelling unit, either voluntarily or involuntarily, the family no longer receives the benefit of the rental assistance.

Numerous Ohio courts, throughout the State, consistently and properly hold that a tenant-based rent subsidy (e.g., HCVP subsidy) is rent, for purposes of the waiver rule.

Given the definition of the HCVP housing assistance payment and the structure of the HCVP, these holdings are the only reasonable interpretation of the waiver rule, for the HCVP. The housi ng assistance payment is solely a rent payment under a lease made by a third-party (the PHA) directly on behalf of the tenant, for all or part of the contract rent.

These holdings are also consistent with the definition of rent: “compensation rendered for use of lands by a person permitted to hold or enjoy them.”Dayton Metro.
Hous. Auth. v. McKee , 37 Ohio App.3d 102, 104, 524 N.E.2d 180 (2nd Dist. 1987) (citing Sutliff v. Atwood , 15 Ohio St. 186, 193 (1864)). The housing assistance payment is simply the full or partial compensation to the owner for permitting the tenant to reside in the dwelling unit and is thus rent.

The only exceptions to these holdings are two court decisions concerning landlords who received a single HCVP rent subsidy check for multiple units. The decisions are Premier Mgmt., LLC v. Nutt , No. 6-09-15, 2010 WL 1175201 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist.,
Mar. 29, 2010), and Vienna Forest Apts. v. Passmore, No. CA2004-05-104, 2005 WL 1131742 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., May 16, 2005). In these circumstances, these courts held that the landlord’s cashing of the HCVP check did not constitute a waiver of the notice to vacate.

The problem and a reasonable solution for a landlord receiving a multi -unit rent subsidy check is well described in Macleay Woods Hous. Co. v. Franks , 16 Misc.3d 1136(A), 2007 WL 2597918 (N.Y. City Ct. 2007). “To require a landlord so situated to reject such an omnibus check would potentially jeopardize its financial health and thereby its ability to provide shelter for its other low income tenants.” Id., 2007 WL 25977918, at *4. The court thereupon held that the landlord had not accepted the HCVP rent subsidy, for waiver purposes, when it cashed the multi -unit HCVP rent subsidy check and then returned (within fourteen days) the HCVP s ubsidy for the tenant’s dwelling unit only. See also Woodbridge Homes Ltd. P’ship v. Gregory, 697 S.E.2d 370 (N.C. App. 2010) (project-based rental assistance).

This procedure – cashing the HCVP check and immediately returning the amount of the subsidy payment for the relevant tenancy – is consistent with the service of the notice to vacate. The landlord cashes the check to receive the subsidy payments for the other HCVP tenancies but does not retain the subsidy payment for the relevant
tenancy. Therefore, courts should find that a landlord’s use of this procedure does not waive the notice to vacate.

When a HCVP landlord receives a rent -subsidy check solely for the relevant tenancy, the landlord may request the PHA to hold the subsidy payments pending th e eviction action. Most (if not all) PHAs will honor this request and, at the conclusion of the eviction action, tender the withheld subsidy payments to the landlord.

While the Nutt decision is distinguishable because of the multi -unit rent subsidy check, it is also significantly flawed in two aspects.
