In an eviction action, the right to present possession 56 may depend on present ownership of record title to the premises. See Haas v. Gerski, 175 Ohio St. 327, 330, 197 N.E.2d 765 (1963); Hallmark Mgmt. v. Tartt , No. 49014, 1985 WL 9036, at *4 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., May 23, 1985) (“an occupant can properly deny any interest derived from the purported landowner and assert the occupant’s own ownership to defeat an eviction action”).

“In Ohio, a deed does not have to be recorded to pass title. Whether or not recorded, a deed in Ohio passes title upon its proper execution and delivery, so far as the grantor is able to convey it.” Wayne Bldg. & Loan Co. of Wooster v. Yarborough, 11 Ohio St.2d 195, 212, 228 N.E.2d 841 (1967). Accord In re Estate Ault , 80 Ohio App.3d 399, 402, 609 N.E.2d 568 (12th Dist. 1992).

Therefore, when a right to possession depends on when title has transferred to a new owner, the new owner has standing to bring the eviction action as of the date that the deed is executed and delivered, irrespective of whether the deed has been recorded.

Delli-Gatti v. Kokolari, No. 23469, 2007 WL 3356900 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Nov. 14, 2007).

When the right to present possession depends on present ownership of record title and there is no dispute as to which party holds present record title, municipal and county courts have jurisdiction to hear the eviction action.Estate of Allen v. Allen, No.

91-T-4580, 1992 WL 188346, at *2 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., June 12, 1992). See also Eckart v. Newman, No. WM-18-006, 2019 WL 3765412, ¶ 13 (Ohio App., 6th Dist.,
Aug. 9, 2019). In such cases, a municipal or county court “as an incident to determining the right to possession may determine in whom the present title rests ....”57 Haas, 175 Ohio St. at 130.

In such cases, present record title conclusively establishe s the right to present
possession. Haas v. Gerski, 175 Ohio St. 327, 330 -31, 197 N.E.2d 765 (1963); First Bank Nat’l Ass’n v. Willis, Nos. 56263,56763, 1989 WL 69409, at *2 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., June 22, 1989); Fredricks v. Stromberg , No. 42601, 1981 Ohi o App. LEXIS 10469, at *4-5 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Apr. 2, 1981). A party cannot raise invalidity of the present record title as a defense to the eviction action. Willis, 1989 WL 69409, at *2; Fredricks, 1981 Ohio App. LEXIS 10469, at *4-5.

In such cases, if the validity of the present record title is the subject of a pending action in a court of common pleas, the pendency of that action is not a permissible ground for granting a stay of the eviction action. State ex rel. Carpenter v. Warren
Mun. Court, 61 Ohio St.2d 208, 210, 400 N.E.2d 391 (1980). See also State ex rel.

Weiss v. Hoover, 84 Ohio St.3d 530, 705 N.E.2d 1227 (1999); Dvorak v. Habeeb, No.

60971, 1991 WL 106059 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., June 13, 1991). Cf. CS/RW Westlake Indoor Storage, L.L.C. v. Kesi, L.L.C. , No. 102535, 2015 WL 6819281, ¶¶ 6,7,17 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Nov. 5, 2015) (pending action on disputed purchase price of business); Clark v. Reynolds , No. 77749, 2000 WL 1714927 (Ohio App., 8th Dist.,
Nov. 16, 2000) (pending action on life interest in the property).

Hardy , No. 89905, 2008 WL 1822394, at *1 n.1 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Apr. 24, 2008) (in FED defe nse based on defendant’s color of title, “the defendant must show that there is a bona fide controversy as to title”). Municipal courts are without jurisdiction to hear such actions because R.C. 1901.18 does not grant municipal courts jurisdiction over qu iet title
actions. Id. Likewise, county courts are without jurisdiction to hear such actions because R.C. Chapter 1907 does not grant county courts jurisdiction over quiet title
actions. See R.C. 1907.05 (county court jurisdiction “in civil actions in which the title to real estate may be drawn in question” established for two types of actions, neither of which is an eviction action).
