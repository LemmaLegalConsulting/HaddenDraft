# B. Normal Wear and Tear

## Source text

In an R.C. 5321.05(C) claim, the tenant is not liable for normal wear and tear to the premises. Hensel v. Childress, 145 N.E.3d 1159, 2019-Ohio-3934 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., 2019); Warner v. Evans, No. 27536, 2015 WL 3398400 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., May 27, 2015); Snyder v. Waldron, No. 12-CA-9, 2013 WL 3988775 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., July 26, 2013); Whitestone Co. v. Stittsworth, No. 06AP-371, 2007 WL 155299 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Jan. 23, 2007);Bibler v. Nash, No. 5-05-09, 2005 WL 2335191 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., Sept. 26, 2005); Johnson v. Brown, No. 2002-CA-76, 2003 WL 1193795 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Mar. 14, 2003);Kelley v. Johnston, No. 01-CA-5, 2001 WL 1479243 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Nov. 14, 2001); Cuzzort v. Rose, No. CA-9791, 1986 WL 12632 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Nov. 6, 1986);Mentor Lagoons, Inc. v. Mayor,
No. 10-180, 1985 WL 7807 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Mar. 1, 1985);Konig v. Bella Dora Mgmt., Inc., 9 Ohio Misc.2d 7, 458 N.E.2d 477 (Ohio Mun., Akron, 1983).

For example, “[w]hen a tenant vacates a property after a tenancy of eleven (11) years, normal wear and tear typically requires the carpeting to be replaced and the interior walls to be painted.”Elsayed v. Miamisburg by the Mall, No. 28918, 2021 WL 2028661, ¶ 23 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., May 21, 2021) (adopting trial court’s statement).

And “[g]eneral cleaning costs may not be recovered as damages unless they are over and above ordinary wear and tear.”Schaedler v. Shinkle, No. CA99-09-025, 2000 WL 1283776, at *3 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Sept. 11, 2000). Accord May v. Metcalf,
No. 11-CA-8, 2011 WL 5625805, ¶ 33 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Nov. 15, 2011);McNeill
v. Wasserbauer, No. 72846, 1998 WL 323592, at *3 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Ju ne 18, 1998). Also, “[i]t is doubtful that any court would permit a landlord to impose a standard of ‘professional’ cleaning on a tenant in a residential rental property.” Douglas v. Campost Props., LLC , No. 2016 -CV-102094, slip op. at 4 (Ohio Mun., Elyria, July 21, 2020).

Normal wear and tear inclu des “injuries to the building due to the gradual deterioration which results from use, lapse of time, and the operation of the elements” and excludes “injuries from the tenant’s improper or negligent use of the premises.” 49 Am. Jur. 2d Landlord and Tenant § 973 at 946 (1970).

“[O]ne of the critical questions when determining if items are in need of repair due to normal wear and tear, or due to tenant conduct, is whether the items were originally installed or r epaired in a workmanlike way.” Brown v. Williams , No. 2012 -CVG- 17491, slip op. at 4 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Mar. 11, 2014, and May 2, 2014). In Brown, the court held that “Plaintiff [landlord] had not met her burden of proving that it was Defendant’s [tenant’s] conduct, and not the poor quality of repairs, that caused the problem.” Id.

Some courts have used the following guideline: “If damage is not of the type specified in R.C. 5321.05 or the lease, it will normally be considered ordinary w ear and tear.” Kelley v. Johnston, No. 01-CA-5, 2001 WL 1479243, at *3 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Nov. 14, 2001). Accord Elsayed v. Miamisburg by the Mall , No. 28918, 2021 WL 2028661, ¶ 33 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., May 21, 2021); Levine v. Kellogg , 153 N.E.3d 663, 2020-Ohio-1246, ¶ 40 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., 2020); Estie Inv. Co. v.

Braff, No. 2017-L-172, 2018 WL 5433223.¶ 25 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Oct. 29, 2018).

But n o general rule categorically defines the limits of normal wear and tear.

“When determining ordinary wear and tear, the court must look to the circumstances of each case.” Hensel v. Childress , 145 N.E.3d 1159, 2019 -Ohio-3934, ¶ 26 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., 2019).

The court decisions summarized below provide some guidance on th e scope of normal wear and tear.

531 Elsayed v. Miamisburg by the Mall , No. 28918, 2021 WL 2028661 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., May 21, 2021) Condition/repair: sealing concrete beneath carpet due to pet stain, flea treatments, and extra painting due to cat urine an d scratches. Court decision (affirms): each of these items constituted damage to the premises beyond normal wear and tear.

Tillimon v. Tate, No. L-18-1237, 2019 WL 3383890 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., July 12, 2019) Condition/repair: changing and re-keying the locks. Court decision (affirms): the cost for these items is part of the normal cost of doing business and do not constitute damage beyond normal wear and tear.

Wead v. Wade, No. 27204, 2017 WL 1806820 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., May 5, 2017) Condition/repair: dogs and cats urinated on the carpet, requiring replacement of the carpet and pad . Court decision ( reverses): the carpet was only two years old and in good condition at the start of the tenancy, and thus would not normally be replaced at the end of this one-month tenancy.

Tillimon v. Richardson-Long, No. L-16-1055, 2017 WL 169267 (Ohio App., 6th Dist.,
Jan. 13, 2017) Condition/repair: cleaned carpet and trash removal. Court decision: affirms denial of carpet cleaning cost, since noabnormal dirt or stains and need for carpet cleaning after two years was result of normal wear and tear; reverses denial of trash removal cost, since two truckloads of garbage does not constitute normal wear and tear.

Riggle v. McFadden, No. 08-CA-0007, 2008 WL 4766737 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Nov.

3, 2008) Condition/repair: cleaned the apartment and replaced the carpet due to extensive damage by cigarette smoke, including a pervasive odor, yellow stains on the walls and registers, and burn marks on the car pet. Court decision (affirms): these items constituted damage to the premises beyond normal wear and tear.

Schaedler v. Shinkle , No. CA99 -09-025, 2000 WL 1283776 (Ohio App., 12th Dist.,
Sept. 11, 2000) Condition/repair: cleaned trailor, shampooed carpet, and replaced torn paper window
shade. Court decision (affirms): condition of the trailor, including the carpet, did not constitute damage to the premises beyond normal wear and tear.

VanHoose v. McGee , No. CA97 -04-008, 1997 WL 598127 (Ohio App., 12th Dist .,
Sept. 29, 1997) Condition/repair: replaced toilet paper holder, shampooed carpet, and tested gas
fixtures. Court decision (affirms): only the need to replace the toilet paper holder constituted damage to the premises beyond normal wear and tear.

Fornof-Lippencott v. Powell, No. 94-CA-11, 1995 WL 19127 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist.,
Jan. 18, 1995) Condition/repair: (1) damage to the carpet, woodwork, and doors caused by the tenant’s dogs, (2) clogged drain in the dishwasher, (3) damaged toilet seat, and (4) some missing ductwork that served as a vent for the dryer in the basement. Court decision (reverses in part): damage to the carpet, woodwork, and doors constituted damage to the premises beyond normal wear and tear; damage to the dishwasher, toilet seat, and ductwork did not constitute damage to the premises beyond normal wear and tear.

Quinn v. Humphrey, No. 92-L-141, 1993 WL 262582 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., June 30, 1993) Condition/repair: trash throughout unit, filthy stove, hole in plaster wall and screen door, torn wallpaper, and broken water faucet. Court decision (reverses): each of these items constituted damage to the premises beyond normal wear and tear.
