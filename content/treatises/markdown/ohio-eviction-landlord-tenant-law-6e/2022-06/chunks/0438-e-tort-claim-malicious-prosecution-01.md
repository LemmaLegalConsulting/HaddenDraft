# E. Tort Claim, Malicious Prosecution

## Table-of-contents context

- XIV. Tenant Claims for Wrongful
  - E. Tort Claim, Malicious Prosecution

## Source text

Generally, the tort claim of malicious civil prosecution is no longer a viable claim for wrongful eviction due to the Supreme Court’s decision inRobb v. Chagrin Lagoons Yacht Club, Inc., 75 Ohio St.3d 264, 662 N.E.2d 9 (1996). In Robb, the Supreme Court clarified that an element of a malicious civil prosecution claim is seizure of the plaintiff’s person or property during the course of the prior proceeding. Id. at 269-70.

Since a judgment entered in favor of a tenant in an eviction action will prevent any seizure of the tenant’s property during the course of that proceeding, the tenant (potential plaintiff) generally cannot meet the seizure requirement for a malicious civil prosecution claim. E.g., Deli Table, Inc. v. Great Lakes Mall, No. 95-L-012, 1996 WL 761984, at *7 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Dec. 31, 1996) (applies Robb).

The Robb decision implicitly overrules the decision in Reenan v. Klein , 3 Ohio App.3d 142, 444 N.E.2d 63 (1st Dist. 1981), which upheld a malicious civil prosecution judgment in favor of a tenant without considering the seizure element. The basis for the judgment inReenan was four-fold: (1) the landlord, a recent purchaser of the property, knew of the valid lease executed by the prior owner and the tenant, (2) before filing the eviction action, the landlord had received the timely tendered rent check for the month for which the landlord alleged nonpayment of rent, (3) the landlord nonetheless filed the eviction action because he wanted possession of the prem ises, and, (4) in the eviction action, the court entered judgment for the tenant.
