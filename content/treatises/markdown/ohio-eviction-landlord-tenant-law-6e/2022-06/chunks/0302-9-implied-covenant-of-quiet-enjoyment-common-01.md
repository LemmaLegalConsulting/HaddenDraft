The landlord’s obligations under the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment are in addition to the R.C. Chapter 5321 obligations. Ayres v. Simmons , No. 93 -CA-827, 1993 WL 405489 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Sept. 30, 1993).

“The covenant [of quiet enjoyment] is breached when the landlord ‘... obstruct[s], interfere[s] with, or take[s] away from the ... [tenant] in a substantial degree the beneficial use ...’ of the leasehold.” Howard v. Simon, 18 Ohio App.3d 14, 16, 480 N.E.2d 99 (8th Dist. 1984) (quoting Frankel v. Steman, 92 Ohio St. 197, 200, 110 N.E.

747 (1915)). Accord Davis v. Sean M. Holley Agency, Inc. , No. 23891, 2010 WL 4311683 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Oct. 29, 2010);Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. “The degree of impairment required [to constitute a breach] is a question for the finder of fact.” Dworkin, 93 Ohio App.3d at 386. Accord Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Evans,

In Howard, the court held that “there was sufficient evidence for the finder of fact to determine that the frequent absence of air conditioning over a two -summer period breached the covenant.” 18 Ohio App. 3d at 16.

In Evans, the court upheld the trial court ’s breach of covenant finding, based on the owner’s placement of a lock box on the front door and grant of permission to prospective purchasers to enter the house unannounced, for a three-month period. 2010 WL 2334795, ¶¶ 12,25,31,54.

In Dworkin, the cour t held that material issues of fact existed as to whether the amount of cigarette smoke being transmitted into the tenant’s upper rental unit from a lower rental unit was sufficient to constitute a violation of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, and thus reversed a summary judgment for the landlord. 93 Ohio App.3d at 388.

In Liggins, the trial court found no breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, based on its finding that no witness at trial other than the tenant noticed an odor of marijuana in her apartment. 1998 WL 894613, at *4. The court of appeals held that the trial court did not abuse its discretion in making this finding and therefore upheld the finding. Id.

Also, if a landlord breaches the covenant of quiet enjoyment by failing to provide in the common area reasonable security measures against criminal activity, the landlord may be liable for the criminal activity of a third party. See Section VII.C.8, above.

Ohio law on the measure of damages for a breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment is somewhat unsettled. Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Evans, No. WD-09-012, 2010 WL 2334795, ¶ 52 (Ohio App., 6th Dist. June 11, 2010). The conflict is essentially between whether a breach of the covenant relieves the tenant of the obligation to pay any rent or reduces the tenant’s rental obligation to the rental value received (in light of the breach). Id., ¶¶ 52,53. “The latter approach is substantially [actually, precisely] the same as the measure for either a breach of duty set forth in a lease or a statutory duty.” Id., ¶ 53. For a discussion on that measure of damages, see Section IX.A.8.a, below.
