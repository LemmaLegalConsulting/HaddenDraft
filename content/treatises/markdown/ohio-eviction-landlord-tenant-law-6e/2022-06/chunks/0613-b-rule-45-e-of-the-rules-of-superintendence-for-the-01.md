## Source text

Courts of Ohio The records of an eviction action may be sealed under Rule 45(E) of the Rules of Superintendence for the Courts of Ohio (“Rule 45(E)”).

Under Rule 45(E), there is a presumption in favor of allowing public access to court records. In determining whether to seal the records of an eviction action, the court determines whether clear and convincing evidence of a higher public interest outweighs this presumption. Rule 45(E).

The court considers three factors for these purposes, namely: “(a) whether public policy is served by restricting public access; (b) whether any state, federal, or common law exempts the document or information from public access; (c) whether factors that support restriction of public access exist, including risk of injury to persons, individual privacy rights and interests, proprietary business information, public safety, and fairness of the adjudicatory process.” Rule 45(E).

Three court decisions in which the court granted a tenant’s Rule 45(E) motion to seal the records of an eviction action are: (1) Wayne Metro. Hous. Auth. v. L.F. , No.

CVG-12-02-0021 (Ohio Mun., Wayne Cty., Oct. 28, 2020) (where the landlord voluntarily dismissed the eviction action, motion granted because the records of the eviction action adversely affect ed the tenant with one potential landlord and two employers); (2) Evans v. B.S., No. 19-CVG-02190 (Ohio Mun., Akron, June 11, 2019) (where the eviction action was mistakenly filed against the tenant and then dismissed, motion granted because the records of the eviction action will adversely affect the tenant’s future ability to secure housing and public access to these records does not serve any lawful, meaningful public purpose); an d (3) Real Solutions Realty Grove City, LLC v. D.G. , No. 2018 -CVG-29089 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty, Feb. 7, 2019) (where the tenant vacated more than two months before the eviction action filing per a mutual rescission agreement, motion granted because the records of the eviction action adversely affect the tenant’s future ability to secure housing , are misleading and prejudicial to the tenant, and violate public policy by promulgating inaccurate information).

Also, in S.C. v. T.H. , 154 N.E.3d 345, 2020 -Ohio-2698 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., 2020), the trial court summarily denied the tenant’s Rule 45(E) motion to seal the records of the eviction action and, as a result, the court of appeals remanded the case for the trial court to explain its reasoning for denying the motion.
