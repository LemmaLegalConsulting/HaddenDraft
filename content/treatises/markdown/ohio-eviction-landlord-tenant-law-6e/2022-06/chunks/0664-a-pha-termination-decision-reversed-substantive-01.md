Grounds Austin v. Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth., No. 107247, 2019 WL 856776 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Feb. 21, 2019) PHA termination for failing to report timely (reported fifty days late) a change in household composition (move -out of two adult children). “At the time in q uestion, Austin was a single mother in the late stages of pregnancy, who then gave birth by cesarean section. She had two other minor children residing with her and two older children that she was trying to remove from the lease because they were not following household rules. Austin was in and out of the hospital and the birth of the baby left her medically incapacitated during the time CMHA alleged she failed to report the change in household circumstances.” Austin, ¶ 39. The trial court reversed the PHA termination decision on the ground that it was unreasonable, and the court of appeals affirmed the trial court judgment.

McClarty v. Greene Metro. Hous. Auth., No. 2011-CA-30, 2011 WL 3890295 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Sept. 2, 2011) PHA termination for failing to report child -support income. Court of appeals held fraud is a required element of this ground. It found participant did not commit fraud, because her failure to report the start of child -support income was an unintentional mistake – she thought that she or ODJFS had reported it.

Smith v. Hamilton County, Ohio , No. C-060315, 2007 WL 1095680 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Apr. 13, 2007) PHA termination for continuing to list sons as household members, after temporarily losing custody of them. Court of appeals held fraud is an element of this ground. It found participant did not commit fraud, because her continued listing of her sons as household members was an unintentional mistake – she thought that she was required to list them because of their visits.

Holman v. Cuyahoga Metro. Hous.Auth., No. 81475, 2002 WL 31771307 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Dec. 12, 2002) PHA termination for absence of HAP payments for six months, applying 24 C.F.R.

§ 982.455. Court reversed PHA decision because evidence showed HAP p ayments made, not absence of HAP payments, during the relevant six months.

Chalk v. Stark Metro. Hous. Auth., No. 2018-CV-00693 (Ohio C.P., Stark Cty., Jan.

31, 2019) PHA termination for unauthorized occupant. Court found that the PHA did not establish that the alleged unauthorized occupant stayed at the premises for more than fifteen consecutive days or for more than ninety cumulative days in a twelve -month
period. T herefore, under the relevant PHA definition, the PHA did not establish an unauthorized occupancy. In reaching its findings, the court found unpersuasive the PHA’s reliance on the delivery of mail to the premises to the alleged unauthorized occupant, given the participant’s testimony on her unsuccessful efforts to stop those deliveries.

667 Coleman v. Mansfield Metro. Hous. Auth., No. 2011-CV-1071H (Ohio C.P., Richland Cty., July 10, 2012) PHA termination for drug-related criminal activity. Court reversed the PHA decision on two grounds. First, the participant’s conduct– one-time use of marijuana – did not rise to the level of criminal activity because, under Ohio law, it could have led only to a minor misdemeanor charge, for which a conviction would not constitute a criminal
record. Second, the termination decision is unreasonable – which is an R.C. 2506.04 ground for reversing the decision – based on five mitigating factors: one -time drug- use; occurred off the premises; no evidence of ongoing drug -use; model tenant and neighbor; and termination would deny the family the ability to secure safe, reliable housing.

Johnson v. Akron Metro. Hous. Auth. , No. 2010 -08-5472 (Ohio C.P., Summit Cty.,
Feb. 23, 2011) PHA termination for unauthorized occupant. Court held residency means a permanent, fixed location with specific intent to stay, and not mere physical presence in the unit for any period of time during the day (as PHA argued). Even under PHA’s mere physical presence standard, PHA failed to prove violat ion of PHA’s guest limitation, four consecutive days or 15 days per 12 months.

Robertson v. Cuyahoga Metro. Hous.Auth., No. CV-07-635765 (Ohio C.P., Cuyahoga Cty., Aug. 31, 2010) PHA termination for listing children, who did not reside in the unit but vi sited, as household members. According to participant’s brief: she did not commit fraud; she listed her children as household members because the larger accommodation was required by the terms of her reunification plan with the County; and PHA knew or should have known of her children’s residency via recertification information. Court decision simply states that the PHA decision was illegal, arbitrary, and unsupported by the preponderance of the evidence.

Gooden v. Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth., No. A-0710779 (Ohio C.P., Hamilton Cty., July 25, 2008, and Aug. 29, 2008) PHA termination for unauthorized occupant. Court held PHA failed to meet its burden of proof. PHA’s evidence was unreliable and not credible hearsay– court documents in a criminal domestic violence proceeding. Participant presented competent, credible evidence that the guest did not reside there.

Hammond v. Cleveland Metro. Hous. Auth., No. CV-07-616036 (Ohio C.P., Cuyahoga Cty., June 26, 2008) PHA termination for unauthorized occupant and side rental payments. Court held the PHA decision was unsupported by a preponderance of substantial, reliable, and probative evidence. Evidence on unauthorized occupant claim: (1) “mailings consist of junk mail addressed to [ex -husband], and not impo rtant mail such as utility statements, bank statements, or credit card statements;” and (2) participant’s direct testimony that her ex-husband, and other men (uncles, brothers, boyfriend), spent time at the unit, but none stayed overnight. Evidence on sid e rental payments claim: (1) three months of rent receipts that do not show participant was responsible for making the payments; (2) evidence of hostility between former landlord and participant; and
(3) absence of testimony by former landlord.

Jones v. C incinnati Metro. Hous. Auth. , No. A -0709492 (Ohio C.P., Hamilton Cty.,
Apr. 15, 2008) PHA termination for violent criminal activity (robbery). Court held the PHA decision violated HUD regulations because, under 24 C.F.R. § 982.551(l), violent criminal activity is grounds to terminate participation only if it occurs in the immediate vicinity of the rental unit. Court found that 2/3 of a mile – distance between the unit and the site of the criminal activity – did not meet the immediate vicinity test.

May v. Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth., No. CV-07-632536 (Ohio C.P., Cuyahoga Cty.,
Nov. 2, 2007) PHA termination for listing children, who did not reside in the unit but visited, as household members. Court held the PHA decision was arbitrary, unreasonable and capricious, because PHA failed to demonstrate participant committed fraud and PHA failed to consider relevant mitigating circumstances.
