# 2. Writ of Restitution

## Source text

A writ of restitution instructs the bailiff’s (or sheriff’s) office to enforce an eviction judgment that has been entered in favor of the landlord. R.C. 1923.13. It instructs the bailiff’s office to remove the tenant’s person and property from the premises.78 Id.

A writ of restitution is not final and may not be issued until the judge signs it. 79 Del Manor v. Peterson, No. 93-P-0039, 1994 WL 45269 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Feb.

11, 1994). Therefore, a writ of restitution signed only by the clerk of courts may not be issued. Id.

The courts usually issue the writ of restitution on or shortly after the date on which they have filed the judgment for the landlord. However, a court may delay its issuance of the writ of restitution when advised that time is necessary to fi nd another dwelling unit, particularly if there are hardship factors related to elderly persons, persons with disabilities, or children in the household. 80 E.g., Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth. v.

Foster, No. 88-CV-05432 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., Aug. 31, 1988). See generally Zeid v. Brown , No. 12 -259, 1988 WL 59831, at *1 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., June 3, 1988).

The court may make enforcement of the writ contingent on the landlord’s payment 78 If necessary, a landlord may be granted a writ of mandamus to secure enforcement of the writ of restitution. State ex rel. Marsol Apt. Co. v. Vannuci , 68 Ohio App.2d 181, 428 N.E.2d 468 (8th Dist. 1980).

R.C. 1923.14 requires the bailiff’s office to execute the writ of restitution within ten days of receiving it. State ex rel. GMS Mgmt. Co., Inc. v. Wellington, No. 03-MA- 232, 2004 WL 47164, ¶ 6 (Ohio App., 7th Dist., Jan. 7, 2004). “There is no provision in the statute to allow the sheriff [or bailiff] to establish a grace period within which the tenant may have an opportunity to vacate the premises.” Id.

The tenant may not obtain a writ of prohibition to prevent enforcement of the writ of restitution. State ex rel. Cully v. Flanagan , 50 Ohio St.3d 180, 553 N.E.2d 655 (1990). There are two reasons for this conclusion: (1) the bailiff’s enforcement of a writ of restitution is n ot an exercise of judicial or quasi -judicial power; and (2) an appeal of the eviction judgment is an adequate remedy at law. Id.

When the bailiff removes the tenant’s property from the premises, generally, the bailiff places the property either outside by an adjacent curb or in storage. E.g., Riolo
v. Navin, No. 79809, 2002 WL 502408 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Apr. 4, 2002) (curb);

Castle Apts., Inc. v. Allgood, 43 Ohio App.3d 163, 539 N.E.2d 1154 (9th Dist. 1988) (curb); Ringler v. Sias, 68 Ohio App.2d 230, 428 N.E.2d 869 (10th Dist. 1980) (curb);

State ex rel. Marsol Apt. Co. v. Vannuci , 68 Ohio App.2d 181, 428 N.E.2d 468 (8th
Dist. 1980) (storage). The practice varies among jurisdictions.

After the bailiff has removed the property from the premises, the landlord does not have an affirmative duty to protect it, even if the property has been placed on the landlord’s land. Ringler v. Sias , 68 Ohio App.2d 230, 428 N.E.2d 869 (10th Dist.

1980).

If the property is placed in storage, the tenant will be required to pay the storage fee in order to recover the property. E.g., State ex rel. Marsol Apt. Co. v. Vannuci, 68 Ohio App.2d 181, 428 N.E.2d 468 (8th Dist. 1980).
