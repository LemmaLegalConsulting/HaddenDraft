When a Civil Rule 60(B) motion is filed after the execution of the writ of restitution, generally the application of Civil Rule 60(B) is not consistent with the summary nature of eviction proceedings. Larson v. Umoh , 33 Ohio App.3d 14, 17, 514 N.E.2d 145 (8th Dist. 1986). E.g., Consol. Mgmt., Inc. v. R.C.T., Inc., Nos. 2001- L-153,156, 2002 WL 31862650 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Dec. 20, 2002) (one and one- half years after judgment and tenant’s vacation, and one year after building had been razed).

The execution of the writ of restitution is not an absolute bar to a Civil Rule 60(B)
motion. Larson, 33 Ohio App.3d at 17. E.g., Mitchell v. Walker, No. C-050546 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Dec. 6, 2006) (reversed denial and remanded for hearing, where motion filed five months after tenant was “physically evicted”); Mt. Olive-Metro Hous., Inc.
v. Craft, No. 93-G-2858 (Ohio Mun., Cuyahoga Falls, Feb. 28, 1994).

But “the trial court should weigh such delay heavily against granting relief from an eviction judgment.” Larson, 33 Ohio App.3d at 17. Two of the factors that courts consider in determining whether to grant a Civil Rule 60(B) motion after the execution of the writ of restitution are the length of the delay and any justification for the delay.

Id.

As a condition for granting the tenant’s Civil Rule 60(B) motion, the court may require the tenant to deposit with the court all rent then due and owing and to deposit with the court, as it becomes due, the rent that becomes due pending the court’s decision on the motion. Deerwood Mgmt. Co. v. Bernsdorf, No. 78AP-470, 1978 Ohio
App. LEXIS 10021 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Nov. 28, 1978). The authority for imposing this rent deposit condition is the Civil Rule 60(B) provision that permits a court to relieve a party from a final judgment “upon such terms as are just.” Id.

When a rent deposit condition has been imposed and the tenant fails to deposit rent with the court as ordered, the tenant’s noncompliance with the rent deposit condition is a permissible ground for denying the Civil Rule 60(B) motion and thereupon issuing a writ of restitution. Id.

In an eviction action, one common reason for a tenant’s Civil Rule 60(B) motion is the tenant’s failure to appear at trial because of transportation problems.E.g., Urban Hollow Apts. v. Dunning, No. 93AP-830, 1994 WL 55522 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Feb.

22, 1994); Akron Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Mitchell , No. 91 -CVG-840 (Ohio Mun., Cuyahoga Falls, June 4, 1991).

The Dunning court found excusable neglect where the tenant failed to appear at trial because: (1) she did not own a car, (2) she had arranged to obtain a ride to the trial but that person failed to appear, (3) she made other transportation arrangements, (4) she called the court and left a message explaining why she was late and that she was on her way, and (5) she arrived one hour late.

The Mitchell court found that relief was appropriate under Civil Rule 60(B)(5) where the tenant failed to appear at trial because: (1) she did not own a car, (2) she had difficulty arranging transportation, (3) she was ten minutes late for the trial, (4) she sat in the courtroom to which she was directed and forty minutes later, when asked the reason for her presence, she first learned that the trial had proceeded in her absence.85 Another common reason for a tenant’s Civil Rule 60(B) motion is the tenant’s tender and the landlord’s acceptance of one or more future monthly rent payments after the entry of the eviction judgment and, thereafter, the landlord’s attempt to enforce the eviction judgment.

In these circumstances, the landlord’s acceptance of the future monthly rent payment(s) creates a new month -to-month tenancy between the landlo rd and tenant.
