# 11. Forbearance Agreement to Vacate, Breach

## Source text

The good cause grounds for terminating an assisted housing tenancy are limited to those set forth in the HUD regulations. Cmty. Props. of Ohio Mgmt. Servs. v. Taylor,
No. 2009-CVG-001131 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., May 11, 2009).

A tenant’s breach of a forbearance agreement, under which the landlord agrees not to file an eviction action and the tenant agrees to vacate the rental unit by a specified date, is not one of the permissible grounds for terminating an assisted housing tenancy under the HUD regulations. Id. Therefore, it is not a good cause ground to terminate an assisted housing tenancy. Id. See also Dakota County HRA v. Blackwell , 602 N.W.2d 243 (Minn. 1999). Cf. Hous. Auth. of Portland v. Asana, 165 Or. App. 531, 1 P.3d 1025 (2000) (stipulated judgment from earlier eviction for nonpayment of rent;

tenant complied with rent payment requirement, but allegedly breached requirement to work with intervention specialist; the breach is subject to the federal st atute’s good cause requirement; reversal and remand to consider whether the breach constituted good cause).

Because HUD approval is required for any lease amendment, the forbearance agreement may not be construed as an amendment to the lease, absent HUD approval.
Cmty. Props. of Ohio Mgmt. Servs. v. Taylor , No. 2009 -CVG-001131 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., May 11, 2009). Therefore, absent HUD approval, the breach of the forbearance agreement is not grounds to terminate the lease as a lease violation. Id.

In these circumstances, the landlord’s options are: (1) to bring an eviction action for the underlying breach of the rental agreement that prompted the forbearance agreement, or (2) to bring an action to enforce the forbearance agreement. Id.

Also, when an agreed judgment entry (in contrast to a forbearance agreement) requires a tenant to vacate the rental unit if certain conditions occur, the court may enforce its entry. Id.
