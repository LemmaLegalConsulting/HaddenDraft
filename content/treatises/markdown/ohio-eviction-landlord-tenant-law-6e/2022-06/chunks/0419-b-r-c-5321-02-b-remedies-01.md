# B. R.C. 5321.02(B) Remedies

## Source text

When a landlord violates the R.C. 5321.02(A) retaliation prohibition, under R.C.

5321.02(B), the tenant may bring an action against the landlord for actual damages and reasonable attorney’s fees.313 See also Laster v. Bowman , 52 Ohio App.2d 379, 370 N.E.2d 767 (8th Dist. 1977). But see Stuckey v. Vistula Mgmt. Co. , No. L-00-1257, 313 For city of Cleveland tenants, the tenant may recover minimum statutory damages, between $50 and $500, in lieu of the R.C. 5321.02(B) actual damages. Cleveland, OH Code, pt. 3C, tit.

IX, ch. 375, § 375.08. E.g., Leak v. Hale, No. 2006-CVG-33367 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous.

Div., Jan. 10, 2008) ($500 statutory minimum damage award).

2001 WL 477133 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., May 4, 2001).314 Where the retaliation is the commencement of an eviction action, the tenant may also assert the retaliation as a defense to the eviction claim. See Section II.T, above.

Where the retaliation is a rent increase, the tenant is not liable for the rent increase.

E.g., Bland v. Kovein, No. 16179, 1993 WL 473837 (Ohio App. , 9th Dist., Nov. 10, 1993).

When a landlord commences an eviction action in violation of R.C. 5321.02(A), the tenant may bring an R.C. 5321.02(B) damage claim against the landlord even if the court lacks jurisdiction to hear the eviction action. See Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth.
v. Watkins, 23 Ohio App.3d 20, 491 N.E.2d 701 (8th Dist. 1984) (occupancy under a land installment contract for which R.C. 5313.07 required a foreclosure action).

Actual damages under R.C. 5321.02(B) include, for example, the time and expense of looking for another dwelling unit, damage to credit, and the time and expense of defending an eviction action. E.g., Korb v. Doering, No. 91-CVG-4948 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Apr. 10, 1991) (damages awarded for lost wages).

One court decision holds that actual damages under R.C. 5321.02(B) include emotional distress damages suffered as a result of the retaliation. Gateway Apts. v.

Darrah, Nos. 76 -CVG-725,928 (Ohio Mun., Lyndhurst, Aug. 22, 1977). See also Simon v. Solomon, 385 Mass. 91, 431 N.E.2d 556 (1982) (construing Massachusetts Landlord and Tenant Act). But see Ficker v. Diefenbach, 34 Or. App. 241, 578 P.2d 467 (1978) (construing Oregon Landlord and Tenant Act). Irrespective of that holding, the tenant may have a co mmon law claim for intentional or negligent infliction of serious emotional distress. See Section XI.D, below.

Actual damages under R.C. 5321.02(B) do not include punitive damages. Davis
v. Reed, Nos. 68699,68700, 1996 WL 347814 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., J une 20, 1996).

However, the tenant has a common law claim for punitive damages, if the landlord’s conduct is malicious, intentional, and wanton. See Section XI.D, below.

Attorney’s fees may be awarded under R.C. 5321.02(B) only if the tenant has suffered actual damages. Jemo Assocs. v. Garman , 70 Ohio St.2d 267, 436 N.E.2d 314 The Stuckey decision is wrong. It erroneously holds that R.C. 5321.02 “permits a retaliation claim to be asserted only as a defense in an eviction action” and that “no independent cause of action exists” under R.C. 5321.02(B). 2001 WL 477133, at *3. The holding and the decision completely ignore the R.C. 5321.02(B) provision that expressly permits a tenant to “recover from the landlord actual damages together with reasonable attorneys’ fees.” 463 1353 (1982). See also Zamos v. Dunn , No. 2000 -P-0120, 2001 WL 1149844 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Sept. 28, 2001);Neff v. Manashian, 104 Ohio App.3d 528, 662 N.E.2d 856 (8th Dist. 1995) (dicta); Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Watkins, 23 Ohio App.3d 20, 491 N.E.2d 701 (8th Dist. 1984).

Where appropriate, the tenant also may seek injunctive relief to enjoin the threat or continuation of the unlawful retaliation. R.C. 5321.02 does not expressly authorize injunctive relief. This contrasts with other sections of R.C. Chapter 5321 that do expressly authorize injunctive relief (i.e., R.C. 5321.04(B) and 5321.05(C)).

However, the remedies provided tenants in R.C. Chapter 5321 are intende d to supplement other remedial measures. Shroades v. Rental Homes, Inc., 68 Ohio St.2d 20, 427 N.E.2d 774 (1981). Other remedial measures include the authority to grant injunctive relief that is conferred upon courts by R.C. 2727.03 (courts of common
pleas) and 1901.18 (municipal courts). Courts have the right and the responsibility to exercise that authority in any appropriate case. Ex parte Cattell, 146 Ohio St. 112, 64 N.E.2d 416 (1945).
