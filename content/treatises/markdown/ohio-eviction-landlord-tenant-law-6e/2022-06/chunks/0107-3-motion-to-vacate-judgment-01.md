## Source text

A judgment rendered by a court lacking subject -matter jurisdiction or personal jurisdiction is void ab initio. State ex rel. Ballard v. O’Donnell, 50 Ohio St.3d 182, 183, 553 N.E.2d 650 (1990) (personal jurisdiction); Patton v. Diemer, 35 Ohio St.3d 68, 70, 518 N.E.2d 941 (1988) (subject-matter jurisdiction). “[T]he authority to vacate a void judgment is not derived from Civ.R. 60(B), but rather constitutes an inherent power possessed by Ohio courts.” Patton v. Diemer, 35 Ohio St.3d 68, 70, 518 N.E.2d 941 (1988).

Therefore, a motion to vacate the eviction judgment is the appropriate remedy when the court lacked personal jurisdiction over the tenant because of the court’s failure to serve the summons and complaint in conformity with the applicable law. 86 G.K.G. Builders, Inc. v. Burgess, 13 N.E.2d 745, 2014-Ohio-2431, ¶ 7 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., June 6, 2014). E.g., Vistula Mgmt. Co. v. Stuckey , No. L -92-102, 1993 WL 315626 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Aug. 13, 1993). Cf. Corley v. Sullivan -Busman, No.

99420, 2013 WL 4976381, ¶ 15 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Sept. 23, 2013) (“Where a defendant seeking a motion to vacate (a void judgment) makes an uncontradicted sworn statement that he never received service of the complaint, he is entitled to have the judgement against [him] vacated”).

Likewise, a motion to vacate the eviction judgment is the appropriate remedy when the court lacked subject -matter jurisdiction because of the landlord’s failure to serve properly an R.C. 1923.04 notice to vacate (see Section I.C.1.b, above) . Eureka Multifamily Group v. Terrell, No. L-14-1152, 2015 WL 2329168, ¶ 18 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., May 15, 2015). See also MLR Props., Inc. v. Hemmelgarn, No. L-94-349, 1995 WL 504629 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Aug. 25, 1995);Oda v. Davis, No. 13985, 1994 WL 444758 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Aug. 17, 1994).
