The fourth circumstance is when the issue on appeal is capable of repetition yet evades review. T&R Props., Inc. v. Wimberly , 158 N.E.3d 137, 2020 -Ohio-4279, ¶¶ 10-13 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., 2020); Millennia Hous. Mgmt., Ltd. v. Withrow, No.

Lattimore, 166 Ohio App.3d 12, 848 N.E.2d 912, ¶¶ 12 -14 (1st Dist. 2006); Showe
Mgmt. Corp. v. Hazelbaker, No. CA2006-01-004, 2006 WL 3479856, ¶ 7 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Dec. 4, 2006); Hocking Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Martin , No. 1998 WL 100416, at *2 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Mar. 5, 1998).

Two critical questions arise in connection with this mootness exception. The first is whether , in accordance with this exception’s requirements, there i s a reasonable expectation that the tenant will be subject to the same action again . See Schwab v.

Lattimore, 166 Ohio App.3d 12, 848 N.E.2d 912, ¶ 13 (1st Dist. 2006). The second is whether the eviction proceeding fails to meet the “evades review” requirement because of the opportunity to secure a supersedeas bond and stay (see Section I.Q.3, above).

Some court decisions have held that th e “evades review” requirement is not met because of the bond and stay provisions. E.g., Landings at Beckett Ridge v. Holmes,
No. CA2020-04-050, 2020 WL 7690283, ¶ 17 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Dec. 28, 2020);

AKP Props., LLC v. Rutledge , No. 2018 -CA-00058, 2018 WL 6822573, ¶ 19 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Dec. 21, 2018);Rithy Props., Inc. v. Cheeseman, 63 N.E.3d 752, 2016- Ohio-1602, ¶¶ 22,23 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., 2016).

Other court decisions have rejected this position on two alternative grounds. One is that “[t]enants faced with eviction due to failure to pay their rent will be unlikely to have the means to post a bond to obtain a stay of execution.” T&R Props., Inc. v.

Wimberly, 158 N.E.3d 137, 2020-Ohio-4279, ¶ 12 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., 2020). See also Olympic Realty v. Zaleski, No. 11AP-668 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., June 29, 2012) (memorandum decision on applic ation for reconsideration) . The second is that “the natural term of a tenant’s lease may expire during the pendency of an appeal, requiring the tenant to vacate the premises and allowing the landlord to assert the appeal is moot.” T&R Props., Inc. v. Wimberly, 158 N.E.3d 137, 2020-Ohio-4279, ¶ 12 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., 2020).

The Wimberly court found the exception applicable. It found the requisite capable of repetition based on the reasonable expectation that low -income tenants may be subject to an FED action again (and thus subject to the challenged affidavit practice again).

The Hazelbaker court, in holding the exception applicable, did not rely on (or
reference) any expectation that the complaining tenant might be subject in the future to the issue on appeal. Rather, it seemed to rely solely on its finding that the issue is “capable of repetition, yet will continue to evade review,” in other cases with other
litigants. Hazelbaker, 2006 WL 3479856, ¶ 7. The issue was whether, during the period that a first eviction judgment for a landlord is stayed pending an appeal, the landlord may file a second eviction action against the same tenant on new grounds, which arose after the filing of the first eviction action.

The Martin court, in holding the exception applicable, likewise relied solely on its finding that the issue on appeal is capa ble of repetition, yet will continue to evade review, in other cases with other litigants. Martin, 1998 WL 100416, at *2. The issue concerned rules governing the tenant’s right to a jury trial in an eviction action.

The Withrow court found the exception inapplicable because the tenant “ha[d] not shown that a reasonable likelihood exists that she will be subject to the same action again, i.e., having her lease terminated due to the presence of unauthorized persons.” Withrow, 2013 WL 398944, ¶ 11 The Schwab majority found the exception inapplicable because the tenant had not shown a reasonable expectation that the tenant would be subject to the same action again – a trial court’s failure to consider her counterclaim under R.C. 1923.061(B).

The Schwab dissent concluded that this factor is not an element of the exception and that the exception applies “when a situation is likely to occur repeatedly, albeit with different litigants.” Schwab, 166 Ohio App.3d at 18.

The fifth circumstance is when “there are other persons similarly situated who would benefit from the resolution of the issue on appeal.” Sandefur Mgmt. Co. v.
