## Source text

The notice to vacate must “notify the adverse party to leave the premises.” R.C.

1923.04(A).

R.C. 1923.04(A) prescribes certain language10 for so notifying the tenant, when a landlord seeks to recover res idential premises. 11 The notice to vacate may not paraphrase the statutory language. Mularcik v. Adams , No. 03 -JE-17, 2004 WL 549489 (Ohio App., 7th Dist., Mar. 18, 2004). Rather, it must include the statutory language verbatim. Id.

The notice to vacate must contain the statutory language in a conspicuous manner.

This “conspicuous manner” requirement is not defined in R.C. 1923.04(A).

Two appellate districts have relied on the Uniform Commercial Code (R.C.

10 The language required by R.C. 1923.04(A) is: “You are being asked to leave the premises.

If you do not leave, an eviction action may be initiated against you. If you are in doubt regarding your legal rights and obligations as a tenant, it is recommended that you seek legal assistance.” R.C. 1923.04(A).

11 If the prospective FED plaintiff is not seeking to recover residential premises, R.C.

1923.04(A) does not require any particular language for notifying the adverse party to leave.

For example, “[a]lthough the words ‘leave the premises’ appear in R.C. 1923.04(A), the General Assembly has not indicated that these are the only words that a comme rcial landlord can use to instruct the tenant to vacate the property; rather, R.C. 1923.04 merely requires that a landlord ‘notify the adverse party to leave the premises ***.’”Maggiore v. Kovach, 101 Ohio St.3d 184, 803 N.E.2d 790, ¶ 26 (2004).

15 1301.01(J)) definition of “conspicuous,” namely: “a term or clause ... written [so] that a reasonable person against whom it is to operate ought to have noticed it.” Chinnock
v. Kokinda, No. 10-CA-009863, 2011 WL 860417, ¶ 6 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Mar. 14, 2011); Greene Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Manning, No. 98-CA-55, 1999 WL 76456, at *3 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 19, 1999); Adm’r of Veterans Affairs v. Jackson, 41 Ohio App.3d 274, 276, 535 N.E.2d 369 (9th Dist. 1987). See also Dayton Metro. Hous.
Auth. v. Russell, 16 Ohio Op.3d 94 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., 1980).

Court decisions provide additional guidance for determining whether a notice to vacate contains the statutory language in a conspicuous manner.

Where the entire notice to vacate, including the statutory language, is in the same type, color, and font, the statutory language is not conspicuous. Chinnock v. Kokinda,
No. 10-CA-009863, 2011 WL 860417 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Mar. 14, 2011); Dayton
Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Russell, 16 Ohio Op.3d 94 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., 1980);Cherry Lee Apts. v. Walcott, No. 03-CVG-00510 (Ohio Mun., Licking Cty., Apr. 25, 2003).

Where the entire notice to vacate, including the statutory language, is in upper case, generally the statutory language is not conspicuous. Sahara Apts. v. Brown -Austin,
No. CA-9028, 1993 WL 221253 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., June 7, 1993); Owner’s Mgmt.
Co. v. Willis, No. WD-87-59, 1988 WL 13214 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Feb. 12, 1988) (dicta); Queen v. Blakeman , No. 07 -CVG-00808 (Ohio Mun., Gallipolis, Dec. 13, 2007); Neighbors Organized for Action in Hous. v. Smith, No. 91-CVG-11983 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., July 26, 1991),aff’d on other grounds, No. 62219, 1992 WL 25305 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Feb. 13, 1992); Cody v. Bettinger , No. 96 -CVG- 00965 (Ohio Mun., Marietta, Jan. 3, 1997).

Where the entire notice to vacate, including the statutory language, is in upper case type, but the statutory language is set off from the body of the notice by two lines of asterisks, the statutory language is conspicuous. Owner’s Mgmt. Co. v. Willis , No.

WD-87-59, 1988 WL 13214 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Feb. 12, 1988). See also Dayton
Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Westfall, No. 79-CVG-7932 (Ohio Mun., Dayton, Oct. 15, 1979) (statutory language enclosed in bright border and starred).

Where the statutory language is in smaller print than the other print on the notice, but is in bold faced print and is framed with red lines forming a rectangle around it and is placed in the corner of the notice, the statutory language is conspicuous. Cf.

Shewman v. Coons , No. 89 -CA-46, 1990 WL 34814, at *5 -7 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist.,
Mar. 29, 1990) (construing the R.C. 1923.06(B) provision that requires the summons in an eviction action to include certain statutory language in a conspicuous manner).

Where the statutory language is in upper case, a nd the remainder of the notice is in “letter form” upper and lower case, generally the statutory language is conspicuous.

Sahara Apts. v. Brown-Austin, No. CA-9028, 1993 WL 221253 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., June 7, 1993); Adm’r of Veterans Affairs v. Jackson, 41 Ohio App.3d 274, 535 N.E.2d 369 (9th Dist. 1987).

Where the statutory language is in upper case, and the remainder of the notice is in “letter form,” but the statutory language is not set off by a border and is in smaller print than and is in the samecolor as the remainder of the notice, the statutory language is not conspicuous. Detroit Hous. Ltd. P’ship v. Spurlock, No. 03-CVG-08908 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Sept. 23, 2003).

Where the statutory language is in upper case, and the remainder of the notice is a mix of “letter form” and all upper case, the statutory language is not conspicuous.

Scioto Woods Apts. v. Ferko , No. 08 -CVG-1860 (Ohio Mun., Chillicothe, Oct. 21, 2008).

A notice that meets the “conspicuous manner” requirement “for sighted persons is not adequate to provide notice to an individual known to be sight -impaired.” Greene
Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Manning , No. 98 -CA-55, 1999 WL 76456, at *3 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 19, 1999). In these circumstances, any statutory language in a written notice is not conspicuous, if the notice is simply posted on the tenant’s door. “A landlord, knowing that a tenant isblind, cannot claim that the tenant has been properly served with statutory notice when she cannot even see the notice posted on her door.” Id.
