A fee paid to a landlord to hold a dwelling unit for a prospective tenant until a subsequent specified date, on which the tenant’s rental agreement (if any) wi ll commence, is not a security deposit. Kimmie v. Tillman , 66 Ohio App.3d 478, 585 N.E.2d 559 (6th Dist. 1990); Spurgeon v. Sanders, No. 1362, 1988 WL 35794 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Mar. 28, 1988); Walker v. Redd , No. 91 -CVG-15960 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Jan. 28, 1992).

A deposit designated in a rental application as a “full security deposit on the above premises pending execution of a lease agreement” was not a security deposit, where the prospective tenant elected not to sign the lease. Turetsky v. Miller, No. CA96-03- 005, 1996 WL 468758 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Aug. 19, 1996). As a result, the owner was not entitled to retain the deposit. Id.

“Earnest money” paid to a landlord is not a security deposit. Hartman v. Garden Woods Apts., No. 15228, 1995 WL 628021 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Oct. 25, 1995). In this context, earnest money means a payment to the landlord that is: (1) refundable if the landlord rejects the tenant’s application, (2) forfeited as liquidated damages if the landlord approves the tenant’s application and the tenant refuses to execute a lease for the premises, and (3) converted to a security deposit if the landlord approves the tenant’s application and the tenant executes a lease for the premises. Id. See also Gough v. 1031 Props., LLC, No. CA2014-09-196, 2015 WL 4207115 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., July 13, 2015).

These liquidated damage provisions are enforceable only if the amoun t of the deposit bears a reasonable relationship to the landlord’s actual damages. Robertson v.

Court decisions upholding the forfeiture of the deposit include: Hartman v.

1995) ($280 deposit – one-half of one month’s rent); Williams v. Mark IV Apts. , No.

Court decisions rejecting the forfeiture of the deposit include: Robertson v.

Rossing, No. CA98 -02-035, 1999 WL 59698, at *3 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Feb. 8,
1999) (“the $200 amount bears no reasonable relation to actual damage suffered by appellee [landlord]”); Supinski v. Gold Coast Props. , No. 95-CVI-3026 (Ohio Mun., Lakewood, July 15, 1996) (prospective tenant withdrew application one day after signing it and before landlord had accepted it, and landlord did not suffer any damage as a result of prospective tenant’s actions).
