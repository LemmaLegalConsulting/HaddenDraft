## Source text

For LIHTC projects with post -1989 tax credit allocations, gross rent for a low - income unit may not exceed 30% of the initial income limit for the unit, b ased on an assumed household size of 1.5 persons per bedroom (or one person for an efficiency).

26 U.S.C. § 42(g). For example, the gross rent for a two-bedroom unit may not exceed 30% of the initial income limit for a three-person household.

For LIHTC p rojects with pre -1990 tax credit allocations, gross rent for a low - income unit may not exceed 30% of the initial income limit for the unit, based on the size of the actual household that occupies the unit.

All utilities, except telephone, cable television, and internet services, are included in the gross rent restriction. 26 C.F.R. § 1.42-10. If a tenant is required to pay for one of the utilities, either directly to the utility supplier or to the landlord under a submeter system, the tenant’s rent is red uced by the amount of the applicable utility allowance(s). 26 C.F.R. § 1.42 -10; Internal Revenue Service, Notice 2009 -44 (application of § 1.42 -10 to submetered utilities) (available at www.irs.gov/pub/irs - drop/n-09-44.pdf).

The IRS regulations – 26 C.F.R. § 1.42 -10 – permit several alternative methods for determining a development’s utility allowance(s). For Ohio LIHTC properties, OHFA will accept seven of those alternative methods: HUD’s utility allowance, for the properties it regulates; Rural Housing Service’s utility allowance, for the properties it assists; the relevant PHA’s utility allowance; the HUD utility schedule model; utility company estimates; and the OHFA consumption utility allowance model. OHFA, Utility Allowance Policies and Procedures (eff. Jan. 11, 2010, rev. Apr. 18, 2011).
