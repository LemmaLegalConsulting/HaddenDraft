A lease with an option to purchase is not a land installment con tract. R.C.

5313.01(A). See Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Watkins , 23 Ohio App.3d 20, 23, 491 N.E.2d 701 (8th Dist. 1984) (“the statutory definition of land installment contract expressly excludes option contracts for the purchase of real property”).

“To determine whether an agreement is a land installment contract or a lease with an option to purchase, a court must analyze the intent of the parties at the time they executed the agreement.” Judson v. Lyendecker, No. 12AP-615, 2013 WL 1187618, ¶ 12 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Mar. 21, 2013). Accord Shrock v. Spognardi, 46 N.E.3d 1115, 2015 -Ohio-4555, ¶ 19 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., 2015); Am. Servicing Corp. v.

Wannemacher, 19 N.E.3d 566, 2014 -Ohio-3984, ¶ 16 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., 2014);

Vorhees v. Jovingo, Nos. 04-CA-16,17,18, 2005 WL 2292796, ¶ 24 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Sept. 19, 2005); Fadelsak v. Hagley, No. 02-CA-41, 2003 WL 21489613, ¶ 10 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., June 25, 2003).

When the language of a written contract is unambiguous, a court is requi red to determine the intent of the parties from the plain language of the contract. Judson v.

Lyendecker, No. 12AP-615, 2013 WL 1187618, ¶ 12 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Mar. 21, 2013). See also Am. Servicing Corp. v. Wannemacher , 19 N.E.3d 566, 2014 -Ohio- 3984, ¶¶ 25,26,31 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., 2014). When the language of a written contract is ambiguous, “a court may consider extrinsic evidence to ascertain the parties’ intent.” Judson v. Lyendecker, No. 12AP-615, 2013 WL 1187618, ¶ 12 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Mar. 21, 2013).

If the plain language of the contract establishes that the three elements of the R.C.

5313.01(A) definition are met, the contract is a land installment contract. Am.

Servicing Corp. v. Wannemacher , 19 N.E.3d 566, 2014 -Ohio-3984, ¶¶ 28 -30 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., 2014). If the plain language of the contract establishes that one of these elements is not met, the contract is not a land installment contract. Lovejoy v.

Diel, No. 2020CA-06-067, 2021 WL 1246295, ¶¶ 23-26 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Apr.

5, 2021) (contract did not contain any of the three elements of the R.C. 5313.01(A) definition); Judson v. Lyendecker, No. 12AP-615, 2013 WL 1187618, ¶ 12 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Mar. 21, 2013) (contract lacked requirement to purchase).

In this de termination, the title of the contract is not dispositive. Am. Servicing
Corp. v. Wannemacher, 19 N.E.3d 566, 2014-Ohio-3984, ¶ 27 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., 633 2014). “The fact that an agreement is titled ‘Option to Purchase,’ however, does not necessarily make it so.” Anderson v. Ballard, No. L-10-1007, 2010 WL 3292685, ¶42 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Aug. 20, 2010).

The determination is based primarily on the language employed in the body of the contract, not its title. See, e.g. Am. Servicing Corp. v. Wannemacher, 19 N.E.3d 566, 2014-Ohio-3984, ¶¶ 27,28 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., 2014) (contract titled “Lease Agreement with Option to Purchase;” based on body of contract, it is land installment contract); Fadelsak v. Hagley, No. 02-CA-41, 2003 WL 21489613, ¶ 12 (Ohi o App., 4th Dist., June 25, 2003) (same).

A split among the appellate districts exists on when extrinsic evidence of the intent of the parties may be considered in these determinations. “[S]ome courts apply a six- factor test supplied by the Fourth Distric t to ascertain the intent of the parties” in all
cases. Am. Servicing Corp. v. Wannemacher , 19 N.E.3d 566, 2014 -Ohio-3984, ¶ 25 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., 2014).

Those courts hold that, “[i]n determining intent, the court should consider factors such as: (1) the characterization of the document; (2) the lessee’s rights at the end of the lease term; (3) the application of rent to the purchase price; (4) the responsibility for payments for repairs, utilities, and taxes; (5) the nonexistence of a financing statement; and (6) whether an option to purchase existed. If necessary, the court may also consider the factual circumstances surrounding the parties’ agreement.”Fadelsak
v. Hagley, No. 02 -CA-41, 2003 WL 21489613, ¶ 10 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., June 25,
2003) (citations omitted). Accord; Vorhees v. Jovingo , Nos. 04 -CA-16,17,18, 2005 WL 2292796, ¶ 24 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Sept. 19, 2005).

The Third District, inAm. Servicing Corp. v. Wannemacher, 19 N.E.3d 566, 2014- Ohio-3984, ¶¶ 25,31 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., 2014), expressly rejected the Fourth District’s approach. With its statement of contract law and analysis based solely on the unambiguous language of the contract, the Tenth District, inJudson v. Lyendecker,
No. 12AP -615, 2013 WL 1187618, ¶ 9 (Ohio App. , 10th Dist., Mar. 21, 2013), implicitly rejected the Fourth District’s approach. Likewise, the Eighth District, in A & J Homes, Inc. v. Green , No. 101416, 2015 WL 1511020, ¶¶ 10,15,16 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Apr. 2, 2015) (follows Judson), implicitly reje cted the Fourth District’s approach.
