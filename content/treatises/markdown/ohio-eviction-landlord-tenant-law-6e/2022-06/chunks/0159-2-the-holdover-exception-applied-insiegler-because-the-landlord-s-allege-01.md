The holdover exception should not apply to month -to-month tenancies when the alleged retaliatory act is the service of the R.C. 5321.17(B) notice of termin ation (see Section I.D, above). But the appellate districts are split on this issue.

Three appellate districts have held that the holdover exception does not apply i n these circumstances: Ruble v. M & L Props., Ltd. , No. 10 -COA-006, 2010 WL 5452110 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Dec. 20, 2010); Reid v. Plainsboro Partners, III, Nos.

09AP-332,446, 2010 WL 3610931 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Sept. 16, 2010); Voyager
Vill. Ltd. v. W illiams, 3 Ohio App.3d 288, 444 N.E.2d 1337 (2nd Dist. 1982) (construing R.C. 3733.091(A)(3) (now R.C. 4781.37(A)(3)), the manufactured home park holdover provision that is essentially the same as R.C. 5321.03(A)(4)). See also Tolle v. Cross , No. CVG -1400181 (Ohio Mun., New Philadelphia, Dec. 12, 2014) (follows Ruble).

One appellate district has held that the holdover exception does apply in these circumstances: Associated Estates Realty Corp. v. Samsa , No. 84297, 2004 WL 2847838 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Dec. 9, 2004).

In determining whether Ruble, Reid, and Voyager Vill. are correctly decided, or Samsa is correctly decided, a court is obligated to consider which decision(s) better advance the object sought to be obtained by the R.C. 5321.02,.03 prohibition a gainst
retaliation. See R.C. 1.49(A). In making this determination, a court is also obligated to consider the consequences of the statutory construction in each of these decisions (see R.C. 1.49(E)) and the presumption that the legislature intended each provision in R.C. 5321.02 to be effective (see R.C. 1.47(B)).140 Under these criteria, Ruble, Reid, and Voyager Vill. are correctly decided because they, and not Samsa, advance the object sought to be obtained by the R.C. 5321.02,.03 prohibition against retaliation. The Samsa decision is also flawed because it renders meaningless the R.C. 5321.02(B) retaliation defense.

The purpose of the R.C. 5321.02,.03 prohibition a gainst a landlord’s retaliatory termination of a tenancy and the related eviction action (“purpose of the retaliation prohibition”) is as follows:

In order to protect the tenant in the exercise of his new statutory rights and to promote reporting of housin g code violations, the new Act restricts the landlord’s absolute right to terminate a periodic tenancy.

Traditionally, some landlords would respond to a complaining tenant by terminating the lease. The effect of termination was to silence that particular tenant while setting an example for other tenants. Since retaliatory eviction on the part of the landlord has the potential to undermine the entire Landlord -Tenant Act, the legislature has prohibited such conduct.

Robert J. Croyle, Comment, Ohio Residential Landlord-Tenant Act, 8 Akron L. Rev.

519, 538-39 (1975). See also John E. Campion, Note, The Ohio Landlord and Tenant Reform Act of 1974, 25 Case W. Res. L. Rev. 876, 929-30 (1974-75).

The Ruble, Reid, and Voyager Vill. decisions correctly recognize that the purpose of the retaliation prohibition can be attained only if the R.C. 5321.02(A) prohibition encompasses a landlord’s retaliatory service of an R.C. 5321.17(B) notice of
termination. In retaliatory eviction actions against month -to-month tenant s, the landlord invariably claims a right to possession under the landlord’s service of an R.C.

5321.17(B) notice of termination. If R.C. 5321.02(A) does not encompass the landlord’s retaliatory motive for serving the R.C. 5321.17(B) notice of termination, the landlord’s eviction claim will never constitute a violation of R.C. 5321.02(A), notwithstanding the landlord’s retaliatory motive.

As noted in Voyager Vill. , if the holdover exception were applicable to the landlord’s service of the R.C. 5321.17(B) notice of termination, it would “vitiate the protection afforded by the defense of retaliation as regards any periodic tenant, since it could be reasoned that a periodic tenant becomes a holdover after the landlord declares the tenancy terminated or decline s to renew.” Voyager Vill., 3 Ohio App.3d at 295. Accord John E. Campion, Note, The Ohio Landlord and Tenant Reform Act of 1974 , 25 Case W. Res. L. Rev. 876, 941 (1974 -75). “Such an interpretation is circular, it makes a nullity of the retaliation defen se because the label of ‘holdover’ summarily disposes of the entire issue, i.e., whether the landlord had a right to evict in the first instance.” Id.

Furthermore, if the holdover exception were applicable to the landlord’s service of 167 the R.C. 5321.17(B) notice of termination, it would also directly conflict with the R.C.

1.47(B) presumption that “the entire statute is intended to be effective.” Since the R.C.

5321.02 retaliation defense would be unavailable to both month-to-month tenants and tenants with a fixed-term lease ( i.e., virtually all tenants), this construction of R.C.

5321.03(A)(4) would render meaningless the R.C. 5321.02 retaliation defense.

Ruble, Reid, and Voyager Vill. recognize that, relative to month -to-month tenancies, applying the hol dover exception only to cases in which the landlord’s retaliatory act occurred after the tenant became a holdover tenant, as in Siegler, is consistent with the purpose of the retaliation prohibition. In those cases, the landlord’s retaliatory act did not cause the tenant to be without a possessory right. “Where the tenant no longer has possessory rights to protect he should not be protected from eviction.” Voyager Vill. Ltd. v. Williams, 3 Ohio App.3d 288, 295, 444 N.E.2d 1337 (2nd Dist. 1982). Accord John E. Campion, Note, The Ohio Landlord and Tenant Reform Act of 1974, 25 Case W. Res. L. Rev. 876, 941 (1974-75).
