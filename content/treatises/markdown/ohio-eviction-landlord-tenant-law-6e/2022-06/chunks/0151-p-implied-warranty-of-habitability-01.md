# P. Implied Warranty of Habitability,

## Source text

Common Law The early common law in Ohio, as in other jurisdictions, did not recognize an implied covenant to make repa irs in residential leases. Holder v. Farmakis, 66 Ohio Law Abs. 279, 117 N.E.2d 491 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., 1951).

Ohio courts uniformly adhered to that position until a small number of courts, between 1972 and 1975, recognized a common law implied warranty of habitability in residential leases. E.g., Wymer v. Kimbaugh, No. 74AP-547, 1975 Ohio App. LEXIS 7359 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Mar. 13, 1975); Smith v. Lewis, Nos. B93544-47 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Sept. 30, 1974); Lable & Co. v. Brooks, Nos. B-89207,89208 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Aug. 16, 1974); Glyco v. Schultz, 35 Ohio Misc. 25, 289 N.E.2d 919 (Ohio Mun., Sylvania, 1972).

The Ohio Landlord-Tenant Act of 1974 (see Section V.A, below) did not explicitly address the status of this common law warranty. One court held that a warranty of habitability exists only insofar as the Act so provides. Laster v. Bowman , 52 Ohio App.2d 379, 370 N.E.2d 767 (8th Dist. 1977). It relied solely on its conclusion that the Act provides the exclusive remedies as to the right s and obligations contained in the Act. Id.

Thereafter, the Ohio Supreme Court overruled this conclusion of the Laster
decision. Shroades v. Rental Homes, Inc. , 68 Ohio St.2d 20, 25, 427 N.E.2d 774 (1981). It held that “the new remedies given tenants inR.C. Chapter 5321 are intended to be preventive and supplemental to other remedial measures.” Id. Under Shroades, the common law may supplement the R.C. 1923.061(B) remedy.

However, a tenant still must establish that, under Ohio common law, a warrantyof habitability exists. This may require the tenant to prove that the legislature, in enacting R.C. 1923.061(B), failed to redress adequately the deficiencies of the early common law and, for that reason, the courts should establish a common law warranty of habitability defense that is more expansive than R.C. 1923.061(B). It will, in all likelihood, be a difficult position on which to prevail. But a reasonable argument for this warranty defense can be made, based on a comprehensive analysis of the impl ied warranty of habitability theory and its reliance on contract law.

When the R.C. 1923.061(B) defense is available to a tenant, a common law warranty of habitability will afford the tenant no additional rights. Accordingly, the prudent approach for a te nant would be not to rely upon a common law warranty of habitability defense in lieu of R.C. 1923.061(B), but rather to consider using it only if R.C. 1923.061(B) is unavailable.
