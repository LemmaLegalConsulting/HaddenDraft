# B. Authority Under Common Law

## Source text

The general rule in Ohio is t hat, absent statutory authorization, a prevailing party may not recover attorney’s fees. Wilborn v. Bank One Corp., 121 Ohio St.3d 546, 906 N.E.2d 396, ¶ 7 (2009);Sorin v. Bd. of Educ., Etc., 46 Ohio St.2d 177, 179, 347 N.E.2d 527 (1976); State ex rel. Grosser v. Boy , 46 Ohio St.2d 184, 185, 347 N.E.2d 539 (1976). See generally Alyeska Pipeline Serv. Co. v. Wilderness Soc’y , 421 U.S. 240
(1975) (discussion of American rule on attorney’s fees); Fleischman Distilling Corp.
v. Maier Brewing Co., 386 U.S. 714 (1967) (same).

However, there are numerous exceptions to this general rule.

543 One exception is that attorney’s fees are allowed when the defendant acted “in bad faith, vexatiously, wantonly, or for oppressive reasons.”Sorin v. Bd. of Educ., Etc., 46 Ohio St.2d 177, 181, 347 N.E.2d 527 (1976). Accord Wilborn v. Bank One Corp., 121 Ohio St.3d 546, 906 N.E.2d 396, ¶ 7 (2009);Taylor v. McCullough-Hyde Mem’l Hosp., 116 Ohio App.3d 595, 600, 688 N.E.2d 1078 (12th Dist. 1996); Sutherland v.

Nationwide Gen. Ins. Co. , 102 Ohio App.3d 297, 301, 657 N.E.2d 281 (10th Dist.

1995); Sladoje v. Slettebak , 44 Ohio App.3d 206, 207, 542 N.E.2d 701 (10th Dist.

1988).

A second exception is that attorney’s fees are allowed when punitive damages are
awarded. Villella v. Waikem Motors, Inc. , 45 Ohio St.3d 36, 41, 543 N.E.2d 464 (1989); Columbus Fins. Inc. v. Howard , 42 Ohio St.2d 178, 183, 327 N.E.2d 654 (1975); Davis v. Tunison, 168 Ohio St. 471, 477, 155 N.E.2d 904 (1959); Roberts v.

Mason, 10 Ohio St. 277, 282 (1859) (the seminal opinion); Sutherland v. Nationwide
Gen. Ins. Co., 102 Ohio App.3d 297, 301, 657 N.E.2d 281 (10th Dist. 1995).

Under this second exception, attorney’ s fees are also allowed when punitive damages properly could have been awarded but were not awarded. Atram v. Star Tool & Die Co., 64 Ohio App.3d 388, 581 N.E.2d 1110 (8th Dist. 1989).

However, when attorney’s fees are awarded in connection with punitivedamages, the prevailing party might not be entitled to attorney’s fees for time spent defending the judgment on appeal. See LaFarciola v. Elbert , No. 08 -CA-009471, 2009 WL 2858059 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Sept. 8, 2009).

A third exception is that attorney’s fees are allowed when an enforceable contract specifically provides that the losing party shall pay the prevailing party’s attorney’s
fees. Wilborn v. Bank One Corp. , 121 Ohio St.3d 546, 906 N.E.2d 396, ¶ 7 (2009);

Nottingdale Homeowners’ Ass’n, Inc. v. Darby, 33 Ohio St.3d 32, 34, 514 N.E.2d 702 (1987). These provisions are not allowed in residential leases. R.C. 5321.13(C).

A fourth exception is that attorney’s fees are allowed in an action on the breach of a settlement agreement. Scott v. Reedy, 5 Ohio Dec. Reprint 388 (1876).

A fifth exception is that attorney’s fees are allowed in a civil contempt proceeding, if the defendant is found guilty of civil contempt. State ex rel. Fraternal Order of Police v. City of Dayton, 49 Ohio St.2d 219, 230, 361 N.E.2d 428 (1977).

A sixth exception is that attorney’s fees are allowed “when the litigation has created or protected a common fund.” Rocca v. Wilke , 53 Ohio App.2d 8, 18, 371 N.E.2d 223 (1st Dist. 1977). Accord Sutherland v. Nationwide Gen. Ins. Co., 102 Ohio App.3d 297, 300-01, 657 N.E.2d 281 (10th Dist. 1995); City of Seven Hills v. City of Cleveland, 47 Ohio App.3d 159, 163 -64, 547 N.E.2d 1024 (8th Dist. 1988). See generally Trs. of Internal Improvement Fund v. Greenough , 105 U.S. 527 (1881) (announcing the “common fund” doctrine).

Equity alone is not a basis for awarding attorney’s fees. In Sorin v. Bd. of Educ., Etc., 46 Ohio St.2d 177, 182 n.4, 347 N.E.2d 527 (1976), the Supreme Court reversed an equitable award of attorney’s fees and stated that it was “unpersuaded by the ‘reasoning’ of the Court of Appeals inChannell ....” The Channell decision awarded attorney’s fees on purely equitable grounds. Channell v. N.C.R. Employee’s Indep.

Union, 28 Ohio App.2d 260, 263-64, 277 N.E.2d 85 (2nd Dist. 1971).
