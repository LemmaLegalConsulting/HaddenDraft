# d. Court’s Inherent Authority

## Table-of-contents context

## Source text

A court has inherent authority to enter an order that seals the records of an eviction
action. Lokal Real Estate v. G.R ., No. 2017 -CVG-004609 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland,
Hous. Div., Aug. 20, 2018) (relying onSchussheim v. Schussheim, 137 Ohio St.3d 133, 998 N.E.2d 446 (2013).

In exercising this inherent authority, the court weighs its legitimate need to maintain public records against the tenant’s interest in not being denied housing based on the past filing of an eviction action. Id.

Courts consider a variety of factors in this weighing process. Id. Those factors include whether the landlord dismissed the eviction action, whether the landlord agrees to the sealing of the records or does not oppose it, and whether the tenant has been denied housing because of the unsealed records. Id.

Courts also consider whether unusual and exceptional circumstances exist. Id.

Those circumstances may be the tenant’s age, physical disability, low income, or limited English proficiency, or the disability of the tenant’s child. Id.
