# 2. Municipal Court

## Source text

Municipal courts (exclusive of municipal courts within Cuyahoga County and exclusive of housing and environmental divisions 362) have equity jurisdiction in landlord-tenant actions only when it is an action at law based on contr act. In such actions, under R.C. 1901.18(A)(3), a municipal court may enforce the equitable rights of a party to the contract and may grant any equitable relief necessary or proper for a complete determination of the rights of the parties.

Municipal courts have equity jurisdiction in eviction actions because an eviction action is an action at law upon a contract. See Sections I.A.4 and II.BB, above.

An action that seeks only equitable relief generally is not an action at law and, as a result, does not fa ll within the purview of R.C. 1901.18(A)(3). E.g., Dudkeiwicz v.

United Mobile Homes, No. 9-95-23, 1995 WL 577817 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., Sept. 19,
1995) (complaint sought only to enjoin defendant from unlawfully removing plaintiff and her trailer from the premises); Johnson v. Middleton , 66 Ohio App.3d 783, 586 N.E.2d 1093 (3rd Dist. 1989) (action for specific performance of a land installment contract).

In any action brought in a municipal court within Cuyahoga County, and in any action brought in a housi ng or an environmental division of a municipal court: the court or division “has jurisdiction to ... hear and determine all legal and equitable remedies necessary or proper for a complete determination of the rights of the parties.” R.C. 1901.13(B) (municipal courts within Cuyahoga County); R.C. 1901.131 (housing and environmental divisions). For further discussion on the equity jurisdiction of housing divisions, see Section XX.E, below.

Excluding cases in Cuyahoga County or in a housing or an environmental division, the equity jurisdiction of municipal courts is often an issue in R.C. 5321.15 actions.

See Chapters XII and XIII, above. The tenant typically seeks monetary and injunctive relief in response to a lockout, a utility termination, or a seizure of the tenant’s personal
property. Id. The resulting issue is whether municipal courts have equity jurisdiction under R.C. 1901.18(A)(3) to grant the injunctive relief. The better reasoned conclusion is that municipal courts have this equity jurisdiction because the R.C. 5321.15(C) claim 362 See note 361, above.

561 for monetary relief is an action at law based on contract (i.e., the rental agreement).363 Spaulding v. Sign of the Cross, No. 01-CV-19578 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., Sept. 4, 2001); Colquett v. Byrd, 59 Ohio Misc. 45, 392 N.E.2d 1328 (Ohio Mun., Mansfield, 1979). E.g., Parker v. Hiles, No. 02-CVH-362 (Ohio Mun., Chillicothe, Jan. 6, 2003) (enjoined landlord’s termination of water service).

Under the same reasoning, in a tenant’s R.C. 5321.04 contract claim for monetary and injunctive relief (see Sections IX.A.7,8, above), a municipal court has equity jurisdiction to enjoin the landlord from failing to remedy the R.C. 5321.04(A)
