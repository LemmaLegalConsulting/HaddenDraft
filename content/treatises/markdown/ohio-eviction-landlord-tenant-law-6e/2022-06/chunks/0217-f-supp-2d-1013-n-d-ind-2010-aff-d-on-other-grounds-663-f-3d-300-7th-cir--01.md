In addition, three post-Rucker Ohio appellate court decisions address the authority of a trial court to apply the equity defense in 42 U.S.C. § 14 37d(l)(6) eviction actions against innocent tenants.

In Portage Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Brumley, No. 2008-P-0019, 2008 WL 4693200, ¶¶ 93-97 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Oct. 24, 2008), the court held that a trial court may apply the equity defense in such cases . The trial court had considered the equity defense and had denied it based on the totality of the relevant circumstances. The Court of Appeals affirmed the trial court’s decision on the ground that the trial court had not abused its discretion.

In Dayton Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Kilgore , 194 Ohio App.3d 767, 958 N.E.2d 187 (2nd Dist. 2011) (2-1 decision), the court held that the trial court erred on two grounds in sustaining the innocent tenant equity defense. First, it held that equity does not apply to “positive statutes” and, according to the Court, 42 U.S.C. §1437d(l)(6) is a “positive statute.” Second, it found and relied on a distinction between Ms. Kilgore’s case and Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Harris, 139 Ohio Misc.2d 96, 861 N.E.2d 179 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., 2006). Unlike the Harris tenant, Ms. Kilgore was not an innocent tenant because she had “furthered her guests’ criminal purposes” by leaving them alone in her apartment and thereby “making her apartment open and available” for them to engage in the illegal drug activity. Kilgore, 194 Ohio App.3d 767, ¶ 29. The court did not address whether 42 U.S.C. § 1437d(l)(6) preempts the equity jurisdiction of a trial court in eviction actions. And the court did not reject the Harris holding.

In Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Davis, 197 Ohio App.3d 411, 967 N.E.2d 1244 (8th Dist. 2011), the court held (at least implicitly) that a trial court may apply the equity defense in such cases. The court: (1) briefly reviewed Harris and Kilgore, (2) noted that Kilgore distinguished Harris on the facts, and (3) thereupon ruled “[w]e find the facts in the present case more akin to Kilgore than Harris” (id., ¶ 33). The court, reviewing the facts under the competent, credible evidence standard, found sufficient evidence to support the trial court’s finding that the tenant was not an innocent tenant and, thereupon, upheld the trial court’s denial of the innocent tenant equity defense.
