“Under Ohio law, tenants holding leasehold estates have a recognized property interest.” Jones v. City of Norwood , No. C -120237, 2013 WL 454909, ¶ 58 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Feb. 6, 2013). “This property interest invokes the protection of procedural due process.” Id., ¶ 59.

Hudson v. Palmer, 468 U.S. 517 (1984); Logan v. Zimmerman, 455 U.S. 422 (1982).

485 Generally, due process requires notice and a hearing opportunity before a tenant may be evicted under a city’s code enforcement. Gardner v. Evans, 920 F.3d 1038, 1057-59 (6th Cir. 2019); Flatford v. City of Monroe, 17 F.3d 162, 167 (6th Cir. 1994);

Jones v. City of Norwood, No. C-120237, 2013 WL 454909, ¶ 59 (Ohio App., 1st Dist.,
Feb. 6, 2013).

“There are, however, ‘extraordinary circumstances where some valid governmental interest is at stake that justifies postponing the hearing until after [eviction].’” Flatford, 17 F.3d at 167 (quoting Fuentes v. Shevin , 407 U.S. 67, 82 (1972)). Accord Gardner v. Evans, 920 F.3d 1038, 1057-59 (6th Cir. 2019); Grayden
v. Rhodes, 345 F.3d 1225, 1227 (11th Cir. 2003) ; Jones v. City of Norwood , No. C- 120237, 2013 WL 4549 09, ¶ 60 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Feb. 6, 2013) . “Protecting citizens from an immediate risk of serious bodily harm falls squarely within those ‘extraordinary circumstances’ contemplated by Fuentes.” Flatford, 17 F.3d at 167.

But these exceptions to the general rule are rare. Jones, 2013 WL 454909, ¶ 60.

In Jones, the alleged overcrowding – the occupancy of two individuals in a one - bedroom apartment – did not pose an immediate threat to the safety of its occupants and thus did not fall within the exception to the general rule.

In Flatford, the Sixth Circuit granted city officials qualified immunity, on the due process prior hearing claim, because they had a reasonable basis to believe that the disrepair and dilapidation of the wooden -frame structure as a whole posed an immediate danger to its occupants and the public.

In Gardner, the Sixth Circuit applied its holding in Flatford that, absent code violations that pose an immediate danger, due process requires a pre -deprivation hearing before a tenant may be evicted under the city’s code enforcement. It thereupon held that, under the summary judgment record, the requisite immediate danger did not exist and therefore the inspectors were “neither immune from suit nor entitled to summary judgment on the pre -deprivation [hearing] claims. Gardner, 920 F.3d at 1059.

A tenant may enforce these due process rights under 42 U.S.C. § 1983. E.g., Gardner v. Evans, 920 F.3d 1038 (6th Cir. 2019); Flatford v. City of Monroe, 17 F.3d 162 (6th Cir. 1994); Jones v. City of Norwood, No. C-120237, 2013 WL 454909 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Feb. 6, 2013).
