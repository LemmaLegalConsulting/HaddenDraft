# 4. Do Not Disturb Neighbors’ Peaceful Enjoyment,

## Source text

R.C. 5321.05(A)(8) R.C. 5321.05(A)(8) requires a tenant and the tenant’s guests to conduct themselves in a manner that will not disturb the neighbors’ peaceful enjoyment of the premises.

If the conduct does not disturb the neighbors’ peaceful enjoyment of the premises, it does not constitute an R.C. 5321.05(A)(8) violation. Walnut Hills Apts. v. Costner, 108 Ohio Misc.2d 43, 739 N.E.2d 416 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., 1999) (when the tenant threatened a security guard with a gun, it did not disturb the neighbors’ peaceful enjoyment of the premises).

Some courts have excluded criminal conduct from the R.C. 5321.05(A)(8) obligation, even if the criminal conduct disturbs the neighbors’ peaceful enjoyment of the premises. E.g., Lorain Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Fonseca, 110 Ohio App.3d 292, 673 N.E.2d 1019 (9th Dist. 1996) (assaulted neighbor with steak knife); Akron Metro.
Hous. Auth. v. Sistrunk , No. 12253, 1986 WL 2214 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Feb. 12,
1986) (shot gun at neighbor); Walnut Hills Apts. v. Costner, 108 Ohio Misc.2d 43, 739 N.E.2d 416 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., 1999) (threatened security guard with gun);

D.C. Hughes Mgmt. Co. v. Webster , No. B101,837 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Dec. 20,
1974) (discharged gun into the air).

In these cases, the courts are excluding criminal conduct from the R.C.

5321.05(A)(8) obligation in order to avoid application of the R.C. 5321.11 notice and right to cure requirements (see Section I.D.2, above). The unintended consequence of this position is that, absent a written lease with a provision that prohibits the criminal conduct, the criminal conduct is not grounds for terminating the tenancy.

At least one court has held that criminal conduct is not excluded from the R.C.

5321.05(A)(8) obligation. College Hills Apts. v. McLaughlin, No. 2010-CVG-04025 (Ohio Mun., Clermont Cty., Mar. 10, 2011). In McLaughlin, the tenant’s daughter assaulted another resident of the apartment complex. The court held that this criminal conduct constitute d an R.C. 5321.05(A)(8) violation and, therefore, the tenant was 341 entitled to an R.C. 5321.11 notice of termination.

Two reasonable arguments support the position that criminal conduct is not excluded from the R.C. 5321.05(A)(8) obligation.

First, in 1990, the legislature revised R.C. 5321.05 and 5321.11 so as to add an R.C. 5321.05 obligation relative to certain criminal conduct (i.e., R.C. 5321.05(A)(9)) and to expressly exclude that criminal conduct from the R.C. 5321.11 requirements.

In failing to exclude any other criminal conduct from the R.C. 5321.11 requirements, the legislature evidenced an intent not to exclude any other criminal conduct from the R.C. 5321.11 requirements.

Second, R.C. 5321.05(A) does not expressly exclude criminal conduct from its list of obligations. Indeed, some of its obligations (in addition to those in R.C.

5321.05(A)(9)), on their face, constitute criminal conduct – for example, the R.C.

5321.05(A)(5) code obligations. Since the R.C. 5321.05(A) obligations , including those in R.C. 5321.05(A)(8), are not ambiguous, courts are obligated to enforce them as written, and not infer a criminal conduct exclusion. See Cheap Escape Co. v.

Haddox, L.L.C., 120 Ohio St.3d 493, 900 N.E.2d 601, ¶ 9 (2008).
