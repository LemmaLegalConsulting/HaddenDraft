## Source text

First, the Nutt decision relies on the erroneous underlying premise that the HCVP rent subsidy is not rent because the payment is made by a third party. The definition of rent, as set forth in McKee and elsewhere (see Section VII.F.1, below), does not exclude payments made by a third party on behalf of a tenant. Indeed, “there are many examples of third parties making rental payments on behalf of actual occupants of rented premises (such as parents m aking rental payments for premises occupied by their children).” Woodbridge Homes Ltd. P’ship v. Gregory , 697 S.E.2d 370, 379 (N.C. App. 2010).

Second, the above analysis of rent under the HCVP contracts and regulations undermines the Nutt finding that the HCVP rent subsidy is not rent. TheNutt decision ignores the HCVP contracts and regulations and, instead, relies on two project -based rent subsidy cases – Chapman and Helgason.

With respect to the project -based rent subsidy programs, in Ohio and amon g the states, the case law is split on whether the rent subsidy constitutes rent, for purposes of the waiver rule.

Court decisions holding that the project-based rent subsidy is rent, for purposes of the waiver rule, include: Classic A Props. v. Brown , No. 02-CA-2868, 2003 WL 22461816 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Sept. 30, 2003); White Rose Props. v. Babb, No. 00- CVF-1355 (Ohio Mun., Marietta, Jan. 23, 2001);Bella Vista Apts. v. Ragland, No. 91- CVG-54-471 (Ohio County Ct., Clermont Cty., May 31, 1991). See also Woodbridge Homes Ltd. P’ship v. Gregory, 697 S.E.2d 370 (N.C. App. 2010); Royal Am. Mgmt.,
Inc. v. Godfrey, 14 Fla. Supp.2d 56 (County Ct. 1985); Greenwich Gardens Assocs. v.

Pitt, 126 Misc.2d 947, 484 N.Y.S.2d 439 (Civ. Ct. 1984); Cent. Brooklyn Urban Dev.
Corp. v. Copeland, 122 Misc.2d 726, 471 N.Y.S.2d 989 (Dist. Ct. 1984).

Court decisions holding that the project-based rent subsidy is not rent, for purposes of the waiver rule, include:Nat’l Corp. for Hous. P’ships v. Chapman, 18 Ohio App.3d 131 104, 481 N.E.2d 654 (9th Dist. 1984). See also Midland Mgmt. Co. v. Helgason, 158 Ill.2d 98, 630 N.E.2d 836 (1994);E. Lake Mgmt. & Dev. Corp. v. Irvin, 195 Ill. App.3d 196, 551 N.E.2d 272 (1990).

The better reasoned view is that the project-based rent subsidy is rent, for purposes of the waiver rule, since it is a payment under the lease of a portion of the tenant’s contract rent. When the dwelling unit is vacant, the owner of a project -based development no longer receives a rent subsidy payment for the unit. Rather, the owner may receive vacancy payments for certain periods of time. E.g., 24 C.F.R.

§§ 880.611(c),(d), 886.109(c).

The only difference between the HCVP rent subsidy and the project -based rent subsidy, for purposes of the waiver rule, is that the HCVP rent subsidy is tied to the family and the project-based rent subsidy is tied to the dwelling unit. The relevance of this difference, if any, would be that the HCVP rent subsidy more clearly is only a rent payment made on behalf of a particular tenant, whereas the project-based rent subsidy might also be considered a subsidy for maintaining the entire subsidized development.

For this reason, the project-based rent subsidy decisions are an improper basis on which to rely in HCVP rent subsidy cases.
