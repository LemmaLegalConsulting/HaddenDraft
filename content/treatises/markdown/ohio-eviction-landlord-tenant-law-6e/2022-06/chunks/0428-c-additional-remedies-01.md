## Source text

When a landlord violates R.C. 5321.15(A), the tenant may assert common law claims against the landlord, in addition to the R.C. 5321.15(C) claim. See Section V.C., above. For example, the tenant may have a claim for:
(1) trespass, Meacham v. Miller, 79 Ohio App.3d 35, 606 N.E.2d 996 (4th
Dist. 1992); Babcock v. Milligan, No. 99-CI-420 (Ohio C.P., Coshocton Cty., Apr. 27, 2001);
(2) invasion of privacy, Meacham v. Miller, 79 Ohio App.3d 35, 606 N.E.2d 996 (4th Dist. 1992); Hall v. Lacheta , No. 92AP -020013, 1992 WL 362546 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Nov. 18, 1992);Babcock v. Milligan, No.

99-CI-420 (Ohio C.P., Coshocton Cty., Apr. 27, 2001);
(3) conversion, Foster v. Benson , No. 107366, 2019 WL 1858391 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Apr. 25, 2019); Gordon v. Morris , No. 2000 -CA-69, 2001 WL 85797 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Feb. 2, 2001); Meacham v.

Miller, 79 Ohio App.3d 35,606 N.E.2d 996 (4th Dist. 1992);Sanchez v.

Tompkins, No. 2021 -CVI-006506 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div.,
Sept. 13, 2021); Malcom v. Tate , No. 99 -CVH-21689 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., June 29, 2001); and/or
(4) intentional or negligent infliction of serious emotional distress , Stolzenburg v. Frush , No. 92 -CVC10-8217 (Ohio C.P., Franklin Cty.,
Dec. 8, 1994).

When the R.C. 5321.15(A) violation concerns a utility termination, the utility company may be a participant in the landlord’s unlawful activity. A typical situation is where: (1) the landlord is the customer for the utility service to the rental unit, (2) the landlord is obligated under the rental agreement to pay for the utility service, (3) the landlord instructs the utility company to terminate the utility service to the rental unit, and (4) the utility company then terminates the utility service. For a discussion on potential tenant claims against the utility company in these circumstances, see National Consumer Law Center, Access to Utility Service (5th ed. 2011).
