Three court decisions illustrate the manner in which constructive notice is applied in R.C. 5321.04 negligence per se claims: Kishman v. Lakeside Six Cottages, No. E- 83-24, 1983 WL 2339 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Dec. 30, 1983), Klump v. Douglas, No.

C-910060, 1991 WL 278262 (Ohi o App., 1st Dist., Dec. 31, 1991), and Brinson v.

Jenkins, No. C-960120, 1996 WL 626726 (Ohio App., 1st Dist., Oct. 30, 1996).

Kishman concerned water leaks that caused a weak bathroom floor through which the tenant stepped. The court held that the landlo rd had constructive notice of the defective condition. The landlord’s employees, who had inspected the premises ten days before leasing it, knew (or should have known if they had inspected properly) of the defective condition.

Klump concerned a toy on which the tenant tripped in the common area. The court held that the landlord did not have constructive notice of the defective condition.

“[T]he record in this case does not contain evidence from which to infer that the landlord had knowledge of the toy on the lawn or that it was there long enough that he should have discovered the potential danger by reasonable inspection.” 1991 WL 278262, at *2.

Brinson concerned a portion of a living room ceiling falling on the tenant. The court held that the landlord did not have constructive notice of the defective condition.

Notice to the landlord of water leaking in the kitchen and bathroom ceilings did not provide constructive notice of the defective living room ceiling because “nothing in the record, testimonial or otherwise, indicates that whatever problem or defect caused the water to leak in the kitchen or bathroom would, could or did cause the living-room ceiling to fall. No water dripped from or collected on the living -room ceiling; the portion of the ceiling that fell was not wet or moist when it fell, and there is no indication that it had been wet or moist.” 1996 WL 626716, at *4.
