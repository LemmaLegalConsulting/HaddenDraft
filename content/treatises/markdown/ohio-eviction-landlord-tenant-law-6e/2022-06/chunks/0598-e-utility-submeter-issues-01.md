# E. Utility Submeter Issues

## Table-of-contents context

## Source text

Some residential landlords and manufactured home park operators (“mh park operators”) install a utility submeter system for each of their rental units or lots and then charge the tenant or manufactured home park owner (“mh park owner”) for the service, at the public utility rate (or higher) plus an administrative fee. See Wingo v.

Nationwide Energy Partners, 163 Ohio St.3d 208, 169 N.E.3d 617 (2020); Pledger v.
Pub. Utils. Comm’n of Ohio, 109 Ohio St.3d 463, 849 N.E.2d 14 (2006); Anderle v.

Ideal Mobile Home Park, Inc., 114 Ohio App.3d 385, 683 N.E.2d 348 (8th Dist. 1996);

Sliwinski v. Capital Props. Mgmt. Ltd. , No. CV2006 -02-0884, 2007 WL 5843 246 (Ohio C.P., Summit Cty., Dec. 11, 2007).

In these circumstances, the landlord or mh park operator often uses a submeter company to manage the billing system.

A potential issue, relative to these submeter systems, is whether the landlord, mh park operator, or submeter company is operating as a public utility (and then subject to a determination on whether it is doing so unlawfully). PUCO makes this determination under the R.C. 4905.05 definitions of a public utility, as implemented in 613 the PUCO -established Shroyer test for submeter systems. 385 Wingo v. Nationwide Energy Partners, 163 Ohio St.3d 208, 169 N.E.3d 617 (2020); Pledger v. Pub. Utils.

Comm’n of Ohio, 109 Ohio St.3d 463, 849 N.E.2d 14 (2006) . In Pledger, the court upheld the Shroyer test. In Wingo, the court reaffirmed its support of the original Shroyer test but held unlawful PUCO’s 2016 and 2017 modifications of the Shroyer test.386 Another potential issue, relative to these submeter systems, is whether, under R.C.

Chapter 1327, these meters are subject to inspection and certification by the Weights and Measures Division of the Ohio Department of Agriculture (“ODA”). The ODA has stated that these meters are not subject to such inspection and certification because they are used for residential (and not commercial) purposes. Letter from William A.

Hopper, Jr., Chief Legal Counsel, ODA, to John Wood, Esq. (July 27, 2006).

However, in Sliwinski v. Capital Props. Mgmt. Ltd. , No. CV2006-020884, 2007 WL 5843246 (Ohio C.P., Summit Cty., Dec. 11, 2007), the court denied the landlord’s motion to dismiss the tenant’s claim that was premised on this inspection and certification requirement.387 Another potential issue, relative to these submeter companies, is whether the Ohio Consumer Sales Practices Act applies to th eir third-party billing system. For a discussion on this issue, see Section XXII.F, below.

385 PUCO adopted the Shroyer test in Matter of the Complaints of Inscho, Etc. v. Shroyer’s Mobile Homes, Pub. Utils. Comm’n of Ohio, Nos. 90 -182-WS-CSS, Etc., 1992 WL 937210 (Opinion and Order, filed Feb. 27, 1992). It is the following three-part test: (1) Has the landlord manifested an intent to be a public utility by availing itself of special benefits available to public utilities such as accepting a grant of a franchised territory, a certificate of public convenience and necessity, or use of the public right of way for utility purposes? ( 2) Is the utility service available to the general public rather than just to tenants? (3) Is the provision of utility service ancillary to the landlord’s primary business?
