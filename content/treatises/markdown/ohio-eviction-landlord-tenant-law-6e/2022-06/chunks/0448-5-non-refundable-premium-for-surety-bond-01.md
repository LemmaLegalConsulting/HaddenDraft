# 5. Non-Refundable Premium for Surety Bond

## Source text

Some landlords offer tenants an option to purchase a surety bond, from a designated third party (“bond company”), in lieu of a security deposit payment to the landlord (“deposit payment”). E.g., Kopp v. Ass ociated Estates Realty Corp. , No.

09AP-719, 2010 WL 1510196 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Apr. 15, 2010).

To obtain the surety bond, the tenant pays a non -refundable premium to the bond
company. The bond premium is less than the deposit payment. For example,in Kopp, the bond premium was $437 .50 and the deposit payment was $850.00 (one month’s rent).

Under the surety bond, at the end of the tenancy, the landlord is reimbursed by the bond company (instead of from a security deposit) for the landlord’s unpaid rent and/or property damage claims against the tenant. The amount of the reimbursement cannot exceed the bond’s coverage (e.g., in Kopp, $2,500.00). The tenant is then liable to the bond company for the amount of the reimbursement payment to the landlord. The tenant does not receive an opportunity to dispute the landlord’s claim before the bond company pays it.324 In Kopp, the court held that the bond premium is not a security deposit for two
