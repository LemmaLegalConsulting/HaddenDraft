# D. Tort Claim, Fraud and Deceit

## Table-of-contents context

- XIV. Tenant Claims for Wrongful
  - D. Tort Claim, Fraud and Deceit

## Source text

A tort claim, predicated upon fraud and deceit, may be a claim for wrongful
eviction. The claim arises in the context of an eviction action where the landlord’s right to possession concerns the landlord’s intended use or renovation of the premises after the eviction action.

For example, under the Housing Choice Voucher program, “other good cause” for a termination of the tenancy includes a landlord’s decision to use the dwelling unit for personal or family use or for a purpose other than as a residential rental unit. 24 C.F.R.

§ 982.310(d)(1)(iii).

Similarly, under the Housing and Rent Act of 1949, a landlord was permitted to bring an eviction action only if the landlord was seeking in good faith: (1) to use the premises for personal or family use, or (2) to alter, remodel, or repair the premises, and the alteration, remodeling, or repair could not be done with the tenant in possession.

Calvin v. Martin, 64 Ohio Law Abs. 265, 268, 111 N.E.2d 786 (8th Dist. 1952).

As a general rule, common law requires that the intent of the landlord, in seeking to evict the tenant, be determined in the original eviction action. Id. at 273 -74.

However, when the eviction action concerns a landlord’s future use or renovation of the premises, the lan dlord’s intent cannot ultimately be ascertained until after the eviction action. Id. at 274. Contra Harrison v. Webb , 62 Ohio Law Abs. 273, 100 N.E.2d 728 (Ohio C.P., Cuyahoga Cty., 1951).

In response to this dilemma, the courts created a claim for wro ngful eviction that is an exception to the general rule. Calvin, 64 Ohio Law Abs. at 274 -75. Contra Harrison v. Webb, 62 Ohio Law Abs. 273, 100 N.E.2d 728 (Ohio C.P., Cuyahoga Cty., 1951). The elements of this wrongful eviction claim are an eviction ofa tenant, during the term of the tenant’s right of occupancy, procured by fraud and deceit. Calvin, 64 Ohio Law Abs. at 268-75.

The fraud and deceit in this wrongful eviction claim concern whether the landlord has misrepresented the reason for seeking p ossession of the premises. All of the elements necessary to maintain a fraud and deceit action at common law must be
present. Id. at 272-75.

A tenant’s vacation of the premises without protest, upon receipt of a notice to leave the premises, constitutes a waiver of this wrongful eviction claim. Id. at 273.

The general rules on tort damages govern this wrongful eviction claim. The tenant is permitted to recover all damages sustained as a proximate result of the wrongful
eviction. If the landlord acted with malice, the tenant is permitted to recover punitive damages under R.C. 2315.21.

And, if the tenant is awarded punitive damages, the tenant may also be entitled to an award of attorney’s fees under Ohio common law. See Section XIX.B, below.
