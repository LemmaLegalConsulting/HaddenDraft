When a tenant tenders a check (or money order) to a landlord for a rent payment, “the act of cashing [the] check constitutes acceptance” of the rent payment.Associated Estates Corp. v. Bartell, 24 Ohio App.3d 6, 9, 492 N.E.2d 841 (8th Dist. 1985).Accord Miami Valley United Methodist Mission Soc’y v. White-Dawson, No. 17873, 2000 WL 234712, at *3 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Mar. 3, 2000). See generally First Nat’l Bank & Trust Co. v. Fireproof Warehouse & Storage , 8 Ohio App.3d 253, 456 N.E.2d 1336 (10th Dist. 1983).

A la ndlord may not negate such acceptance of the check (or money order) by tendering back to the tenant a subsequent check in the amount of the rent payment.

Bartell, 24 Ohio App.3d at 9.

An exception to this rule applies when a landlord inadvertently cashes one check, out of many checks that are offered and promptly returned to the tenant, and the money for the cashed check is returned as soon as the landlord discovers the mistake.

Associated Estates Corp. v. Smith , No. 46054, 1984 WL 5227 (Ohio App., 8th Dist.,
Oct. 4, 1984). The inadvertent element was not met in a case where the landlord accepted rent payments for April and May and then returned the payments at the end of May. Woodson v. Howard , No. 2008 -CVG-9709 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous.

Div., June 30, 2008).

If the landlord does not cash the check or otherwise convert the check to the landlord’s use, but nevertheless retains the check, there may be acceptance of the rent
payment. The landlord’s retention of the check constitutes acceptance of the rent payment, unless the landlord: (1) notifies the tenant, upon receipt of the check, that it is not being accepted or that it is being held only for evidentiary purposes; and (2) returns the check to the tenant on or before the day of trial.
