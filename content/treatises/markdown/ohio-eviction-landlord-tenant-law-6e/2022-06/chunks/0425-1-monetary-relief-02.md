“The punitive damages should bear a reasonable relationship to Plaintiff’s [tenant’s] damages ... but need not be limited to any particular multiple of this amount.” Smith v. Akarui Enters., LLC , No. 2010 -CVH-18860 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous.

Div., June 30, 2011) (punitive damage award in amount 30 times other damages).See, e.g., Fisher v. Barker , 159 Ohio App.3d 745, 825 N.E.2d 244 (2nd Dist. 2005) (unlawful entry and conversion claims – compensatory damages reduced from $2,000 to $600 and, thereupon, punitive damages reduced from $20,000 to $5,000);Gaston v.

Seals, No. CV-03-505578 (Ohio C.P., Cuyahoga Cty., May 4, 2004) (punitive damage award, $5,347.60, equal to the compensatory damages, $668.45, multiplied by eight, the number of tenants harmed by the landlord).

As to due process limitations, “[t]he U.S. Supreme Court did not lay down a 4-to- 1 or single-digit-ratio rule – it merely said that ‘there is a presumption against an award that has a 145-to-1 ratio’ ...– and it would be unreasonable to do so.”Mathias v. Accor
Econ. Lodging, Inc., 347 F.3d 672, 676 (7th Cir. 2003) (upheld 37.2 to 1 ratio, where injury from bed bugs in a hotel room that was not to be assigned until treated).

But query whether these claims are tort claims subject to the R.C. 2315.21 punitive damages limitation (2 to 1 ratio). See Sivit v. Vill. Green of Beachwood, L.P., 143 Ohio St.3d 168, 35 N.E.3d 508, ¶ 5 (2015) (R.C. 5321.04 negligence per se claim is a tort action within the purview of R.C. 2315.21).
