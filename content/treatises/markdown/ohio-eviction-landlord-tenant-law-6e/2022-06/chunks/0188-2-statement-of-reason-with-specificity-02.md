The decision in Driver v. Hous. Auth. of Racine County , Nos. 2005-AP-410,411, 2006 WL 288152 (Wis. App. Feb. 8, 2006), summarizes well the three primary reasons that the tenant’s actual knowledge is and should be immaterial to whether a notice of termination has met the specificity requirement. First, the HUD regulations require the landlord to provide the tenant with written notice of the reasons for the proposed termination of tenancy, with the requisite specificity. Second, due process places the burden of providing adequate notice on the landlord. With an “actual knowledge” exception, this burden often would be transferred to the tenant, relative to whether or not the tenant received an alleged or al notice or otherwise already knew. Third, an “actual knowledge” exception would invite landlord noncompliance with the regulation’s written notice requirement.
