Underlying Judgment In an R.C. Chapter 5321 action, because the amount of the money judgment is often relatively small, the amount of the attorney fee award may be limited by Rule 1.5(a)(4) of the Ohio Rules of Professional Conduct , which requires that attorney’s fees be reasonable in relation to the amount involved an d the results obtained. E.g., Nolan v. Sutton , 97 Ohio App.3d 616, 647 N.E.2d 218 (1st Dist. 1994) ($4,170 fee requested, $500 fee awarded); Cook v. Downey , No. 87AP -1075, 1988 WL 70446 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., June 30, 1988) ($4,322.76 fee requested, $650 fee awarded);

Lewis v. Romans , No. 43594, 1981 WL 4713 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Dec. 24, 1981) ($8,800 fee requested, $350 fee awarded). See also Smith v. Padgett , 32 Ohio St.3d 344, 350, 513 N.E.2d 737 (1987) (Wright, J., concurring).

However, as one cou rt observed, in applying this factor, it is important to be mindful that “limiting the attorney fees available might discourage attorneys from taking these cases”358 and thereby undermine an important purpose of R.C. Chapter 5321 – to discourage violations of the Act. Johnson v. Morelli Props. LLC, No. 2008- CVF-023275, slip op. at 5 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., Oct. 7, 2009) ($3,350 fee award, $960 judgment). E.g., Timoneri v. Northsteppe Realty , 71 N.E.3d 630, 2016 -Ohio- 5901 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., 2016) (a ffirms $10,505 fee award, $695 judgment);

Spring Hill Townhomes v. Pounds , No. 25887, 2014 WL 1887690 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., May 9, 2014) ( affirms $4,637.50 fee award, $539 judgment) ; Liston v. Jurek ,
No. 59758, 1992 WL 41858 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Mar. 5, 1992) (affirms $3,972 fee award, $313.70 judgment).

Another court similarly observed: “Simply because the attorney fees incurred in this case were greatly disproportionate to the damages recovered on [the tenant’s] security deposit claim does not mean t hey were unreasonable. As stated above, R.C.

5321.16 is a remedial statute intended to ensure that a tenant incurs no expense when seeking a refund of a wrongfully withheld security deposit. Consistent with that purpose, [the tenant] was entitled to reco ver reasonable attorney fees [the landlord’s] actions forced her to incur to enforce her statutory rights.” Alcorso v. Correll , No.

110218, 2021 WL 4317396, ¶ 49 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Sept. 23, 2021) ($26,825 fee award, $1,200 judgment).

Appellate court decisions that found an abuse of discretion and reversed the trial court’s attorney fee award because the fee award was significantly less than the lodestar amount include: Congdon v. Congdon concerned a tenant’s R.C. 5321.16(C) claim for attorney’s fees. The trial court awarded the tenant a judgment of $550 for the security deposit, $550 for statutory damages, and $600 for attorney’s fees. Based on the trial court’s findings on reasonable hours (36.7) and reasonable rate ($140 per hour), the lod estar amount was $5,138.359 Hall concerned a tenant’s R.C. 5321.15(C) claim for attorney’s fees. The trial court awarded the tenant a judgment of $500 for the R.C. 5321.15 claim, $2,500 for an invasion of privacy claim, and $3,632 for attorney’s fees. Thetrial court’s finding on reasonable hours (86.8) was not disputed. But despite the uncontested testimony on reasonable rate ($75 per hour), the trial court used hourly rates of $50 (in -court) and $40 (out-of-court).
