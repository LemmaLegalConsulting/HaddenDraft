# A. Police Assisting Landlord

## Table-of-contents context

- XV. Tenant Claims for Wrongful

## Source text

A tenant may be wrongfully evicted by a person who is acting under color of state
law. This typically occurs when a private landlord unlawfully removes a tenant from the premises by force, with the assistance of a police officer. E.g., Soldal v. Cook County, Ill. , 506 U.S. 56 (1992); Thomas v. Cohen , 304 F.3d 563 (6th Cir. 2002);

Howerton v. Gabica, 708 F.2d 380 (9th Cir. 1983); Pellowski v. Burke, 686 F.2d 631 (8th Cir. 1982). See also Jones v. Gutschenritter , 909 F.2d 1208 (8th Cir. 1990) (termination of the electrical service to the premises). Cf. Revis v. Meldrum, 489 F.3d 273 (6th Cir. 2007) (seizure of judgment debtor’s residential property under a writ).

In these circumstances, the tenant may have one or two constitutional claims. One is a claim for a violation of the tenant’s procedural due process rights under the Fourteenth Amendment. The tenant’s right to possession of the premises under the tenancy is a “property interest” for Fourteenth Amendment purposes. Thomas v.

Cohen, 304 F.3d 563 (6th Cir. 2002). See also Revis v. Meldrum , 489 F.3d 273, 284 (6th Cir. 2007). Likewise, a tenant’s right under state law not to be evicted by force, except through a court proceeding, is a “property interest” for Fourteenth Amendment
purposes. Pellowski v. Burke, 686 F.2d 631 (8th Cir. 1982).

The second is a claim for a violation of the t enant’s rights under the Fourth Amendment’s protection against unreasonable seizure. Soldal v. Cook County, Ill. , 506 U.S. 56 (1992); Tinney v. Shores, 77 F.3d 378 (11th Cir. 1996). See also Thomas
v. Cohen, 304 F.3d 563 (6th Cir. 2002) (defendants grant ed qualified immunity, 2 -1 decision); Kernats v. O’Sullivan, 35 F.3d 1171 (7th Cir. 1994) (defendants granted qualified immunity). Cf. Harvey v. Plains Twp. Police Dep’t, 421 F.3d 185 (3d Cir.
2005) (police officer assisted former joint tenant recover pro perty from tenant’s apartment).

The tenant may bring these claims under 42 U.S.C. § 1983. The “under color of state law” element of the § 1983 claim is met if the police officer actively assisted in the forcible removal of the tenant, or if the police o fficer so intimidated the tenant as to cause the tenant to refrain from exercising the tenant’s legal right to resist the
removal. Jones v. Gutschenritter, 909 F.2d 1208, 1211-13 (8th Cir. 1990); Howerton
v. Gabica, 708 F.2d 380, 382 (9th Cir. 1983). Cf. Harvey v. Plains Twp. Police Dep’t, 421 F.3d 185 (3d Cir. 2005) (a police officer directed the landlord to open the door to enable a former joint tenant to enter the apartment).

The “under color of state law” element of the § 1983 claim is not met if thepolice officer is simply standing by in case of trouble. Id. See also Mann v. Hillsborough County Sheriff’s Office, 946 F. Supp. 962 (M.D. Fla. 1996). Similarly, it is not met if the police officer appears at the scene for a short time, and does not participate in the landlord’s attempt to forcibly remove the tenant from the premises.Corbin v. Stevens,
No. 91-Civ-1054, 1992 WL 96684 (S.D.N.Y. Apr. 30, 1992).

When the wrongful eviction of the tenant is the result of random and unauthorized acts of a subordinate police officer, under the Parratt doctrine,322 a procedural due process claim might not exist if there is an adequate state law remedy. E.g., Tinney v.

Shores, 77 F.3d 378 (11th Cir. 1996) (random acts, Parratt applicable); Scott v.

O’Grady, 760 F. Supp. 1288 (N.D. Ill. 1991) (not random acts, Parratt inapplicable), aff’d on other grounds, 975 F.2d 366 (7th Cir. 1992).

Under the joint participation doctrine, these § 1983 claims may be asserted against the police officer and the private landlord. Howerton v. Gabica, 708 F.2d 380 (9th Cir.

1983).

In these § 1983 claims, the tenant may recover compensatory damages and, for malicious, willful, or wanton misconduct, punitive damages. Pellowski v. Burke, 686 F.2d 631 (8th Cir. 1982).

If the facts available to the sheriff made the existence of a tenancy questionable, the sheriff may have a qualified immunity defense, based on the ground that the sheriff could not objectively, reasonably know that the sheriff’s action violated the Fourth or Fourteenth Amendment. See, e.g., Higgins v. Penobscot County Sheriff’s Dep’t, 446 F.3d 11 (1st Cir. 2006).
