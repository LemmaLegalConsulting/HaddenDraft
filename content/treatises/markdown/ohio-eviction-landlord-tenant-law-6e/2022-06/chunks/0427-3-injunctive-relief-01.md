# 3. Injunctive Relief

## Source text

In an R.C. 5321.15(A),( C) action, the tenant may request injunctive relief, in addition to monetary relief and reasonable attorney’s fees. For a discussion on the jurisdiction of municipal courts to grant injunctive relief in these cases, see Section XX.C.2, below.

Due to the emergency nature of a utility termination or a lockout, the request for injunctive relief often includes a request for a temporary restraining order. In a utility termination case, the utility company may be an appropriate additional defendant to ensure that the tenant receives full and timely relief (i.e., immediate restoration of the utility service).

When requesting this injunctive relief, the tenant may face arguments concerning:
(1) the equity requirement of clean hands, (2) the bond requirement forthe issuance of a temporary restraining order, and (3) the absence of any express authorization in R.C.

5321.15 for granting injunctive relief.

Since the request for injunctive relief (including a temporary restraining order) is a request for equitable rel ief, it is appropriate for the court to consider whether the tenant comes into court with clean hands. However, courts typically do not deny the request for a temporary restraining order on this basis, especially when the landlord’s conduct is egregious and constitutes a clear violation of R.C. 5321.15(A). Nonetheless, when the tenant is far behind in rent payments, some courts are reluctant to issue a temporary restraining order.

Civil Rule 65(C) establishes the bond requirement for the issuance of a temporary restraining order. Under Civil Rule 65(C), the discretion committed to the court for determining the size of the bond includes the discretionary authority to require no bond at all. Vanguard Transp. Sys., Inc. v. Edwards Transfer & Storage Co. , 109 Ohio App.3d 786, 793, 673 N.E.2d 182 (10th Dist. 1996); Colquett v. Byrd, 59 Ohio Misc.

45, 49-50, 392 N.E.2d 1328 (Ohio Mun., Mansfield, 1979).

Among the reasons for which a court may exercise its discretion to require no bond (or a minimal bond) is the moving party’s lack of financial means to post a bond.

Colquett v. Byrd, 59 Ohio Misc. 45, 49-50, 392 N.E.2d 1328 (Ohio Mun., Mansfield, 1979). See also Wayne Chemical, Inc. v. Columbus Agency Serv. Corp., 562 F.2d 692, 701 (7th Cir. 1977); Doe by Doe v. Perales, 782 F. Supp. 201, 206 (W.D.N.Y. 1991);

473 Walker v. Pierce, 665 F. Supp. 831, 843-44 (N.D. Cal. 1987).319 In utility termination cases, another reason on which courts have relied for not requiring a bond is that the landlord “will sustain no damages if it is finally decided that the injunction should not have been granted.” Brown v. Ray, No. 08-CVG-25314, ¶ 41 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Nov. 26, 2008).

As a result, Ohio courts have issued injunctions in R.C. 5321.15 actions without a bond requirement or with a minimal bond requirement. E.g., Brown v. Ray, No. 08- CVG-25314 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Nov. 26, 2008);Colquett v. Byrd, 59 Ohio Misc. 45, 392 N.E.2d 1328 (Ohio Mun., Mansfield, 1979).

R.C. 5321.15 does not expressly auth orize injunctive relief. This omission contrasts with other sections of R.C. Chapter 5321 that do expressly authorize injunctive relief (i.e., R.C. 5321.04(B) and 5321.05(C)).

But the remedies provided tenants in R.C. Chapter 5321 are intended to supplement other remedial measures. See Section V.C., above. Other remedial measures include the authority to grant injunctive relief that is conferred upon courts by R.C. 2727.03 (courts of common pleas), 1901.18 (municipal courts), R.C.

1901.13(B) (municipal c ourts, Cuyahoga County), and R.C. 1901.131 (municipal courts, housing divisions). Courts have the right and the responsibility to exercise that authority in any appropriate case. Ex Parte Cattell, 146 Ohio St. 112, 64 N.E.2d 416 (1945).

When a tenant files an R.C. 5321.15 action for injunctive relief, an eviction action by the landlord is a compulsory counterclaim to the R.C. 5321.15 action. Trimble v.

Jones, No. 89-CVGT-589 (Ohio Mun., Miami Cty., June 13, 1989).

When a court grants injunctive relief in an R.C. 5321.15 action, a landlord’s noncompliance is typically grounds for a contempt order, which may mandate compliance and award the tenant compensatory damages and attorney’s fees. E.g., Brown v. Ray, No. 08-CVG-25314 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous.Div., Dec. 17, 2008).

For a discussion on compensatory damages and attorney’s fees in civil contempt proceedings, see Section IX.A.7, above.
