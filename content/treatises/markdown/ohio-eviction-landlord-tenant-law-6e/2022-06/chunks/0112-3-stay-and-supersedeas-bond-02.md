After the court has determined the amount of the landlord’s potential damages from the granting of a stay of execution, it must determine the form of the bond to be
imposed. Courts have discretion to tailor the requisites and conditions of the bond to the needs of the litigation. T&R Props. Inc. v. Wimberly , No. 2019 -CVG-029217 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., May 4, 2021) (cites this book’s fifth editiondiscussion on this issue); 4 Ohio Jur. 3d Appellate Review § 229 (2011).

In an eviction action, generally the court will demand one of two basic types of
bonds. T&R Props. Inc. v. Wimberly , No. 2019-CVG-029217 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., May 4, 2021) (cites this book’s fifth edition discussion on this issue). One is a use and occupancy bond, whereby the tenant is required to pay to the court or the landlord each month, during the pendency of the appeal, the amount of rent that is due for the month. Id. The other is a lump sum bond in the amount of the rent that is likely to become due during the pendency of the appeal. Id.

The obvious merit and inherent fairness of the use and occupancy bo nd is that it allows any tenant, regardless of the tenant’s financial means, to pursue an appeal without jeopardizing the protection due the landlord. A use and occupancy bond adequately protects the landlord because, if the tenant misses a monthly rent payment, the court may vacate the stay of execution.

In contrast, if a court refuses to permit a use and occupancy bond and, instead, requires a low-income tenant to post a lump sum bond in the amount of several months’ rent, the tenant is effectively denie d the right to appeal. Most low -income tenants cannot acquire that amount of money at one time.

A court’s refusal to permit a use and occupancy bond may violate a low -income 113 tenant’s due process and/or equal protection rights.See Usher v. Waters Ins. & Realty Co., Inc., 438 F. Supp. 1215 (W.D.N.C. 1977);Jack Spring, Inc. v. Little, 50 Ill.2d 351, 280 N.E.2d 208 (1972); Harrington v. Harrington , 269 A.2d 310 (Me. 1970). See generally Lindsey v. Normet, 405 U.S. 56 (1972). Cf. M.L.B. v. S.L.J., 519 U.S. 102 (1996). But see Letendre v. Fugate, 701 F.2d 1093 (4th Cir. 1983). But cf. Bankers Life & Cas. Co. v. Crenshaw, 486 U.S. 71 (1988).

Because “the purpose of the supersedeas bond is [to] ensure an appellee can be made whole if the appellant is not successful with the appeal,” when the appellant is successful on the appeal, the appellee does “not have a legal basis to recover against the deposited bond money.” T&R Props. Inc. v. Wimberly , No. 2019-CVG-029217, slip op. at 3 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., May 4, 2021). In these cases, “[l]ogically, the appeal did not delay plaintiff’s enforcement of the eviction judgment, since the judgment was reversed.” Id.
