# b. Hearsay Evidence

## Source text

In an administrative hearing on the termination of a family’s participation in the HCVP, “evidence may be considered without regard to admissibility under the rules of evidence applicable to judicial proceedings.” 24 C.F.R. § 982.5 55(e)(5). Therefore, the hearing officer may consider hearsay evidence.

However, there are limits under Ohio law on the type of hearsay evidence that is admissible in these hearings.

R.C. 2506.04 requires substantial, reliable, and probative evidence. “( 1) ‘Substantial’ evidence is evidence with some weight; it must have importance and
value. (2) ‘Reliable’ evidence is dependable; that is, it can be confidently trusted. In order to be reliable, there must be a reasonable probability that the evidence is true.
(3) ‘Probative’ evidence is evidence that tends to prove the issue in question; it must be relevant in determining the issue.”Our Place, Inc. v. Ohio Liquor Control Comm’n, 63 Ohio St.3d 570, 571, 589 N.E.2d 1303 (1992). Accord City of Cleveland v. Posner, 193 Ohio App.3d 211, 951 N.E.2d 476, ¶ 27 (8th Dist. 2011).

For example, Ohio law permits reliance on hearsay evidence where it has anindicia of reliability and other evidence supports it. E.g., Doersam v. City of Gahanna , No.

96-APF12-1766, 1997 WL 607473, at *9,10 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Sept. 30, 1997);

Habe v. S. Euclid Civil Serv. Comm’n, No. 61786, 1993 WL 27682, at *3 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Feb. 4, 1993). See generally Consol. Edison Co. v. NLRB, 305 U.S. 197, 230
(1938) (“Mere uncorro borated hearsay or rumor does not constitute substantial evidence”).

Evidence is inherently unreliable where it “consists of subjective judgments of third parties, not present at the hearing.”Dudukovich v. Lorain Metro. Hous. Auth., 58 Ohio St.2d 202, 208, 389 N.E.2d 1113 (1979).

Two Ohio court decisions that reversed PHA HCVP termination decisions (concerning alleged unauthorized occupants ) because the PHA decisions relied on unreliable hearsay evidence are: Gooden v. Cincinnati Metro. Hous. Auth. , No. A - 0710779 (Ohio C.P., Hamilton Cty., July 25, 2008, and Aug. 29, 2008) (court documents in domestic violence criminal proceeding), andRodriguez v. Lorain Metro.
Hous. Auth., No. 05-CV-140942 (Ohio C.P., Lorain Cty., Oct. 23, 2006) (newspaper articles).

A related issue is whether, under due process, a PHA may rely solely on hearsay evidence, when contrary, non-hearsay evidence raises issues of credibility.

663 Some court decisions hold that, in these circumstances, due process bars the PHA from relying solely on hearsay evidence. E.g., Stevenson v. Willis, 579 F. Supp.2d 913, 919-20 (N.D. Ohio 2008); Edgecomb v. Hous. Auth. of Town of Vernon, 824 F. Supp.

312, 316 (D. Conn. 1993); Young v. Maryville Hous. Auth., No. 3:09-cv-37, 2009 WL 2043891, at *8 (E.D. Tenn. July 2, 2009) (preliminary injunction). See also Davis v.

Mansfield Metro. Hous. Auth., 751 F.2d 180, 185 (6th Cir. 1984) (due process requires the right to confront and cross -examine all witnesses upon whose testimony or information the PHA relies and a written decision based solely on the evidence presented at the hearing).

In Woods v. Willis, 515 F. App’x 471, 282-83, 2013 WL 611141 (6th Cir. 2013), the court reviews the split in the court decisions on this issue. The court does not need to (and el ects not to) render a decision on the issue. “We need not decide whether Defendant may rely solely on reliable hearsay testimony, or if such reliance violates due process or the regulations absent the right to question the declarant, because the evidence here fails under either rule.” Id., 515 F. App’x at 483.
