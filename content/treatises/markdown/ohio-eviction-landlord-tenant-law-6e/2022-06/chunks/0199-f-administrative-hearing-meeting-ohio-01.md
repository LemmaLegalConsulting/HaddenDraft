# F. Administrative Hearing/Meeting, Ohio

## Source text

Case Law In project-based assisted housing, when a landlord proposes to terminate a tenancy, generally the tenant is entitled to an opportunity to contest the proposed termination in an administrative hearing or a meeting, before the commencement of an eviction action 223 against the tenant.

Subject to three exceptions, a Public Housing tenant is entitle d to an opportunity to contest the proposed termination in an administrative hearing under the housing authority’s grievance procedure. 24 C.F.R. §§ 966.4(l)(3), 966.4(n), 966.51(a). The three exceptions are cases in which the termination involves; (1) any criminal activity that threatens the health, safety, or right to peaceful enjoyment of the premises of other residents or housing authority employees, (2) any violent or drug -related criminal activity on or off the premises, or (3) any criminal activity that resulted in felony conviction of a household member. Id.

In addition, a Public Housing tenant may file an R.C. Chapter 2506 appeal (see Section XXIV.C, below) from a grievance procedure decision on a proposed termination of tenancy, even if an eviction action has been filed. Perry v. Akron Metro.
Hous. Auth., No. CV-05-01-0290 (Ohio C.P., Summit Cty., Feb. 28, 2005). However, pending the R.C. Chapter 2506 appeal, unless the eviction action is stayed, the eviction court will likely enter a final order and thereby determine the R.C. Chapter 2506 appeal under res judicata or collateral estoppel principles. In Perry, the court of common pleas entered an order that stayed the pending eviction action in the Cuyahoga Falls Municipal Court, “pending a fina l decision in the administrative appeal.” Perry v.

Akron Metro. Hous. Auth. , No. CV -05-01-0290 (Ohio C.P., Summit Cty., Mar. 1, 2005).

A project-based subsidized housing tenant is entitled to an opportunity to contest the proposed termination in a meeti ng with the landlord. 24 C.F.R. § 880.607(c)(1);

HUD Handbook 4350.3 REV-1,195 ¶ 8-13(B). Neither the federal regulations nor due process provide project -based subsidized housing tenants with a right to an administrative hearing. Perry v. Royal Arms Apts., 729 F.2d 1081 (6th Cir. 1984) (no due process right to an administrative hearing). Nonetheless, at the meeting, the landlord is required to attempt to resolve the controversy and thereby avoid the tenant’s loss of subsidized housing. Gorsuch Homes, Inc. v. Wooten, 73 Ohio App.3d 426, 434, 597 N.E.2d 554 (2nd Dist. 1992); Crossroads Somerset Ltd. v. Newland , 40 Ohio
App. 3d 20, 24, 531 N.E.2d 327 (10th Dist. 1987).

Ohio state court decisions on these administrative hearing and meeting rights are set forth below.

Fed. Prop. Mgmt. v. Brown, No. 17424, 1999 WL 961275 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., June 25, 1999) (Section 8 New Construction) The notice of termination stated that the tenant had ten days within which to discuss the proposed termination with the landlord. This satisfied the requirement to provide the tenant with an opportunity to meet with the landlord, notwithstanding the landlord’s letter to the tenant, fourteen days earlier, stating that “it is unfortunate that that there is no need to request meet ings or telephone to discuss this matter; the context is very clear.” The earlier letter did not foreclose the possibility of a meeting between the landlord and tenant. The tenant did not request a meeting in response to the notice of termination.
Vill. S. Apts. v. Jensen , No. CA96-10-018, 1997 WL 148067 (Ohio App., 12th Dist.,
Mar. 31, 1997) (project-based subsidized housing) During the required meeting, the landlord told the tenant that there was nothing that she could do to avert the termination of the tenancy because she repeatedly had breached the lease agreement despite numerous warnings. The landlord’s position did not deny the tenant the required opportunity to meet with the landlord. The requirement to meet does not require “the landlord to resolve the dispute in a manner favorable to the tenant or to refrain from filing a forcible entry and detainer action simply because the tenant requests a meeting to discuss the termination.” Vistula Mgmt. Co. v. Armstrong , No. L -93-168, 1994 WL 262571 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., June 10, 1994) (Section 8 New Construction) “While we recognize the purpose of the ten -day meeting required under the federal regulations and realize that a landlord should not enter into a meeting already having decided to evict the tenan t, that does not mean that a landlord must absolutely let a tenant cure her breach.” Gorsuch Homes, Inc. v. Wooten, 73 Ohio App.3d 426, 597 N.E.2d 554 (2nd Dist. 1992) (Section 8 New Construction) “The purpose of the requirement of the federal regulationsand the lease agreement that a tenant must be afforded an opportunity to meet with the landlord to discuss a proposed termination of tenancy is to attempt to resolve the controversy in a mutually satisfactory manner, that will, if possible, avoid the tenant’s loss of subsidized housing while protecting the rights of the landlord.” The landlord’s failure to give the tenant an opportunity to meet with the landlord to discuss the notice of proposed termination, after the tenant had requested timely such a mee ting, was grounds to reverse the eviction judgment.

Chautauqua Park Apts. v. McMullen, No. 791, 1992 WL 303139 (Ohio App., 4th Dist.,
Oct. 14, 1992) (project-based subsidized housing) 225 The landlord met the requirement for a meeting even though thelandlord’s agent at the meeting did not have personal authority to halt the proposed termination of tenancy.

The landlord’s agent at the meeting “served as a conduit through which appellants [tenants] could reach the party who did have authority.” Nothing in the record suggests that the tenant’s arguments were not listened to and given due consideration.

Crossroads Somerset Ltd. v. Newland, 40 Ohio App.3d 20, 531 N.E.2d 327 (10th Dist.
1987) (project-based subsidized housing) At the meeting between the la ndlord and tenant, the parties are required to attempt to resolve whatever problems gave rise to the notice of termination.

Trumbull Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Reid , No. 3673, 1987 WL 18019 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Sept. 30, 1987) (Public Housing) The tenant did not have a right to cure, during the pendency of her grievance procedure hearing, her prior refusal to furnish certain income verification information.

Fitzpatrick v. Wallick Props., Inc., No. L-83-036, 1983 WL 6817 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., June 3, 1983) (Section 8 New Construction) The landlord’s cancellation of an informal meeting, which had been scheduled pursuant to 24 C.F.R. § 880.607, and the landlord’s failure to afford the tenant any other opportunity to meet with the landlord, did not prejudice the tenant and therefore were not grounds to reverse the eviction judgment.
