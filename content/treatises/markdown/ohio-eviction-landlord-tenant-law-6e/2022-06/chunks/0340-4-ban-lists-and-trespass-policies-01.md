# 4. Ban Lists and Trespass Policies

## Source text

A common issue is whether a landlord may ban specific p ersons (who are not
tenants) from entering the landlord’s rental property and, if so, in what circumstances.

The issue arises in two contexts.

One context is a trespass action against the banned person under R.C.

2911.21(A)(1). One element of this trespass charge is that the person is on the land or premises “without privilege.” “‘Privilege’ for purposes of criminal trespass includes permission to enter the premises given by a resident of the premises.”State v. Clelland, 83 Ohio App.3d 474, 490, 615 N.E.2d 276 (4th Dist. 1992).

A second context is an eviction action against the tenant for a lease violation.

The typical landlord practice on ban lists is comprised of four elements: (1) the landlord maintains a list of persons who are banned from the property; (2) the landlord (and police) furnish names to place on the list; (3) the landlord (or police, acting as landlord’s agent per an agreement) serve the banned person with a trespass notice; and
(4) the police enforce the ban by bringing criminal trespass charges for violations.

For entries not pursuant to a tenant’s invitation, generally the ban is permissible and, in those cases, a violation may give rise to a criminal trespass charge. See State
v. Wood, No. 03-CA-2901, 2004 WL 1631468 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., July 15, 2004).

For entries pursuant to a tenant’s invitation, where a lease provision authorizes the ban, the court decisions are split on whether the lease provision (and thus the ban) is permissible.

The rationale of the court decisions holding that the ban is not permissible in these circumstances is: “Under applicable property law, the owner sacrifices his possessory interests in the property, and he cannot prohibit a tenant from inviting guests into the tenant’s abode.” City of Kent v. Hermann, No. 95 -P-0042, 1996 WL 210780, at *2 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Mar. 8, 1996). Accord City of Columbus v. Parks, No. 10AP- 574, 2011 WL 1781911 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., May 5, 2011); State v. Hites, No. 1- 2000-22, 2000 WL 1114809 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., Aug. 8, 2000).

367 The rationale of the court decisions holding that the ban is permissible in these circumstances is: “[T]he tenant derived her rights as a tenant from DMHA [landlord] and, consequently, her rights to invite guests were subject to DMHA’s right to preclude certain guests by means of the criminal trespass policy,” which is part of the lease and thus qualifies the lease right to accommodate guests. State v. Scott, No. 19902, 2004 WL 103175, ¶ 18 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Jan. 23, 2004). See, e.g., State v. Blackford,
No. 2012-CA-00146, 2013 WL 1737858 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Apr. 22, 2013). See also Williams v. Nagel, 162 Ill. App.2d 542, 643 N.E.2d 816 (1994).

For entries pursuant to a tenant’s invitation, where the tenant’s lease does not authorize the ban, the ban is impermissible and invalid. Absent any restriction in the lease, the ban would violate the tenant’s common law right to invite guests, which is an implied provision in every lease or tenancy. See Section VII.G.1, above.

In some c ases, the determination on whether an entry is pursuant to a tenant’s invitation will depend on whether a tenant may grant a person a standing invitation for any date and time, in lieu of an explicit invitation for each entry.

Some ban policies exempt tenants’ guests, and thereby avoid any interference with the right of tenants to invite guests.

One court held, where a Public Housing ban policy encompassed tenants’ guests and the manager had unfettered discretion, that the ban policy was an unreasonable interference with the right of tenants, under 24 C.F.R. § 966.4(d)(1), to invite guests.

Diggs v. Hous. Auth. of City of Frederick, 67 F. Supp.2d 522 (D. Md. 1999) (enjoined the policy, except for the persons on the ban list due to violent or drug-related criminal activity on the PHA’s property).

Conflicting case law exists on whether a Public Housing ban policy may violate the First Amendment right of banned persons, especially relative to child rearing.

Compare Ash v. City of Clarksville , No. 3:03-0380, 2004 WL 5913273 (M.D. Tenn.
Sept. 3, 2004) (violated) with Brown v. Dayton Metro. Hous. Auth. , No. C-3-93-037, 1993 WL 1367433 (S.D. Ohio Aug. 26, 1993) (not violated), Commonwealth of Virginia v. Hicks, 267 Va. 573, 596 S.E.2d 74 (2004), and State v. Hites, No. 1-2000- 22, 2000 WL 1114809 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., Aug. 8, 2000). See also Johnson v. City of Cincinnati, 310 F.3d 484 (6th Cir. 2002) (held that the right of a grandmother to participate in the upbringing of her grandchildren is constitutionally prot ected and, to the extent thatThompson v. Ashe, 250 F.3d 399 (6th Cir. 2001), can be read to proclaim the absence of this right, it conflicts with Supreme Court precedent and therefore is not binding on the court).

Other issues concerning Public Housing ban policies include:
(1) Potential due process procedural issues relative to unclear standards, unclear notices as to the scope, basis, or appeal rights, lack of notice to tenants, appeal procedures, etc.; and
(2) A potential Fourth Amendment unreasonable search issue, as illustrated by the facts and decision in State v. Hayes , 188 S.W.2d 505 (Tenn.

2006).

For further discussion on Public Housing trespass policies, see: (1) No Trespass Policies – Hicks and Its Aftermath , 34 Hous. L. Bull. 129 (National Housing Law Project July 2004); (2) Gregory A. Beck, Ban Lists: Can Public Housing Authorities Have Unwanted Visitors Arrested , 2004 U. Ill. L. Rev. 1223 (2004); and (3) Elena Goldstein, Kept Out: Responding to Public Housing No -Trespass Policies, 38 Harv.

C.R.-C.L. L. Rev. 215 (2003).
