# 1. Definition

## Table-of-contents context

## Source text

Common law generally defines rent as consideration for the use and occupancy of real property for a specified term. Dayton Metro. Hous. Auth. v. McKee , 37 Ohio App.3d 102, 104, 524 N.E.2d 180 (2nd Dist. 1987) (“The ordinary meaning of the term ‘rent’ implies an amount paid as consideration for use or occupancy of property”);

Loveless v. Erie R.R., 2 Ohio App. 404 (11th Dist. 1914) (“Rent may be defined to be the consideration agreed to be paid by one party for the use and occupancy of the real estate of another”). See also M.E. Blatt Co. v. United States, 305 U.S. 267, 277 (1938) (“Rent is a fixed sum … to be paid at stated times for the use of property”); Velez v.

Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth., 795 F.3d 578, 585 (6th Cir. 2015) (“The definition of rent plainly includes the tenant’s total expense for the us e of land during the term of occupancy”);Aujero v. CDA TODCO, Inc., 756 F.2d 1374, 1376 (9th Cir. 1985) (“Rent normally connotes ‘consideration paid for use or occupation of property’”).

The specified term element of the definition of rent is well-established in the case law that also defines a lease. “A lease is a conveyance of an estate in real property for a limited term, with conditions attached, in consideration for rent.” Judson v.

Lyendecker, No. 12AP-615, 2013 WL 1187618, ¶ 9 (Ohio App., 10th Dis t., Mar. 21, 2013). Accord Amick v. Sickles, 177 Ohio App.3d 337, 894 N.E.2d 733, ¶ 19 (4th Dist.

2008); Fadelsak v. Hagley, No. 02-CA-41, 2003 WL 21489613, ¶ 11 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., June 25, 2003); Cuyahoga Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Watkins , 23 Ohio App.3d 20, 23, 491 N.E.2d 701 (8th Dist. 1984). See also State Nat’l Bank of El Paso v. United States, 509 F.2d 832, 835 (5th Cir. 1975) (“In black letter law, a lease is a transfer of an interest in and possession of property for a prescribed period of time in exchange for an agreed consideration, called ‘rent’”).

Under common law, when the consideration due from a tenant to a landlord is not for the use and occupancy of the real property for a specified term, it is a “miscellaneous charge.” Miscellaneous charges are charges for lease violations, for security deposits, and for special services. See Dayton Metro. Hous. Auth. v. McKee, 37 Ohio App.3d 102, 103, 524 N.E.2d 180 (2nd Dist. 1987) (unreasonable wear and tear penalties, late charges, and security deposits are miscellaneous charges, not rent);

Aujero v. CDA TODCO, Inc. , 756 F.2d 1374, 1376 (9th Cir. 1985) (mandatory meal plan charges (i.e., special services) are miscellaneous charges, not rent).

Generally, miscellaneous charges are not part of the rent. See Dayton Metro. Hous.
Auth. v. McKee, 37 Ohio App.3d 102, 103, 524 N.E.2d 180 (2nd Dist. 1987) (“we must determine if the retroactive payments [for past due rent] are to be considered rent or miscellaneous charges”).

A miscellaneous charge is part of the rent only if the lease so designates it. E.g., Strip Del., L.L.C. v. Landry’s Rests., Inc. , 191 Ohio App.3d 822, 947 N.E.2d 1233, ¶ 28 (5th Dist. 2010) (attorney’s fees part of the rent because the lease defined rent to include all charges under t he lease). See also In re Carol Janet, Inc. , 49 B.R. 847
(Bankr. E.D. Wis. 1985) (late payment charges and certain costs part of the rent because the lease defined rent to include all charges under the lease).
