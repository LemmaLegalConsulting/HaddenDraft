## Source text

The R.C. 1923.06 service of process rules purport to apply to any complaint that alleges an eviction claim. See R.C. 1923.06(A). This includes any complaint that alleges both an eviction claim and one or more non -eviction claims. See R.C.

1923.06(H)(2). The non-eviction claim is typically a monetary claim for nonpayment of rent and/or damage to the premises. See Section I.E.2.f, above.

The R.C. 1923.06 service of process rules establish a two-fold process. The rules require ordinary mail service and, at the landlord’s option, either bailiff service or certified mail, return receipt requested service.

Under the bailiff service, the bailiff makes one trip to the premises– (1) the bailiff tenders the summons and complaint to the tenant, or (2) if the bailiff cannot find the tenant, the bailiff leaves the summons and complaint with “a person of suitable age and discretion found at the premises,” or (3) if the bailiff cannot find the tenant or a person of suitable age and discretion at the premises, the bailiff posts the summons and complaint “in a conspicuous place on the subject premises.”30 R.C. 1923.06 service is deemed complete on the date that any of the following occurs: (1) bailiff service is made on the tenant, (2) bailiff service is made on another person at the premises, (3) bailiff service is made by posting, after ordinary mail service has been made, (4) ordinary mail service is made, after bailiff service has been made by posting, (5) certified mail service is mailed, if, as of the trial date for the eviction claim, the certified mail has not been returned for any reason othe r than refused or unclaimed, or (6) certified mail service is mailed, if, as of the trial date for the eviction claim, the certified mail has not been endorsed but the ordinary mail service has been mailed and has not been returned. R.C. 1923.06(G).

When the R.C. 1923.06(C) ordinary mail envelope is returned to the clerk of court due to failure o f delivery, the question arises whether this ordinary mail service is subject to the Civil Rule 4.6(D) provisions on perfecting ordinary mail service . At least three court decisions have addressed this issue: Don Ash Props. v. Dunno , No.

03AP-375, 2003 WL 22480323 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Nov. 4, 2003), Showe Mgmt.
Corp. v. Cunningham, 191 Ohio App.3d 123, 944 N.E.2d 1234 (10th Dist. 2011); and G.K.G. Builders, Inc. v. Burgess , 13 N.E.2d 745, 2014 -Ohio-2431 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., June 6, 2014).

In Dunno, the monetary claim, but not the eviction claim, was before the court of
appeals. The court of appeals assumed arguendo, without deciding, that the R.C.

1923.06 service of process rules applied to the monetary claim. It held that the R.C.

1923.06(C) ordina ry mail service is subject to the rules in Civil Rule 4.6(D) on perfecting ordinary mail service. Thereupon, it held that, because the R.C. 1923.06(C) ordinary mail service was returned to the clerk of court due to failure of delivery, it had not been perfected.

In Cunningham, both the eviction claim and the monetary claim were before the court of appeals. The court of appeals held that, with respect to the eviction claim, the R.C. 1923.06(C) ordinary mail service is not subject to the rules in Civil Ru le 4.6(D) on perfecting ordinary mail service. Thereupon, it held that R.C. 1923.06(C) ordinary mail service is perfected for the eviction claim when the summons and complaint are placed in the mail, notwithstanding any return of this mail service to the clerk of court due to failure of delivery (as in that case). 31 It distinguished Dunno as a ruling on a monetary claim, with dicta on the eviction claim, and held that the Dunno ruling remains good law for the monetary claim.32 In Burgess, the complaint alleged only an eviction claim.
