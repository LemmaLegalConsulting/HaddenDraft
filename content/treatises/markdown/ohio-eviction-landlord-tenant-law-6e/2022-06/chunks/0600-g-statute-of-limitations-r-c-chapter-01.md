5321 Claims The statute of limitations for a statutory claim, under a remedial statute that does not contain a statute of limitations, is six years under R.C. 2305.07. Cleveland Mobile Radio Sales, Inc. v. Verizon Wireless , 113 Ohio St.3d 394, 865 N.E.2d 1275, ¶ 7 (2007). R.C. Chapter 5321 is a remedial statute. See Section V.A, above.

The test for determining whether a claim is a statutory claim, for R.C. 2305.07 purposes, is set forth in McAlliffe v. W. States Import Co. , 72 Ohio St.3d 534, 651 N.E.2d 957, syllabus no. 1 (1995):

In order for a statutory cause of action to be “an action ... upon a liability created by statute” under R.C. 2305.07, that cause of action must be one that would not exist bu t for the statute. Any statutory “modification, alteration or conditioning” of a common-law cause of action which falls short of creating a previously unavailable cause of action is not “an action ... upon a liability created by statute.” Applying McAllife, courts have held that the statute of limitations for a tenant’s claim for bodily injury or injury to personal property, arising from a landlord’s R.C.

5321.04(A) violation, is two years under R.C. 2305.10 (and not six years under R.C.

2305.07). E.g., Shorter v. Neapolitan, 179 Ohio App.3d 608, 902 N.E.2d 1061 (7th
Dist. 2008); Segal v. Zehman-Wolf Mgmt., Inc., 139 Ohio App.3d 146, 743 N.E.2d 425 (8th Dist. 2000); Daniels v. Thompson , No. 49015, 1985 WL 7992 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Apr. 25, 1985); Craver v. Doogan, No. 2003 -CVC-00982, 2005 WL 4985282 (Ohio C.P., Clermont Cty., May 27, 2005). Cf. Pummill v. Carnes, No. 02-CA-2659, 2003 WL 896349 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Feb. 4, 2003) (R.C. 3733.10 (now R.C.

4781.38) violation, although court refers to R.C. 5321.04).

The R.C. 2305.10 statute of limitation applies to these claims “regardless of whether it is framed as a tort claim, a contract claim, or a warranty -of-habitability claim.” Shorter v. Neapolitan, 179 Ohio App.3d 608, 902 N.E.2d 1061, ¶ 18 (7th Dist.

2008).

The statute of limitations for a tenant’s R.C. 5321.04 contract claim for other damages (such as general damages) is eight years under R.C. 2305.06 for written rental agreements, and six years under R.C. 2305.07 for oral rental agreements. 389 Rental agreements are treated as contracts. See Section VII.A, above.

For written rental agreements, the statute of limitations for a landlord’s claim for damage to the rental premises is governed by R.C. 2305.06. Young v. McCleese, No.

97-CA-2351, 1998 WL 399861 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., July 13, 1998).

The statute of limitations for each of the remaining claims under R.C. Chapter 5321 is still to be determined. The remaining claims are those under R.C. 5321.02(B), 5321.05(C), 5321.07(B)(2), 5321.12, 5321.15(C), and 5321.16(C).

When an R.C. Chapter 5321 claim is ongoing, in month-to-month tenancies, each month constitutes a new contract from which the statute of limitations period runs.

Mozingo v. 2007 Gaslight Ohio, LLC, No. 27759, 2016 WL 3608751,¶ 22 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., July 6, 2016).

389 Generally, an oral rental agreement will be valid and enforceable only if it is taken out of the statute of frauds under the equitable doctrine of partial performance. See Section XXII.A.3, above.
