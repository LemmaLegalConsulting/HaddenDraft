# 3. Proof of Damage Amount

## Table-of-contents context

## Source text

When a landlord claims damages to the premises, the landlord must prove the damages or cost of restoration with reasonable certainty. Estie Inv. Co. v. Braff , No.

2017-L-172, 2018 WL 5433223 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Oct. 29, 2018); Gibson v.

Shephard, 87 N.E.3d 846, 2017 -Ohio-1157 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., 2017); Baker v.

Wade, No. 2002-CVG-15892 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Oct. 2, 2002). See generally Jam es H. Washington Ins. Agency v. Nationwide Mut. Ins. Co. , 95 Ohio App.3d 577, 643 N.E.2d 143 (8th Dist. 1993). This standard is not met where the landlord’s evidence is conjecture and speculation. Id.

This standard is illustrated by the followingfour cases in which the landlord failed to meet it.

In one case, this standard was not met where a landlord testified, relative to the cost of repairing broken windows, that he “thinks the cost will be around $1,000.00,” but he “failed to produce estimates, receipts or testimony to justify how he arrived at the above amount.” Baker v. Wade, No. 2002-CVG-15892, slip op. at 2 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Oct. 2, 2002).

In a second case, this standard was not met where the landlord introduced only photographs of the premises. Moore-El v. Petrella , No. 94749, 2011 WL 553877 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Feb. 17, 2011). The court held that “those photographs alone cannot supplant evidence of the actual costs in the form of bills or invoices for the alleged repairs, taking into consideration normal wear and tear during Moore -El’s tenancy.” Id., ¶ 11.

In a third case, this standard was not met where the landlord’s estimate to repair 539 and replace damaged drywall was not limited to the area of the hole that the tenant had
made. Vlcek v. Brogee, No. 25499, 2013 WL 5436930, ¶ 53 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist.,
Sept. 27, 2013). Rather, it was an estimate to repair and replace the drywall throughout the entire house. Id., ¶ 52.

In a fourth case, this standard was not met where: (1) the landlord produced evidence of the total cost of repairing eleven damage items, without a breakdown of the cost of each item; and (2) the court upheld the landlord’s damage claim for only seven of those items. Estie Inv. Co. v. Braff, No. 2017-L-172, 2018 WL 5433223, ¶ 44 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Oct. 29, 2018).
