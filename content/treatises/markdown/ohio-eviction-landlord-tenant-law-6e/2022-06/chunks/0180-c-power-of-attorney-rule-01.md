“When a person not admitted to the bar attempts to represent another in court on the basis of a power of attorney assigningpro se rights, he is in violation of thisstatute [R.C. 4705.01].” Office of Disciplinary Counsel v. Coleman, 88 Ohio St.3d 155, 158, 724 N.E.2d 402 (2000). The representations that this rule prohibits include commencing, maintaining, or defending any action or proceeding. Id. at 157-58.

This prohibition applies to eviction actions. Dabydeen v. Ortiz, No. 2013-CVG- 011770 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Oct. 29, 2013). In Ortiz, only the person with the power of attorney appeared on behalf of the landlord at the eviction hearing.

The court thereupon dismissed the eviction action for want of prosecution.
FF. Co-Tenant Versus Co-Tenant “Under Ohio law a co -tenant may not terminate the lease rights of another co - tenant.” Burpo v. Burpo , No. 2009 -CVG-7976 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., July 2, 2009). Cf. Collins v. Jackson, 34 Ohio App.3d 101, 517 N.E.2d 269 (8th Dist.
1986) (co-tenant owners of real property). Therefore, if one co -tenant brings an eviction action against the other co-tenant, a defense is the plaintiff co-tenant’s lack of authority to terminate the defendant co-tenant’s right of possession.

In Burpo, a mother and her adult daughter were co-tenants of a dwelling unit. The mother was the designated head of household, both the mother and the daughter had signed the lease, and the lease did not provide the mother with any occupancy right superior to the daughter. Therefore, on the mother’s eviction claim against the daughter, the court entered a judgment for the daughter.
GG. Statute of Limitations, R.C. 1923.01(B) R.C. 1923.01(B) establishes a two-year statute of limitations for eviction actions.

This two -year st atute of limitations begins to run when the right to commence the eviction action (i.e., the right to serve the R.C. 1923.04 notice to vacate) comes into
existence. Reese v. Webb, No. L-04-1257, 2005 WL 1009889 (Ohio App., 6th Dist.,
Apr. 29, 2005); Palmer v. O’Leary, No. 7745, 1975 Ohio App. LEXIS 7945 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Dec. 3, 1975).

For example, in an eviction action for nonpayment of rent, the two-year statute of limitations begins to run on the date that the tenant becomes delinquent in the tenant’s rental obligation, not on the date that the landlord serves the tenant with an R.C.

197 1923.04 notice to vacate. Id.

However, each month that a month-to-month tenant fails to pay rent, a new cause of action accrues. Causey v. Causey, No. 599, 1990 WL138564 (Ohio App., 4th Dist.,
Sept. 25, 1990). Therefore, if a month-to-month tenant fails to pay rent for a period of more than two years, the two-year statute of limitation is not a bar to an eviction action for nonpayment of rent that became due during the two -year period immediately preceding the filing of the action. Id.

In an eviction action for the failure of an assisted housing tenant to report an increase in income (see Section III.G.4, below), the two -year statute of limitation begins to run on the date that the landlord first became aware of this alleged fraud, not the date of any criminal conviction for it. Akron Metro. Hous. Auth. v. Sanders , No.

02-CVG-2692 (Ohio Mun., Akron, July 12, 2002),appeal dismissed, No. 21259 (Ohio App., 9th Dist., Mar. 10, 2003).

If a tenant does not plead this statute of limitation defense or assert it at trial, the tenant waives the limitation. Haas v. Gerski, 175 Ohio St. 327, 331, 194 N.E.2d 765 (1963). A tenant may not assert this defense for the first time in a motion for a new
trial. Id.
HH. Defense for Drug-Related R.C.

1923.02(A)(6) Eviction Under R.C. 1923.02(A)(6)(a), a landlord may evict a tenant for certain drug-related activity at the premises, if the landlord has “actual knowledge or reasonable c ause to believe” that the tenant or the tenant’s guest has engaged in such activity.

A landlord has this “actual knowledge or reasonable cause to believe” only if all of the elements of the R.C. 1923.02(A)(6)(a)(i) definition of that term are met. The definition is set forth in Section I.C.5, above.

A potential defense in these eviction actions is that one (or more) of the elements of the R.C. 1923.02(A)(6)(a)(i) definition of “actual knowledge or reasonable cause to believe” has not been met. The case law provides four examples of this defense.

First, if a search warrant has not been issued and executed for the drug -related activity, the landlord may not evict the tenant under R.C. 1923.02(A)(6)(a).162 Ezzard Charles Apts. v. Washington, No. 98-CV-28786 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., June 22, 1999). Cf. Liggins v. Westminster Arms, Inc., Nos. 98AP-377,378, 1998 WL 894613 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Dec. 22, 1998) (in granting landlord’s application for release of rent deposit, court held that landlord did not violate R.C. 5321.04(A)(9) because no search warrant had been executed at the neighbor’s unit).

Second, if a search warrant is executed for and a controlled substance is found at another rental unit in the building, but not the tenant’s rental unit, the landlord may not evict the tenant under R.C. 1923.02(A)(6)(a). Gorsuch Mgmt. v. Cole, No. 94-CVG- 511 (Ohio Mun., Lancaster, Sept. 22, 1994).

Third, if the execution of the search warrant produces no evidence of a controlled substance at the premises, the landl ord may not evict the tenant under R.C.

1923.02(A)(6)(a). Maliga Props. v. Williams , No. 92 -CVG-2998 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., Mar. 3, 1992).

Fourth, if the landlord fails to introduce into evidence the affidavit for the search warrant and the se arch warrant, the landlord may not evict the tenant under R.C.

1923.02(A)(6)(a). Vistula Mgmt. Co. v. Wells, No. L-00-1224, 2001 WL 279773 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Mar. 23, 2001). The requirement to introduce the affidavit and the warrant exists “[e]ven if the executing officer testified that the warrant complied with Crim.R. 41.” Id., 2001 WL 279773, at *4. Absent the introduction of the affidavit and the warrant, the court cannot determine whether the warrant complies with Criminal Rule 41, “given the fact that a warrant can be found to be so defective on its face that it is rendered invalid and therefore not in compliance with Crim.R. 41.” Id.
