The three-day period is not a seventy-two-hour period. Cooper v. Aberts, No. 98- CVG-384 (Ohio Mun., Mansfield, Feb. 25, 1998).

The case law is split on whether the three -day period is to be computed in accordance with Civil Rule 6(A).

Court decisions holding that Civil Rule 6(A) is applicable to the computation of this three -day period include: Ebbing v. Mathis , No. CA2012 -10-201, 2013 WL 2423702 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., June 3, 2013);Hudgins v. Campbell, No. 2013-CVG- 00086 (Ohio Mun., Elyria, Mar. 11, 2013); Midtown Apts. v. Davis , 20 Ohio B. 128, 1985 WL 6044 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., 1985); GMS Mgmt. Co., Inc. v. Alnazer , No.

11-CVG-02425 (Ohio Mun., Berea, Nov. 4, 2011);Phillips v. Derringer, No. 05-CVG- 000657 (Ohio Mun., Licking Cty., Apr. 12, 2005); Vogt v. Riley, No. 04-CVG-00829 (Ohio Mun., Athens Cty., Nov. 17, 2004); Powers v. Arnold , No. 03 -CVG-01890 (Ohio Mun., Fairfield, Aug. 26, 2003); McCallister v. Pickrel , No. 02 -CVG-00268 (Ohio Mun., Licking Cty., Mar. 12, 2002);Labaki v. Carter, No. 96-CVG-00558 (Ohio Mun., Zanesville, July 1, 1996); Will v. Starcher , No. 87 -CL-254686 (Ohio Mun., Springfield, July 11, 1987); Northpointe Apts. v. Dooley, No. M85-CVG-3909 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., Apr. 8, 1985).

Court decisions holding that Civil Rule 6(A) is not applicable to the computation of this three -day period include: Faqi v. Patti , Nos. L -19-1038,1107, 2020 WL 6375793 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Oct. 30, 2020); Wodzisz v. Bayes, No. 96-APE07-891, 1997 WL 142715 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Mar. 25, 1997); Fed. Prop. Mgmt. v.

Daugherty, No. 12591, 1991 WL 116640 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., June 28, 1991);Willis
v. Thibault , No. 9 -87-47, 1989 WL 83816 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., July 31, 1989);

Pleasant View Mobile Home Court, Inc. v. Prichard, No. 82-CVG-1881 (Ohio Mun., Canton, May 28, 1982). See also State ex rel GMS Mgmt. Co., Inc. v. Lazzaro , No.

97875, 2012 WL 3765055 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Aug. 24, 2012) (court finds petitioner made a “strong argument” for the inapplicability of Civil Rule 6(A) but denies a writ of prohibition on two grounds: an appeal is an adequate remedy at law; and petitioner is essentially seeking a declaratory judgment via the writ).

If Civil Rule 6(A) is not applicable to the computation of this three-day period, the computation is nonetheless governed by R.C. 1.14. Midtown Apts. v. Davis, 20 Ohio
B. 128, 1985 WL 6044 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., 1985); Wayland v. Plymire , No. 03 - CVG-987 (Ohio Mun., Barberton, July 9, 2003). Cf. Dennis v. Coble, 18 Ohio App.

62 (6th Dist. 1923) (applying General Code § 10216). Under R.C. 1.14, this three-day period is computed by excluding the first day, including the last day, and excluding any Sunday or holiday that would otherwise be the last day.

19 Consequently, for this computation, the only significant difference between R.C.

1.14 and Civil Rule 6(A) is that, unlike Civil Rule 6(A), R.C. 1.14 does not exclude every Saturday and Sunday.
