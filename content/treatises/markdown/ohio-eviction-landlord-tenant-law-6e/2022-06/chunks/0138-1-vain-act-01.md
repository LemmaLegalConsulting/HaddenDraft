# 1. Vain Act

## Source text

Generally, the law does not require a party to engage in a vain act. Showe Mgmt.
Corp. v. Moore, No. 08-CA-10, 2009 WL 1372986, ¶ 42 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., May 14, 2009).

This general principle of law applies to a tenant’s obligation to pay rent. Id. “[A] tenant’s obligation under a rental agreement to pay or tender rent can be excused where 145 the landlord, through its conduct, renders the requirement to tender meaningless, vain, or useless.” Id. Accord Pinewood Gardens Apts. v. Whiteside, No. 25989, 2014 WL 2169720, ¶ 17 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., May 23, 2014); Greenbriar Estates v. Eberle , 130 Ohio Misc.2d 11, 14-15, 824 N.E.2d 172 (Ohio Mun., Clermont Cty., 2005).E.g., Century Pacific Hous. P’ship v. Jeffries , No. 21 -CVG-4124 (Ohio Mun., Dayton, Oct.19, 2021).

In Moore, applying this principle, the court held that the tenant’s failure to tender rent in Dec. 2007 was excused, based on the landlord’s refusal to accept the tenant’s tender of rent during the grace period (but after the due date) each month from June to November 2007.

In Whiteside, applying this principle, the court held that t he tenant’s failure to tender rent for August 2013 was excused because of the owner’s policy of not accepting rent payments during the pendency of their security deposit litigation, “which would have made Whiteside’s tender of payment a vain act.” 2014 WL 2169720, ¶ 17.

In Eberle, applying this principle, the court held that the tenant’s failure to tender rent for August 2004 was excused, based on: (1) the tenant’s visit to the management office with a check book and an intent to pay rent for August; (2) the manager’s statement at that time that the tenant’s rent payment for August would not be accepted until the tenant coated the roof of his mobile home; (3) the tenant’s decision not to tender the August rent as a result of that statement; and (4) the absence of authority for the landlord to condition the tenant’s rent payment on whether the tenant coated the roof.
