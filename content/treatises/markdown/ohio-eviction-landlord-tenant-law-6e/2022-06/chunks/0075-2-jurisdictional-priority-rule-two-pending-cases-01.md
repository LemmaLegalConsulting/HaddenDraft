# 2. Jurisdictional-Priority Rule – Two Pending Cases

## Source text

When two cases involving the same parties and the same causes of action are pending in different courts and those courts have concurrent jurisdiction, the court first acquiring jurisdiction of the claim retains it to the exclusion of the other court. State ex rel. Dunlap v. Sarko , 135 Ohio St.3d 171, 985 N.E.2d 450 (2013); State ex rel.

Brady v. Pianka, 106 Ohio St.3d 147, 832 N.E.2d 1202 (2005); State ex rel. Sellers v.

Gerken, 72 Ohio St.3d 115, 647 N.E.2d 807 (1995); State ex rel. Judson v. Spahr, 33 Ohio St.3d 111, 515 N.E.2d 911 (1987); State ex rel. Phillips v. Polcar, 50 Ohio St.2d 279, 364 N.E.2d 33 (1977);Lewis v. Harding, 182 Ohio App.3d 588, 913 N.E.2d 1048 (8th Dist. 2009).

“[T]he jurisdictional-priority rule can apply even when the causes of a ction and relief requested are not exactly the same, as long as the actions present part of the same ‘whole issue.’” State ex rel. Dunlap v. Sarko , 135 Ohio St.3d 171, 985 N.E.2d 450, ¶ 10 (2013).

However, the jurisdictional -priority rule “does not apply when the court first securing in personam jurisdiction is unable to afford the plaintiff all the relief that he 55 However, if “the counterclaim [does] not coherently state sufficient facts to ascertain the basis for the claim or the monetary relief requested,” a municipal court (or county court) might not abuse its discretion by receiving “evidence relating to defendant’s counterclaim prior to making any determination concerning certification in the case.” Lewallen v. Mentor Lagoons, Inc., 85 Ohio App.3d 91, 96, 619 N.E.2d 98 (8th Dist. 1993).

or she is legally or equitably entitled because its powers are limited or defective.” Adams Robinson Enters. v. Envirologix Corp., 111 Ohio App.3d 426, 430, 676 N.E.2d 560 (2nd Dist. 1996). Accord Scott v. Dohse, 194 Ohio App.3d 364, 956 N.E.2d 363 (2nd Dist. 2011).

For purposes of the jurisdictional -priority rule, the determination of which court first acquired jurisdiction is based on the date of completion of proper service, not the date of filing of the complaint. State ex rel. Balson v. Harnishfeger, 55 Ohio St.2d 38, 377 N.E.2d 750 (1978); Adams Robinson Enters. v. Envirologix Corp. , 111 Ohio App.3d 426, 676 N.E.2d 560 (2nd Dist. 1996); State ex rel. Cook v. Court of Common Pleas of Marion County , 28 Ohio App.3d 82, 502 N.E.2d 245 (3rd Dist. 1986). But see Mark I. Wallach & Anthony J. LaCerva, Instant Replay of the Race to the Courthouse: Ohio’s Rule of Priority of Jurisdiction Confronts the Civil Rules, 2 Ohio Civil Practice Journal 61 (1991) (contending that Balson is incorrectly decided and that, under the Civil Rules, this determination should be based on the date of filing of the complaint).

The jurisdictional-priority rule applies to eviction actions. For example, when a defendant in a declaratory judgment action pending in a court of common pleas files an eviction counterclaim and thereafter files an eviction action in a municipal court, involving the same parties and subject matter, the court of common pleas acquires the right to adjudicate the eviction action to the exclusion of the municipal court. C & C
Invs. v. Mackie , No. 87AP -183, 1987 WL 31262 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Dec. 21, 1987).

Likewise, if the vendor of a land installment contract brings an action in a court of common pleas seeking forfeiture of the contract, during the pendency of that act ion, the vendor cannot maintain a subsequent eviction action against the vendee in a municipal court. Stratton v. Robey , 70 Ohio App.2d 4, 433 N.E.2d 938 (10th Dist.

1980).

Also, when a tenant commences an action in a court of common pleas seeking declaratory and injunctive relief with respect to the tenant’s entitlement to continued occupancy of the premises, during the pendency of that action, the landlord arguably cannot commence an eviction action in a municipal court. Compare Lacheta v. Hall,
No. 8-90-CVG-494 (Ohio Mun., New Philadelphia, Oct. 22, 1990) (municipal court without jurisdiction in this circumstance)with State ex rel. Charron-Kofta v. Corrigan,
No. 69434, 1995 WL 603157 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Oct. 12, 1995) (writ of prohibition denied bec ause whether municipal court has jurisdiction in this circumstance is 73 unclear).

However, an action to quiet title in a court of common pleas does not deprive a municipal court of jurisdiction to hear an eviction action involving the same parties and property because the two causes of action involve different subject matters. Haas
v. Gerski, 175 Ohio St. 327, 194 N.E.2d 765 (1963); Hicks v. Holland , No. 53363, 1987 WL 17217 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Sept. 17, 1987).See also State ex rel. Carpenter
v. Warren Mun. Court, 61 Ohio St.2d 208, 400 N.E.2d 391 (1980). Aneviction action only determines which of the parties has a present right to possession (see Section I.A.2, above), whereas an action to quiet title determines which of the parties has a present right to title. Haas, 175 Ohio St. at 330. Cf. Disher v. Bannick, No. 28903, 2021 WL 1499975 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Apr. 16, 2021) (a judgment in an eviction action cannot, under res judicata, bar an action to quiet title).

Similarly, a collateral action b ringing title to real property into question – a declaratory judgment action as to an alleged settlement agreement – is not the same cause of action as an eviction claim and, therefore, the jurisdictional-priority rule does not apply. State ex rel. Bradyv. Pianka, 106 Ohio St.3d 147, 832 N.E.2d 1202 (2005).
