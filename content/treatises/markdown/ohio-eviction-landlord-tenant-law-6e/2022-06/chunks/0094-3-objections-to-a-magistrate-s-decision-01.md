But query whether this Civil Rule 53(D)(3)(b)(iv) provision should apply to eviction actions.

The purpose of the provision is to disallow appellate review where the alleged error was not called to the attention of the trial court at a time when the trial court could have corrected the
error. See Proctor v. Proctor, 48 Ohio App.3d 55, 58, 548 N.E.2d 287 (3rd Dist. 1988). In a n eviction action, timely objections do not automatically stay execution of the judgment (see Section I.N.4, below) and, therefore, do not necessarily call the error to the attention of the trial court at a time when it can take meaningful corrective action, e.g., prevent the tenant’s eviction.

Compare Civil Rule 53(D)(3)(b)(i) (objections to be filed within fourteen days of the filing of the magistrate’s decision)with R.C. 1923.14 (writ of restitution to be executed within ten days after its issuance, which often occurs within a day of the filing of the magistrate’s decision). In Kettering Square Apts. v. Crawford, Nos. 27504,27545,27548, 2017 WL 6398837, ¶ 10 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Dec. 15, 2017), the court summarizes well why “[t]he objection provision s in Civ.R. 53 create a procedural quagmire when applied in a forcible entry and detainer proceeding….” But the court opts not to decide whether they apply to FED actions because, in the context of that case, the error would be harmless. Given that the requirement to file objections applies to FED actions, the Civil Rule 53(D)(3)(b)(i) time period for filing objections – “[w]ithin fourteen days of the filing of the magistrate’s decision”– must likewise apply to eviction actions.

Furthermore, this fourteen-day period does not interfere with the summary nature of eviction proceedings. See generally Miele v. Ribovich, 90 Ohio St.3d 439, 443-44, 739 N.E.2d 333 (2000). Under Civil Rule 53(D)(4)(e)(i), the court may enter a judgment during this fourteen -day period.75 And, in evict ion actions, the automatic stay provision of Civil Rule 53(D)(4)(e)(i) does not apply. See Section I.N.4, below.

“An objection to a factual finding, whether or not specifically designated as a finding of fact under Civ.R. 53(D)(3)(a)(ii), shall be supported by a transcript of all the evidence submitted to the magistrate relevant to that finding or an affidavit of that evidence if a transcript is not available.” Civil Rule 53(D)(3)(b)(iii). “If an affidavit is employed, it must purport to comprise all the relevant evidence submitted to the referee [now magistrate] on the issue, instead of just the evidence the objecting party believes was disregarded.” Pappenhagen v. Payne , 48 Ohio App.3d 176, 178, 549 N.E.2d 208 (8th Dist. 1988).
