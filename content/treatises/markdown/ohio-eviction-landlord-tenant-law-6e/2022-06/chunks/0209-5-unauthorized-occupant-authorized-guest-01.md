Assisted housing tenants have a right to provide their guests with reaso nable
accommodations. E.g., 24 C.F.R. § 966.4(d)(1). However, they may not permit a person to become an occupant of the premises without management authorization.E.g., subsidized housing lease,204 ¶ 13.

The lease identifies the permissible occupants of the premises. Subsidized housing 204 For the definition of “subsidized housing lease,” see note 174, above.

lease, ¶ 13 (project -based subsidized housing); 24 C.F.R. § 982.551(h) (HCVP); 24 C.F.R. § 966.4(d)(1) (Public Housing).

If an assisted housing tenant permits a person to become an unauthorized occupant of the premises, it is good cause to terminate the tenancy. E.g., Streetsboro Green
Apts. v. Rundo , No. 90 -P-2145, 1990 WL 174140 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Nov. 9, 1990).

However, HUD Handbook 4350.3 REV -1,205 ¶ 8 -13(A)(4) categorizes an unauthorized occupant violation as a min or violation of the lease. Therefore, at least for project-based subsidized housing, an unauthorized occupant is grounds to terminate a tenancy only if it is a repeated violation (see Section III.G.12) and it adversely affects the development under one (o r more) of the factors specified in 24 C.F.R.

§ 247.3(c)(2).

For a discussion on guest issues and the related law (including law that applies only to assisted housing), see Section VII.G, below. Those issues concern registration and/or prior approval po licies for overnights guests or short -term visitors, the matter of when a short -term visitor becomes an unauthorized occupant, a tenant’s right to invite guests, and the interplay between that right and a landlord’s ban of certain persons.
