# B. R.C. 5321.04(B) Remedies

## Table-of-contents context

- X. Tenant Claims for Improper Entry,
  - B. R.C. 5321.04(B) Remedies

## Source text

If a landlord enters upon the premises in violation of R.C. 5321.04(A)(8) or 5321.04(B), under R.C. 5321.04(B), the tenant may bring an action against the landlord for actual damages, injunctive relief, and reasonable attorney’s fees, or the tenant may terminate the rental agreement.

R.C. 5321.04(B) expressly authorizes injunctive relief. “[W]hen astatute grants a specific injunctive remedy to an individual or to the state, the party requesting the injunction ‘need not aver and show, as under ordinary rules in equity, that great or irreparable injury is about to be done for which he has no adequate remedy at law.’” Ackerman v. Tri-City Geriatric & Health Care, Inc., 55 Ohio St.2d 51, 56, 378 N.E.2d 145 (1978).

An implied requirement of the R.C. 5321.04(B) right to terminate the rental agreement is that, when exercised, the right must be exercised within a reasonable time.

Limage v. Citiscene Props., No. 92AP-190, 1992 WL 131840, at *2 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., June 9, 1992). See also T.K.D. Enters. v. Zimmermann , No. 97 -CA-44, 1998 WL 380496, at *5 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., July 2, 1998).

One court has created two additional implied requirements for exercising this right to terminate the rental agreement.T.K.D. Enters. v. Zimmermann, No. 97-CA-44, 1998 WL 380496, at *4 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., July 2, 1998). They are: (1) tenant service upon the landl ord of a written notice of the first unlawful entry, and (2) thereafter a second unlawful entry. Id.

Examples of court decisions awarding actual damages under R.C. 5321.04(B) for unlawful entries include: Helfrich v. Mellon, No. 06-CA-69, 2007 WL 1881537(Ohio App., 5th Dist., June 27, 2007) ($2,500 compensatory damages);Fisher v. Barker, 159 Ohio App.3d 745, 825 N.E.2d 244 (2nd Dist. 2005) ($500 compensatory damages – not reviewed or modified on appeal); Blausey v. Frase , No. 06 -CVG-00748 (Ohio Mun., Licking Cty., Feb. 28, 2007) ($500 compensatory damages and $5,000 punitive damages).

Where a landlord has violated R.C. 5321.04(A)(8) or 5321.04(B), and the tenant has not suffered actual damages as a result of the violation, the tenant is still entitled to recover nominal damages. Woodrow v. McGuire, No. 1740, 1989 WL 72799, at *5 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., June 27, 1989). See also Meacham v. Miller, 79 Ohio App.3d 35, 40, 606 N.E.2d 996 (4th Dist. 1992) (“A court will award nominal damages if a tenant cannot prove actual damages flowing from a landlord’s unlawful behavior”).
Cf. Gordon v. Morris, No. 2000-CA-69, 2001 WL 85797, at *6 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist.,
Feb. 2, 2001) (“‘Actual damages’ is a term ‘often used to denote either ‘compensatory damages’ or ‘nominal damages’”).

In an R.C. 5321.04(B) claim, the right of the tenant to recover reasonable attorney’s fees is contingent only on the entry of a judgment on the merits in favor of the tenant. See R.C. 5321.04(B). See also Iwenofu v. Consol. Mgmt., Inc. , 49 Ohio App.3d 33, 35, 550 N.E.2d 505 (8th Dist. 1988) (“R.C. 5321.04 will not justify an award of attorney fees unless the tenant ‘obtains a judgment’ for such conduct”). This prerequisite is met regardless of whether the judgment is for injunctive relief, actual damages, or nominal damages. E.g., Woodrow v. McGuire , No. 1740, 1989 WL 72799, at *5 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., June 27, 1989) (nominal damages only).

In Woodrow, the tenant proved an R.C. 5321.04(A)(8) violation, but not actual
damages. The tenant’s vacation of the premises made injunctive relief unavailable.

The court held that the tenant was “entitled to nominal damages and that portion of attorney’s fees which correspond to litigating the unauthorized entry.”Woodrow, 1989 WL 72799, at *5.

Three court decisions erroneously hold that a tenant may recover R.C. 5321.04(B) attorney’s fees only if the tenant has suffered actual damages as a result of the landlord’s unlawful entry. DuJardin v. Owens, No. 87-B-19, 1988 WL 109086 (Ohio App., 7th Dist., Sept. 22, 1988); Geyer v. Frank, No. CA84-06-074, 1985 WL 8144 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., Jan. 14, 1985); Dyche Real Estate Fund v. Graves , 55 Ohio App.2d 153, 380 N.E.2d 767 (10th Dist. 1978).

These holdings are erroneous because they ignore the fact that, on an R.C.

5321.04(B) claim, a court may enter a judgment on the merits in favor of the tenant 459 without finding or awarding actual damages. The judgment may grant only “injunctive relief to prevent the recurrence of the conduct.” R.C. 5321.04(B). Or the judgment may award only nominal damages. E.g., Woodrow v. McGuire, No. 1740, 1989 WL 72799, at *5 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., June 27, 1989). In either case, the landlo rd made an entry in violation of R.C. 5321.04(A)(8) or 5321.04(B) and, therefore, “the tenant may ... obtain a judgment for reasonable attorney’s fees.” R.C. 5321.04(B).

When an action includes an R.C. 5321.04(B) claim and one or more other claims, the computation of the R.C. 5321.04(B) attorney’s fees is based only on the time spent on the R.C. 5321.04(B) claim “or on claims interrelated thereto.” Helfrich v. Mellon,
No. 06-CA-69, 2007 WL 1881537, ¶ 73 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., June 27, 2007). See also Iwenofu v. Consol. Mgmt., Inc. , 49 Ohio App.3d 33, 550 N.E.2d 505 (8th Dist.

1988). The interrelated claims include common law privacy, trespass, and quiet enjoyment claims arising from the statutory notice violation. Helfrich v. Mellon, 2007 WL 1881537, ¶ 73.
