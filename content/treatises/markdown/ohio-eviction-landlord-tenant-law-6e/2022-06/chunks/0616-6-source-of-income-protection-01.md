Municipalities across Ohio have broadened their fair housing ordinances to include source of income (“SOI”)as a protected class. Those ordinances (and municipalities) include: Akron OH Municipal Code tit. 3, ch. 38; Athens City Code tit. 3, § 3.07.61;

Bexley Codified Ordinances pt. 6, ch. 637; Cincinnati Codified Ordinances tit. 7, ch.

740; Cleveland Heights Codified Ordinances pt. 7, ch. 749; Columbus Code of Ordinances tit. 45, ch. 4551, § 4551.03; Linndale Codified Ordinances pt. 5, ch. 515;

Reynoldsburg Codified Ordinances pt. 5, ch. 503; South Euclid Codified Ordinances
pt. 14, ch. 1408; Toledo Municipal Code pt. 5, ch. 554; University Heights Codified Ordinances pt. 8, tit. 2, ch. 820; Warrensville Heights Codified Ordinances pt. 1, tit. 1,
ch. 113; Westerville Codified Ordinancespt. 5, ch. 511; Wickliffe Codified Ordinances
pt. 11, ch. 1103; Worthington Codified Ordinances pt. 5, ch. 539; Yellow Springs OH Codified Ordinances pt. 6, ch. 626.

These SOI ordinances generally prohibit landlords from discriminating against tenants based on the tenant’s source of lawful income. Examples of those protected sources of income include: welfare assistance, child support, financial aid from any person or from any federal, state, or local government program, and social security disability payments.

Most of these SOI ordinances explicitly include rental assistance in their examples of protected sources of income. They are the ordinances enacted in Akron (“housing assistance, housing vouchers, emergency rental assistance”), Athens (“rent vouchers”), Bexley (“rent vouchers”), Cleveland Heights (“housing vouchers, emergency rental assistance”), Columbus (“rent vouchers”), Reynoldsburg (“rent vouchers”), Toledo (“Section 8 housing assistance payments or any other rent subsidy or rent assistance program”), University Heights (“rent vouchers”), Westerville (“emergency rental assistance”), Worthington (“emergency rental assistance”), Yellow Springs (“rental assistance”).

The other SOI ordinances implicitly include rental assistance in their protecte d sources of income. Rental assistance falls within the general examples of protected sources of income that most of these ordinances use, such as all forms of federal, state, or local government assistance payments or subsidies. Also, rental assistance is, in effect, a source of income.

These SOI ordinances vary in their procedures for asserting a claim for an alleged violation of this prohibition. Some permit the claim to be filed only in their administrative complaint procedure. E.g., Akron, Athens, Bexley, Cleveland Heights, Columbus, Linndale, Reynoldsburg, Warrensville Heights, Westerville, Wickliffe, and Worthington ordinances. Some establish a private right of action. E.g., Cincinnati
ordinance. And some authorize both an administrative comp laint and a private right of action. E.g., University Heights and Yellow Springs ordinances.

629 These SOI ordinances also vary in the penalties that they impose for a violation of this prohibition. The penalties range among injunctive relief, damages, civil penalties, and/or attorney’s fees.

For a nationwide list of the states, counties, and cities that have enacted SOI laws, see the National Multifamily Housing Council website, at www.nmhc.org/research- insight/analysis-and-guidance/source-of-income-laws-by-state-county-and-city.
