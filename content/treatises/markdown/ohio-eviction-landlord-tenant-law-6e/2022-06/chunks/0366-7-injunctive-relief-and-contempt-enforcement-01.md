# 7. Injunctive Relief, and Contempt Enforcement

## Table-of-contents context

- IX. Tenant Claims for Defective
  - A. R.C. 5321.04 Contract Claim
    - 7. Injunctive Relief, and Contempt Enforcement

## Source text

In an R.C. 532 1.04 contract claim, the tenant may secure injunctive relief under Civil Rule 65 to enjoin the landlord from failing to remedy the R.C. 5321.04 violation.

E.g., Medi Invs. LLC v. Mitchell, No 2020-CVG-009347 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous.

Div., Dec. 11, 2020) (landlord “ordered to make any and all necessary repairs to correct the mechanical failure causing a lack of heat, or to replace if necessary any and all heating elements designed to distribute the heat to the premises”);Woods v. Coleman,
No. 01-CVG-444 (Ohio Mun., Jackson Cty., Apr. 26, 2001) (preliminary injunction enjoining landlord from refusing to make the repairs necessary to provide the premises with running water, hot water, and a working toilet).

The injunctive relief will be similar to an R. C. 5321.07(B)(2) order directing the landlord to remedy the defective condition. See Section VIII.H, above. However, unlike an R.C. 5321.07(B)(2) claim, when seeking injunctive relief, the tenant may be required to address irreparable harm and bond issues.

An injunction order (or settlement) is usually most effective if it designates with specificity the repairs and services that the landlord is required to perform, and the date by which each repair and service shall be completed. For examples of such orders (or settlements), see Hardin v. Meyer , No. CVJ -94-00160 (Ohio Mun., Toledo, Hous.

Div., Feb. 14, 1997) (settlement); Mullins v. Vonderhaar , No. 87 -CV-07154 (Ohio Mun., Hamilton Cty., May 27, 1987) (order).

For settlement agreements, the tenant’s interests are best protected if the agreement is filed with the court as a stipulation or entry and signed by the judge. This will enable the tenant to enforce the terms of the agreement through civil contempt proceedings, if
necessary. E.g., Hardin v. Meyer, No. CVJ-94-00160 (Ohio Mun., Toledo, Hous. Div.,
Feb. 14, 1997), aff’d, No. L-97-1060, 1998 WL 636774 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Sept.

18, 1998); Lakota v. McElrath, No. 2014-CVG-003743 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous.

Div., Dec. 9, 2014).

“A trial court may make a finding of contempt even if the party’s failure to comply with the order was not purposeful, willful, or intentional.” Hardin v. Meyer, No. L- 97-1060, 1998 WL 636774, at *6 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Sept. 18, 1998). “It is no defense to a finding of contempt that a party acted in good faith and not in intentional disregard of a court order.” Id.

“A court may punish disobedience of its order, pursuant to R.C. 2705.02(A) or the court’s inherent power to enforce its authority.” Id.

“[T]he power to punish for contempt is inherent in the courts.” First Bank of Marietta v. Mascrete, Inc. , 125 Ohio App.3d 257, 265, 708 N.E.2d 262 (4th Dist.

1998). “Compensation of the injured party has been a traditional function of civil contempt.” Id. Accord Oak Hill Banks v. Ison , No. 03 -CA-5, 2003 WL 22386806 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Oct. 15, 2003). “[A] trial court may award damages and attorney fees as compensation for losses which occurred as a direct result of the contempt.” Hardin, 1998 WL 636774, at *7. Accord Ison, 2003 WL 22386806, ¶ 29; Mascrete, 125 Ohio App.3d at 265.

If a court elects to award damages and attorney’s fees under its inherent authority, the limitation of R.C. 2705.05 does not apply. Hardin, 1998 WL 636774, at *7. The only limitation of R.C. 2705.05 is that, in civil contempt proceedings, it permits courts to impose fines in specified amounts.
