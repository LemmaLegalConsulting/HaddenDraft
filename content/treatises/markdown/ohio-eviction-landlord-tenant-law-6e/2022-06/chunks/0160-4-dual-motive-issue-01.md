# 4. Dual Motive Issue

## Source text

A common issue in retaliatory eviction cases is the “dual motive” issue. The issue arises when a landlord is evicting a tenant for two or more reasons and one of them is
retaliation. The issue is, in such circumstances, to what extent, if any, may the landlord’s motivation be retaliation without violating the law.

An early Ohio decision on the dual motive issue is Gateway Apts. v. Darrah, Nos.

76-CVG-725,928 (Ohio Mun., Lyndhurst, Aug. 22, 1977). It held that R.C. 5321.02 is violated whenever the retaliation is a resultant factor in the decision to terminate the
tenancy. Id.

Other jurisdictions provide a broad spectrum of responses to this issue. See Comment, Landlord-Tenant: Proving Motive in Retaliatory Eviction – Minnesota’s Solution, 61 Minn. L. Rev. 523 (1977); Comment, Prohibition of Retaliatory Eviction in Landlord-Tenant Relations: A Study of Practice and Proposals, 54 N.C.L. Rev. 861, 871-76 (1976). At one end, courts have held that retaliation must be the sole motive.

E.g., Dickhut v. Norton , 45 Wis.2d 389, 173 N.W.2d 297 (1970). At the other end, courts have held that any retaliatory motive is sufficient to e stablish the retaliation
defense. E.g., Silberg v. Lipscomb, 117 N.J. 491, 285 A.2d 86 (1971).141 An Oregon Supreme Court decision on this dual motive issue – Elk Creek Mgmt.
Co. v. Gilbert, 353 Or. 565, 303 P.3d 929 (2013)– may be the most instructive decision for determining this issue for Ohio. Ohio and Oregon have similar retaliation provisions, and both are modeled on the Uniform Residential Landlord -Tenant Act (“URLTA”).

In Gilbert, the Court analyzed in detail the URLTA retaliation provision, rel ative to the dual motive issue, including the URLTA comments and the case law relied on in drafting the provision. It thereupon held that the proper test is a “but for” test. Under this test, a tenant “may prevail by proving that the tenant’s protected a ctivity was a ‘material and substantial’ factor in the landlord’s decision. The tenant need not prove that the tenant’s protected activity was the ‘sole’ or ‘dominant’ reason for the landlord’s decision.” Gilbert, 353 Or. at 584-85.
