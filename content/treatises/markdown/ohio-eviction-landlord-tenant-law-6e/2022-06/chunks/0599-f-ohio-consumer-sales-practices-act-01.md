# F. Ohio Consumer Sales Practices Act

## Source text

The Ohio Consumer Sales Practices Act (“CSPA”), R.C. Chapter 1345, does not apply to residential lease transactions. Heritage Hills, Ltd. v. Deacon, 49 Ohio St.3d 80, 551 N.E.2d 125 (1990). See also Mesnick v. De Ville Apts., No. 71517, 1997 WL 599376 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Sept. 25, 1997); Hartman v. Garden Woods Apts., No.

15228, 1995 WL 628021 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Oct. 25, 1995).

However, the CSPA may apply to the service, between a submeter company and a tenant, for furnishing a rental unit with utility service under a submeter system. See Sliwinski v. Capital Props. Mgmt. Ltd. , No. CV2006 -02-0884, 2007 WL 5843246 (Ohio C.P., Summit Cty., Dec. 11, 2007) (in denying motion to dismiss CSPA claim, “Court finds first that the sale of water and sewage may be an action unrelated to the landlord-tenant relationship, and therefore is not necessarily preempted by the landlord-tenant act”).388 Cf. Nelson v. Am. Power & Light, No. 2:08-cv-549, 2010 WL 3219498 (S.D. Ohio Aug. 12, 2010) (motion to dismiss CSPA claims denied; per lease, tenant maintained electric service with utility company, which had agreement with landlord for commencing eviction upon any tenant delinquency of 60 days on the utility account).

Under these third-party billing systems, typically, the submeter company installs and maintains the submeter, reads the submeter, and bills and collects payments from the tenant for the utility service. The tenant remits payments for the utility service for the rental unit to the submeter company, not the landlord or utility company.

The tenant ’s residential lease incorporates the service – the delivery/payment system between the submeter company and the tenant – and permits the landlord to evict the tenant for any nonpayment of the tenant’s payment obligation to the submeter
company. A separat e contract is executed between the submeter company and the landlord.

While the CSPA does not apply to “pure” real estate transactions, it applies to “mixed” transactions, which involve a transfer of both real property and either a service or personal property. Brown v. Liberty Clubs, Inc., 45 Ohio St.3d 191, 543 N.E.2d 783 (1989). While the Heritage Hills decision was issued after the Brown decision, Ohio appellate courts have continued to follow the holding in Brown, often in cases closer to a pure real estate transaction than the incorporation of the third-party billing system 388 See note 387, above.

615 into the residential lease.

For example, courts consistently have held that the construction of a new home constitutes a “service” within the meaning of the CSPA, even though the CSPA does not apply to the accompanying purchase of real property. See, e.g., Fine v. U.S. Erie Islands Co., No. OT-07-048, 2009 WL 826526 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Mar. 31, 2009);

Morrison v. Skestos, No. 04AP-244, 2004 WL 2944159 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., De c.

21, 2004); Kieber v. Spicer Constr. Co., 85 Ohio App.3d 391, 619 N.E.2d 1105 (2nd
Dist. 1993).

A residential lease that incorporates a third-party billing system for a utility service is arguably a “mixed” transaction, similar to those discussed in Kieber and Fine, and not a pure real estate transaction similar to the one discussed in Heritage Hills. The relationship between the submeter company and the tenant is reasonably classified as a service agreement for a utility service.

This service is separat e and distinct from the real estate transaction between the landlord and tenant, i.e., the transfer of the landlord’s interest in the real property to the tenant for the lease period, which includes the landlord’s obligation to maintain the property and the tenant’s obligation to pay rent. In Heritage Hills, the dispute arose solely from the terms of the lease that concern the transfer of the landlord’s interest in the real property to the tenant – the amount due to the landlord for repairs made to the real property.
