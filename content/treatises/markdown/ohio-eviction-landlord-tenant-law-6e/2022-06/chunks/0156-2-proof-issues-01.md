This burden of proof requires a causal link between the tenant’s complaint (or tenant organization activities) and the landlord’s decision to te rminate the tenancy (“required causal link”). It generally exists if, absent the tenant’s complaint or tenant organization activities, “the eviction action would probably not have been commenced.” Karas, 2 Ohio App.3d at 6. See generally Higbee v. Starr , 698 F.2d 945 (8th Cir. 1983).

An essential element of the required causal link is that, when the landlord decided to evict the tenant, the landlord knew of the tenant’s engagement in the protected
activity. Absent that knowledge, the required causal lin k cannot be shown. E.g., Felman v. Coleman , No. 15686, 1996 WL 325501 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., June 14,
1996) (failure to prove retaliatory motive because landlord served tenant with notice of termination before landlord knew of tenant’s complaint to the ci ty inspection department); Braff Mobile Home Co. v. McFarland, No. 8-265, 1982 WL 5815 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., July 23, 1982) (failure to prove retaliatory motive because no evidence that landlord knew of tenant’s complaint about the water supply or the tenant’s contacts with various governmental agencies regarding the water supply).See generally Barber v. Am. Sec. Bank, 655 F. Cir. 1988).

Other evidence that is needed to establish the required causal link varies from case to case, but often includes evidence that falls into one of two categories.

The first category is evidence that the landlord decided to evict the tenant soon after the tenant’s engagement in a protected activity. Such evidence may be a substantial factor in determining whether the required causal link exists. Cont’l Enters., Ltd. v. Franklin, No. 102899, 2016 WL 1941209 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., May 19, 2016); Reid v. Plainsboro Partners, III , Nos. 09AP-442,446, 2010 WL 3610931 163 (Ohio App., 10th Dist., Sept. 16, 2010); Karas v. Floyd, 2 Ohio App.3d 4, 440 N.E.2d 563 (2nd Dist. 1981).

However, it does not create a presumption of unlawful retaliation. Howard v.

The second category is evidence of the landlord treating the tenant in a different manner than the landlord has treated other tenants. For example, inBelvoir Cliffs Apts.
v. Bembry, 56 Ohio Misc. 37, 383 N.E.2d 1170 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, 1978), the tenant proved retaliation by, among other things, proving that the landlord refused to accept the tenant’s late rent payme nt even though the landlord customarily had accepted such payments from other tenants.

Other circumstantial evidence in support of the retaliation defense may include:
(1) that the tenant paid rent timely and otherwise acted in accordance with the requirements of the rental agreement, (2) that the landlord never complained to the tenant about the conduct alleged in the complaint as the reason for the eviction, and/or
(3) that the landlord is evasive in responding to inquiries about the reason for the eviction.

If a tenant proves that the landlord brought the eviction action in violation of R.C.

5321.02(A), the burden of proof shifts to the landlord. Karas v. Floyd, 2 Ohio App.3d 4, 6, 440 N.E.2d 563 (2nd Dist. 1981). See also Timbercreek Vill. Apts. v. Myles, No.

17422, 1999 WL 335307, at *7 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., May 28, 1999). See generally Meacham v. Knolls Atomic Power Lab., 554 U.S. 84, 91 (2008) (“‘[w]hen a proviso ...

carves an exception out of the body of a statute or contract, those who set up suc h exception must prove it’” (quoting Javierre v. Cent. Altagracia , 217 U.S. 502, 508 (1910))). At this point, the landlord may still defeat the retaliation defense by proving that one of the R.C. 5321.03 exceptions applies. Id.
