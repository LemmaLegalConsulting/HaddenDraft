## Source text

Court decisions provide some guidance on other types of rental agreement
provisions. They include the following court decisions.

A rental agreement may restrict occupancy of the premises to the persons specified in the rental agreement. E.g., Parker v. Fisher, 17 Ohio App.3d 103, 477 N.E.2d 654 (9th Dist. 1984).

A rental agreement may prohibit pets.Douglas v. Campost Props., LLC, No. 2016- CV-102094, slip op. at 3 (Ohio Mun., Elyria, July 21, 2020). But any such prohibition is subject to the reasonable accommodation requirements of the federal and state laws on discrimination that are listed in Section II.U.1, above.

One court found a lease provision that made the tenant liable for any damage to the premises “caused by the tenant” to be consistent with R.C. 5321.05(A)(6).Donegal
Cos. v. White, No. 98-CO-1, 1999 WL 159222, at *5 (Ohio App., 7th Dist., Mar. 18, 1999). However, it did so only upon finding that the provision did not impose strict liability because “[t]he words ‘caused by’ imply that proof of negligence is a threshold to her [tenant’s] liability.” Id.

343 With respect to guest conduct, a rental agreement provision may not ex pand the R.C. 5321.05(A)(6) requirements. Allstate Ins. Co. v. Dorsey, 46 Ohio App.3d 66, 68, 545 N.E.2d 920 (9th Dist. 1988); Aetna Cas. & Surety Co. v. Neff, 30 F. Supp.2d 990, 997 (S.D. Ohio 1998) (applying Ohio law).

Likewise, a rental agreement may not establish any right to terminate the tenancy that is inconsistent with R.C. 5321.11. See R.C. 5321.06. Therefore, a rental agreement may not authorize a termination of the tenancy for tenant conduct that violates an R.C. 5321.05(A) obligation (or a corresponding rental agreement provision) but does not materially affect health and safety.277 See Section I.D.2.a, above.
