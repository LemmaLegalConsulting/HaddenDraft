The equity defense is often asserted by residents of manufactured home parks.

Two strong equity considerations against a forfeiture of their tenancies exist in virtually every case. One is that, if a forfeiture is grante d, the resident loses the resident’s manufactured home (if it cannot reasonably be moved) or incurs significant hardship and cost in moving the manufactured home. The other is that, if a forfeiture is granted, the resident loses a tenancy that the residen t may renew continual ly and the park operator must renew absent good cause. See Section IV.C.1, below.

These explicit and implicit holdings that Civil Rule 17(A) does not apply to eviction actions seem to be erroneous for a two-fold reason.

First, the four court decisions that reference Civil Rule 17(A) ( Hardin, Adlaka, Woods, and Harris) use an erroneous standard for determining whether Civil Rule 17(A) applies to eviction actions. Their erro neous standard is, if any conflict exists between an R.C. Chapter 1923 provision and a Civil Rule provision, the Civil Rule provision per se does not apply. This standard is erroneous for the reasons discussed in Section I.B, above, including especially note 4, above. Under the correct standard, when such conflicts exist, the Civil Rule provision does not apply to eviction actions only if its application would frustrate the summary nature of eviction proceedings. See Section I.B, above.

Second, under the correct standard, Civil Rule 17(A) applies to eviction actions.

Civil Rule 17(A) simply defines the parties who may bring eviction actions. It does not affect in any way the time required to prosecute an eviction action and thus does not interfere with the summary nature of eviction proceedings. See generally Steiner
v. Nadal, No. 91 -CVG-8356 (Ohio Mun., Canton, Apr. 2, 1992) (applies Civil Rule 17(A) to an eviction action without any analysis as to why it applies).

If Civil Rule 17(A) applies to eviction actions, some non-owner property managers will meet its real party in interest test. A real party in interest under Civil Rule 17(A) includes “a party with whom or in whose name a contract has been made for the benefit of another.” Therefore, when a n on-owner property manager signs a lease, as managing agent for the owner, the property manager meets the Civil Rule 17(A) real party in interest test. Tishman Midwest Mgmt. Corp. of Ohio v. Hunt, No. CA-10519, 1987 WL 28181, at *2 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Dec. 10, 1987).
