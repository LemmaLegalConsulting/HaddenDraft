# 2. Other Prohibited Provisions, R.C. 5321.06,

## Source text

5321.13, and 5321.131 R.C. 5321.06 prohibits any rental agreem ent provision that is inconsistent with a right or duty that is established in either R.C. Chapter 5321 or any other rule of law.

For example, a provision that constitutes a penalty (see Section VII.F.9.b, above) is inconsistent with with an “other rule o f law” for R.C. 5321.06 purposes. Berkelely Park, LLC v. Pile, No. 2005-CVG-046214 (Ohio Mun., Franklin Cty., Apr. 11, 2007).

This statutory prohibition is consistent with Ohio common law that provides that a rental agreement may include any terms th at are not inconsistent with or prohibited by law or against public policy. Langfan v. Carlton Gardens Co. , 183 Ohio App.3d 260, 916 N.E.2d 1079, ¶ 30 (3rd Dist. 2009).

R.C. 5321.13 prohibits any lease provision that:
(1) modifies or waives any right or obl igation that is established in R.C.

Chapter 5321, except an agreement by the landlord to assume responsibility for any of the tenant’s R.C. 5321.05 obligations (R.C.

5321.13(A),(F));
(2) is a confession of judgment for the recovery of rent or damage to the premises (R.C. 5321.13(B));
(3) provides for the payment of the landlord’s or tenant’s attorney’s fees (R.C. 5321.13(C));
(4) limits or provides for indemnification for the landlord’s liability (R.C.

5321.13(D)); or 379
(5) permits the receipt of rent free of the obligation to comply with R.C.

5321.04 (R.C. 5321.13(E)).

The R.C. 5321.13(C) prohibition on agreements to pay attorney’s fees extends to “any other agreement between a landlord and tenant.” One issue relative to this clause is whether “any other agreement” includes a settlement agreement. If so, any provision for payment of attorney’s fees to the landlord or tenant, in a settlement agreement between a landlord and a tenant, would be unenforceable. However, similar to the ruling in LaCourse v. Fleitz, 28 Ohio St.3d 209, 503 N.E.2d 159 (1986) (discussed in Section V.C, above), courts may find that R.C. 5321.13(C) is not sufficiently explicit on this issue to abrogate the longstanding rule of common law that permits payments of attorney’s fees in settlement agreements.

R.C. 5321.131 prohibits any provision in a rental agreement that restricts a tenant’s right to display the flag of the United States or certain other specified flags, provided the flag is displayed in accordance with certain specified requirements.

Court decisions on these R.C. 5321.06 and 5321.13 prohibitions are summarized below.

Obligation to Maintain Premises in Habitable Condition:

Anderson v. Ballard, No. L-10-1007, 2010 WL 3292685 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Aug. 20, 2010) R.C. 5321.13(A) prohibits a landlord from modifying or waiving the landlord’s R.C. 5321.04 obligation to maintain the rental premises in a habitable condition. The “as is” provision in the rental agreement was an unlawful attempt to waive this obligation.

Liability for Damage to Premises or Property:

Kinn v. Showe Mgmt. Corp., No. 5-01-46, 2002 WL 462859 (Ohio App., 3rd Dist., Mar. 27, 2002) The lease provision made the tenant liable for any damage to the premises caused by the negligence of other parties ( e.g., guest) w ithout any determination of tenant negligence. The lease provision was inconsistent with R.C. 5321.05(A)(6) and, therefore, invalid under R.C. 5321.06.

Smith v. Ohio Edison, Inc. , No. 98 -CA-37, 1999 WL 6444 (Ohio App., 2nd Dist., Jan. 8, 1999) The lease provision provided that the landlord shall not be liable for any damage caused by a failure of the plumbing, heating, sewage, electrical, water, or gas system at the premises or caused by a roof leak at the
premises. The lease provision was inconsistent wi th the landlord’s obligations under R.C. Chapter 5321 and, therefore, was invalid under R.C. 5321.06 and 5321.13(A),(D).

Kearney v. Crown , No. 93 -T-4945, 1994 WL 587382 (Ohio App., 11th Dist., Aug. 12, 1994) The lease provision made the tenant liable for any damage to the premises that the tenant’s pet caused. The lease provision was consistent with R.C.

5321.05 and, therefore, was enforceable.

Wilmington Green III v. Rickman , No. CA90 -07-013, 1991 WL 81850 (Ohio App., 12th Dist., May 13, 1991) (2-1 decision) The tenant executed a payment agreement with the landlord to reimburse the landlord for damage to the premises that an uninvited visitor (former
boyfriend) had caused. The payment agreement did not violate R.C.

5321.05 and, therefore, did not violate R.C. 5321.13.

Allstate Ins. Co. v. Dorsey, 46 Ohio App.3d 66, 545 N.E.2d 920 (9th Dist.

1988) The lease provision provided that the tenant shall be liable for damage to property, appliances, or plumbing caused by carelessness. The lease provision, insofar as it made the tenant liable for all damages caused by anyone’s carelessness, was inconsistent with R.C. 5321.05(A)(6) and, therefore, was unenforceable under R.C. 5321.06.

Smith v. W. Terrace , No. 50938, 1986 WL 11033 (Ohio App., 8th Dist.,
Sept. 18, 1986) The lease provision provided that the landlord shall not be liable for any damage or injury caused by water, steam, or the breaking or leaking of pipes resulting from any cause whatsoever. The lease provision violated R.C. 5321.06 and 5321.13.

Towne Realty Co. v. Henson , No. 4696, 1977 Ohio App. LEXIS 10248 (Ohio App., 5th Dist., Dec. 14, 1977) The lease provision provided that the landlord shall not be liable for any loss or damage to the tenant’s property that is placed in the premises, regardless of the cause. The lease provision violated R.C. 5321.13(D) and, therefore, was unenforceable.

Vesta Corp. v. Harris , No. 2021-CVG-004702 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland,
Hous. Div., Jan. 20, 2022) 381 The lease provision made the tenant liable for any damage to thepremises caused by the negligence of other parties ( e.g., guests), without any determination of tenant negligence. The lease provision conflicts with R.C. 5321. 05(A)(6) and, therefore, was unenforceable under R.C.

5321.06.

Weingarden v. Eagle Ridge Condos., 71 Ohio Misc.2d 7, 653 N.E.2d 759 (Ohio Mun., Toledo, Hous. Div., 1995) The lease provision assessed the tenant a fee for carpet cleaning at the end of the tenancy. To the extent that the lease provision assessed this fee independent of any evidence of damage beyond normal wear and tear, the lease provision was inconsistent with R.C. 5321.16 and, therefore, was unenforceable.

Nonrenewal or Termination of Tenancy:

Oldendick v. Crocker, 70 N.E.3d 1033, 2016-Ohio-5621 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., 2016) In a one -year lease, a provision permitted the tenant to effect an early termination of the lease if: (1) the tenant gave 60 days advance notice; (2) the landlord approved; (3) the tenant paid an early termination fee equal to one month’s rent; and (4) the tenant p aid the monthly rent until the landlord re-rented the property. The early termination fee operated as a penalty (and not liquidated damages) and thus was not enforceable. The early termination fee was also unenforceable because it conflicted with R.C. 5321.16.

Detweiter v. Galt , No. 01 -CA-34, 2001 WL 1682822 (Ohio App., 4th Dist., Dec. 26, 2001) An oral agreement permitted the tenant (or landlord) to provide the other party with 30 days (or more) advance notice to terminate their month -to- month tenancy , regardless of the periodic rental date. The oral agreement’s disregard of the periodic rental date was inconsistent with R.C. 5321.17(B) and, therefore, was unenforceable under R.C. 5321.06.
