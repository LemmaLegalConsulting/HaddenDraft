# d. Additional Time – Landlord Delay

## Source text

Generally, R.C. 1923.04(A) does not impose any limit on the amount of time beyond three days that a landlord may delay between serving the notice to vacate and filing the eviction complaint. See, e.g. , Reese v. Webb , No. L -04-1257, 2005 WL 1009889 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Apr. 29, 2005) (76 days between service of the notice to vacate and filing of the complaint); Adm’r of Veterans Affairs v. Jackson, 41 Ohio App.3d 274, 535 N.E.2d 369 (9th Dist. 1987) (five months between service of the notice to vacate and filing of the complaint, although landlord delayed filing the complaint at tenant’s request to allow tenant to present a purchase proposal); Owner’s
Mgmt. Co. v. Johnson , No. 61691, 1992 WL 309362 (Ohio App., 8th Dist., Oct. 22,
1992) (45 days between service of the notice to vacate and filing of the complaint).

One exception to this general rule applies when the eviction action is fora tenant’s violation of R.C. 5321.05(A)(9). In those cases, the landlord is required to commence the eviction action “promptly” after the service of the notice to vacate. R.C.

5321.05(C)(2), 5321.17(C).

The courts are split on whether a second exception to this general rule exists. They are split on whether a landlord’s delay, between serving the notice to vacate and filing the FED action, may constitute a waiver of the notice to vacate.

One appellate court has held: “If the tenant does not vacate the premises by the end of the three-day period, the landlord may then file the FED action as long as the action is filed within two years of the date on which the tenant was in default of the terms of the rental agreement.” Reese v. Webb, No. L-04-1257, 2005 WL 1009889, ¶ 4 (Ohio App., 6th Dist., Apr. 29, 2005).

In contrast, one municipal court has held: “A landlord who, … after its service of a notice to vacate, can waive its right to pursue an eviction action if it acts inconsistently with its pursuit of the eviction….[An] action that can be inconsistent is a delay in taking action … between the service of a notice to vacate and the filing of an eviction.” CMHA v. Hawthorne, No. 2012-CVG-007158, slip op. at 1 (Ohio Mun., Cleveland, Hous. Div., July 30, 2012, objections overruled Nov. 30, 2012).

Irrespective of the general rule and the exceptions, in most cases, the landlord’s inability to accept future rent without waiving the notice to vacate (see Section II.C,
below) prompts the landlord to file theeviction action promptly upon the expiration of the three-day period.
