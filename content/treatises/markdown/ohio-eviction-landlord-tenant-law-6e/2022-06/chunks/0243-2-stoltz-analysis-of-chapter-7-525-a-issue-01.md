## Source text

11 U.S.C. § 525(a) prohibits governmental units from, among other things, refusing to renew a person’s grant because of the person’s debt, if the grant is protected under § 525(a) and the debt has been discharged in a bankruptcy proceeding.

Congress enacted § 525(a) to codify the decision in Perez v. Campbell, 402 U.S.

637 (1971). S. Rep. No. 95 -989, at 81, reprinted in 1978 U.S.C.C.A.N. 5787, 5867.

In Perez, the Supreme Court invalidated, under the Supremacy Clause, a state law that suspended the drivers’ licenses of debtors who failed to satisfy motor -vehicle-related tort judgments. The Supreme Court held that the state statute was in conflict with the Bankruptcy Act because it frustrated the “fresh start” policy of the Bankruptcy Code.

“Notably, the text of Section 525(a) does not limit its scope to the facts presented in Perez. Section 525(a) applies to any governmental unit, not just a State agency or department; it covers not only licenses, but also permits, charters, franchises, and other similar grants ....” Stoltz v. Brattleboro Hous. Auth., 315 F.3d 80, 87 (2d Cir. 2002).

As noted in Section III.J.1, above, when a Public Housing tenant files a chapter 7 bankruptcy, a primary issue, on which the case law is split, 238 is whether § 525(a) precludes a public housing authority (“PHA”) from evicting a tenant on the basis of nonpayment of discharged, prepetition rent.

The answer to this issue depends on four sub -issues: (1) whether a PHA is a governmental unit for § 525(a) purposes; (2) whether the Publ ic Housing lease – the tenant’s right to continue to participate in the Public Housing program – falls within the § 525(a) “other similar grants” provision; (3) whether the alleged unpaid rent that has been discharged is the proximate cause of the eviction – the “solely because” 238 The court decisions are listed in Section III.J.1, above.

requirement;239 and (4) whether the § 525(a) protection trumps the PHA’s general right under 11 U.S.C. § 362(b)(22)240 to pursue state law remedies, including eviction, for a prepetition breach of the lease, when the trustee rejects the lease.

The only federal appellate court to rule on this issue is the Second Circuit, inStoltz
v. Brattleboro Hous. Auth ., 315 F.3d 80 (2d Cir. 2002). It analyzed each of the four sub-issues and thereupon held that § 525(a) prohibits the PHA from evic ting a Public Housing tenant for nonpayment of discharged prepetition rent.

First, the Court found that a PHA is a governmental unit for § 525(a) purposes.

Stoltz, 315 F.3d at 88. The PHA did not dispute this finding. Id.

Second, the Court held that “other similar grants” include the Public Housing lease and the right to continue to participate in the Public Housing program.Stoltz, 315 F.3d at 89-91. The Court rejected the PHA’s argument “that a public housing tenant’s future right to participate in the public housing program is the only protected public housing grant under section 525(a).” Id. at 89.

Third, the Court held that, in these circumstances, the eviction claim is solely because of the unpaid rent that has been discharged in a chapter 7 bankruptcy. Stoltz, 315 F.3d at 91. The Court rejected the PHA’s argument “that it desires to evict Stoltz only because she breached her public housing lease, not because she failed to pay prepetition rent that was discharged in bankruptcy. Id. The Court n oted that “this unpersuasive argument [of the PHA] is premised on an ‘illusory distinction’ between the breach of the lease and the nonpayment of the discharged prepetition rent.” Id.

Fourth, the Court held that, in this context, § 525(a) and 11 U.S.C. § 365 (now 239 For § 525(a) purposes, a governmental unit’s action is “solely because” of the failure to pay a discharged debt if the nonpayment is “the proximate cause of the cancellation – the act or event that triggers the agen cy’s decision to cancel, whatever the agency’s ultimate motive in pulling the trigger may be.” FCC v. NextWave Personal Commc’ns Inc., 537 U.S. 293, 301-02 (2003). As the Supreme Court explained: “When the statute refers to failure to pay a debt as the sole cause of cancellation (‘solely because’), it cannot reasonably be understood to include, among the other causes whose presence can preclude application of the prohibition, the governmental unit’s motive in effecting the cancellation. Such a reading w ould deprive § 525 of all force. It is hard to imagine a situation in which a governmental unit would not have some further motive behind the cancellation ....” Id., at 301.
